SECTION 1: WATER QUALITY PARAMETERS

Physical, chemical, and biological parameters that define drinking water quality. Focus on IS 10500:2012 limits, measurement methods, and health significance.

1.1 PHYSICAL PARAMETERS

Turbidity: Caused by suspended clay, silt, algae, colloidal organics. Measured nephelometrically in NTU (Nephelometric Turbidity Units). High turbidity shields pathogens from disinfection (UV, Cl₂).
Colour:
  • True colour = measured after filtering through 0.45 micron membrane (removes suspended matter)
  • Apparent colour = includes colour from suspended particles
  • Measured in TCU (True Colour Units) by visual comparison with platinum-cobalt standard or spectrophotometry at 456 nm
Odour - Threshold Odour Number (TON):
  • TON = (Volume sample + Volume odour-free water) / Volume sample at minimum dilution where odour just detectable to 50% of trained panel
  • TON = 50 means 50× dilution needed before odour disappears; higher TON = more objectionable
  • IS 10500:2012 requires taste and odour to be 'agreeable' - no numerical limit
Temperature effects: Palatability (cold water preferred <15°C), decreases DO solubility, increases microbial growth rate, decreases CT values (faster kill), increases reaction rates, makes taste/odour more perceptible.
IS 10500:2012 Physical Limits:
Turbidity: Desirable = 1 NTU; Permissible (no alternative source) = 5 NTU
Colour: Desirable = 5 TCU; Permissible = 15 TCU
Taste & Odour: 'Agreeable' (no numerical limit)

1.2 CHEMICAL PARAMETERS

pH: IS 10500 range = 6.5 to 8.5 (no relaxation permitted).
  • pH < 6.5 → corrosion of pipes, leaching of heavy metals
  • pH > 8.5 → reduces chlorination efficiency (HOCl → OCl⁻, which is 80× less germicidal)
  • Optimal disinfection pH: 7.0-7.5 (HOCl dominates)
Hardness:
  • Temporary hardness: Ca(HCO₃)₂ and Mg(HCO₃)₂ - removed by boiling → CaCO₃ ↓ + H₂O + CO₂
  • Permanent hardness: CaSO₄, MgSO₄, CaCl₂, MgCl₂ - requires lime-soda or ion exchange softening
  • Hardness as CaCO₃ = (ion mg/L ÷ equivalent weight) × 50
Alkalinity interpretation (P = phenolphthalein, M = methyl orange):
  • P = 0, M > 0 → HCO₃⁻ only = M
  • P = M → OH⁻ only
  • P = M/2 → CO₃²⁻ only
  • P < M/2 → CO₃²⁻ + HCO₃⁻
  • P > M/2 → CO₃²⁻ + OH⁻
Dissolved Oxygen (DO): Measured by Winkler iodometric method (azide modification removes NO₂⁻ interference). Field: membrane electrode (Clark-type polarographic sensor).
IS 10500:2012 Chemical Limits:
pH = 6.5-8.5 | Total Hardness: Desirable 200, Permissible 600 mg/L as CaCO₃
TDS: Desirable 500, Permissible 2000 mg/L | Chloride: 250/1000 mg/L
Sulphate: 200/400 mg/L | Iron: 0.3/no relaxation | Fluoride: 1.0/1.5 mg/L
Nitrate: 45 mg/L (no relaxation) | Arsenic: 0.01/0.05 mg/L | Lead: 0.01/0.05 mg/L

1.3 BIOLOGICAL PARAMETERS

Coliform Bacteria: Used as indicator organisms. E. coli is chosen over pathogens because:
  • Abundant: 10⁸-10⁹ per gram of human feces
  • Simple, inexpensive detection (MPN, MF technique)
  • Survival similar to most enteric pathogens
  • Does not multiply in treated water
  • Thermotolerant coliforms at 44.5°C = fecal indicator
MPN Test (3-stage procedure):
  1. Presumptive: Lactose broth with inverted Durham tube. Gas production (CO₂ + H₂) within 24-48 h at 35°C = positive
  2. Confirmed: Brilliant Green Bile Broth (BGBB)
  3. Completed: Plated on EMB agar - metallic sheen on dark colonies = E. coli
BOD₅ Test:
  • 5 days at 20°C ± 1°C in DARK (darkness prevents algal photosynthesis producing O₂ which would give falsely low BOD)
  • Bottles must be completely full (no air space) and sealed
  • DO depletion should be 2-6 mg/L; residual DO > 1 mg/L after 5 days
COD Test (Standard APHA):
  • Oxidant: K₂Cr₂O₇ in concentrated H₂SO₄
  • Catalyst: Ag₂SO₄ (oxidises straight-chain hydrocarbons)
  • Conditions: 150°C for 2 hours (reflux)
  • Chloride interference removed by HgSO₄ (ratio 10:1 HgSO₄:Cl⁻)
  • Excess Cr₂O₇²⁻ back-titrated with FAS (ferrous ammonium sulphate)
BOD/COD Ratio Interpretation:
> 0.5 = Readily biodegradable (domestic sewage 0.5-0.6)
0.3-0.5 = Moderately biodegradable
< 0.3 = Poorly biodegradable (industrial with solvents, dyes - needs physico-chemical/AOP)
Coliform standard: ABSENT (0 MPN/100 mL) in drinking water

1.4 IS 10500:2012 vs WHO GUIDELINES

WHO GDWQ Framework: Health-based targets derived from risk assessment:
  • Carcinogens: 10⁻⁶ additional lifetime cancer risk
  • Non-carcinogens: 1 μDALY/person/year tolerable health burden using TDI
  • Non-mandatory international benchmarks; national standards adapt them
WHO Water Safety Plan (WSP): Multi-barrier preventive approach adapted from HACCP:
  1. Catchment characterisation
  2. Treatment barrier adequacy
  3. Distribution safeguards
  4. Verification monitoring
  5. Management procedures
Key WHO vs IS differences:
  • WHO free chlorine residual at tap: min 0.1 mg/L, max 5 mg/L (CPHEEO India: min 0.2 mg/L)
  • WHO PM₂.₅ annual (2021): 5 μg/m³ (India NAAQS: 40 μg/m³ - 8× higher)
  • WHO uranium: 0.03 mg/L (basis: kidney nephrotoxicity, not radiation)

SECTION 2: SOURCES, QUANTITY & POPULATION FORECASTING

Estimation of water demand based on population forecasts, design periods, and fire demand. Foundation for water supply scheme design.

2.1 SURFACE WATER vs GROUNDWATER SOURCES

Parameter Surface Water Groundwater
Turbidity High (seasonal variation) Low (natural filtration through soil)
Microbial load High Low
Hardness Moderate Often high (Ca²⁺, Mg²⁺ from rock)
Iron/Manganese Low Often high (Fe²⁺, Mn²⁺ from anoxic conditions)
Dissolved Oxygen Good (near saturation) Low/anaerobic
Special contaminants Agricultural runoff, pollution High F⁻, As, NO₃⁻ possible (geogenic)
Treatment required Full treatment train Mainly disinfection + Fe/Mn removal
Aquifer Types:
  • Unconfined (phreatic): Water table at atmospheric pressure; specific yield Sy = 0.05-0.30 (drains by gravity)
  • Confined (artesian): Sandwiched between aquitards; piezometric head > aquifer top; storativity 10⁻⁵ to 10⁻³ (elastic storage)
  • Artesian well: Piezometric surface above ground → water flows without pumping
Rippl's Mass Curve Method: Determines reservoir storage capacity. Cumulative inflow plotted vs time; demand line at constant slope. Maximum vertical gap between tangent demand lines and mass curve = required storage. Critical period = longest stretch where cumulative demand exceeds supply. Perennial rivers maintained by groundwater baseflow (gaining streams). Overextraction of groundwater reduces baseflow (seen in Rajasthan, Gujarat).

2.2 POPULATION FORECASTING METHODS

1. Arithmetic Method: Pn = P₀ + n × Ka
Ka = average decadal arithmetic increase | Suitable for large established cities | Gives LOWEST projection

2. Geometric Method: Pn = P₀ × (1 + r)n
r = average decadal growth rate (fraction) | Suitable for rapidly growing cities | Gives HIGHEST projection

3. Incremental Increase: Pn = P₀ + n·Ka + [n(n+1)/2]·Ks
Ks = average of second differences (change in increment) | Captures acceleration/deceleration

4. Logistic (S-curve) Method: P = Ps / (1 + m·e-rn)
Ps = saturation population (carrying capacity) | Most realistic long-term model | Requires 3 census data points
WORKED EXAMPLE - Geometric Method:
Given: 1991=1,00,000; 2001=1,30,000; 2011=1,69,000
r₁ = (130-100)/100 = 30%; r₂ = (169-130)/130 = 30% → Average r = 30%
P(2021) = 169,000 × 1.30 = 2,19,700

2.3 PER CAPITA DEMAND & VARIATIONS (CPHEEO)

Recommended Per Capita Supply:
  • Jal Jeevan Mission rural target (FHTC): 55 LPCD
  • CPHEEO domestic urban: 135 LPCD
  • Total (including commercial, fire, losses): 200-250 LPCD for large cities
  • Delhi actual supply: ~220 LPCD average (unequal - planned 300+, slums <50)
Demand Variation Factors:
  • Maximum daily demand = 1.8 × average daily
  • Maximum hourly demand = 1.5 × maximum daily = 2.7 × average daily
  • Small towns peak factor: up to 3.5×; large metros: ~2.5× (averaging effect)
  • Peak hour: typically 7-9 AM and 6-8 PM
Seasonal variation: Summer (April-June) is peak demand due to higher consumption, cooling, irrigation. Maximum daily factor (1.8×) applied to summer consumption. Coincident Draft (design peak): = Maximum daily demand + Fire demand (simultaneous). Governs service reservoir capacity and distribution main sizing. Maximum hourly demand (without fire) governs pumping station capacity.

2.4 DESIGN PERIOD & FIRE DEMAND

CPHEEO Design Periods:
Civil infrastructure (dams, WTPs, mains, reservoirs): 30 years
Mechanical/electrical equipment (pumps, motors): 15 years (replaced mid-project)
Rationale: Civil has 50-100 yr physical life; buried pipe replacement is disruptive; matches 25-30 yr loan financing
Fire Demand Formulae (Q in L/min, P in thousands):
Kuichling: Q = 3182 × √P (simpler, predominantly used in India per CPHEEO)
Freeman: Q = 4637 × √P × (1 - 0.01√P) (includes correction factor)
NBFU: Q = 4637 × √P (simplified Freeman)

Example: P = 250,000 (250 thousand):
Kuichling: Q = 3182 × √250 = 3182 × 15.81 = 50,325 L/min

Fire Reserve Volume = Q × Duration (typically 4-8 hours)
Example: Q = 20,000 L/min × 4 hr × 60 min = 4,800,000 L = 4,800 kL
Fire Hydrant Design (CPHEEO):
  • Residential/commercial: max 150 m spacing (hose length limitation)
  • Congested areas: 120 m | Industrial: 250 m
  • Connected to main of minimum 100 mm diameter
  • Minimum flow: 1000-1500 L/min at 10.5 m residual pressure (1.5 kg/cm²)

SECTION 3: WATER TREATMENT PROCESSES

Sequential treatment processes converting raw water to potable quality. Covers screening, sedimentation, coagulation-flocculation, filtration, and disinfection.

3.1 SCREENING & PLAIN SEDIMENTATION

Bar Screens (intake):
  • Coarse: 25-75 mm spacing (logs, fish, large solids)
  • Fine: 1-10 mm (drum/band type) - algae mats, leaves
  • Purpose: Protect pumps, valves, pipelines from damage
Plain Sedimentation: Gravity settling of coarse SS without coagulants. Removes 30-60% SS, 10-25% BOD. Pre-treatment for high-turbidity sources.
Hazen's Ideal Sedimentation Theory:
Surface Overflow Rate (SOR) = Q/A (m³/m²/day = m/day)
Physically equals upward water velocity in tank

Particle Removal:
Particles with Vs ≥ SOR: 100% removed
Particles with Vs < SOR: Fraction removed = Vs/SOR

Detention Time: t = H/SOR = V/Q
Example: SOR = 20 m/day, H = 3 m → t = 3/(20/24) = 3.6 hr
Typical SOR Values:
Plain sedimentation: 15-30 m/day | Primary clarifier: 30-50 m/day
Secondary clarifier: 15-25 m/day | Tube settler: 50-200 m/day
Detention Times: Plain sedim. 2-8 hr | Primary/Secondary clarifier 1.5-2.5 hr
Weir Loading Rate (WLR): = Q/Lweir (m³/m/day). Prevents excessive upward velocity near weir (short-circuiting, floc carry-over). Primary clarifier max: 125-250 m³/m/day. Tube/Plate Settlers (Lamella):
  • Inclined plates at 55-60° to horizontal with 50-100 mm spacing
  • Particle settles only across plate spacing (not full depth)
  • Effective SOR: 3-10× higher for same footprint
  • Cut-size reduction: d50 ∝ √(H/L); H from 3 m to 0.05 m = 7.7× better
  • Flow regime: Laminar (Re < 100)

3.2 COAGULATION & FLOCCULATION

Colloidal Stability: Natural colloids have negative zeta potential (-15 to -30 mV). Electrostatic repulsion prevents aggregation. Coagulation goal: reduce |ZP| to ~0 (isoelectric point). Alum Coagulation Mechanisms:
  1. Charge neutralisation: Al³⁺ and hydrolysis products (Al(OH)²⁺, Al₈(OH)₂₀⁴⁺) compress electrical double layer
  2. Sweep floc: Al(OH)₃ gelatinous precipitate enmeshes colloids
  3. Adsorption-bridging: Polymer-like Al species bridge particles
Jar Test Procedure (Standard):
  1. Rapid mix: 60-100 rpm for 1-2 min (coagulation phase - disperses coagulant)
  2. Slow mix: 20-40 rpm for 15-20 min (flocculation phase - forms settleable floc)
  3. Settle: 30 min
  4. Measure supernatant turbidity; plot vs dose → minimum = optimum dose
Typical Alum Doses:
  • Low turbidity (<10 NTU): 5-10 mg/L
  • Moderate (50-200 NTU): 10-50 mg/L
  • High (>500 NTU): 20-80 mg/L
  • High colour: up to 100 mg/L
Coagulant Aids:
  • Synthetic polymers (polyacrylamide - anionic/cationic/non-ionic): 0.1-2 mg/L; bridging
  • Activated silica: anionic, strong bonds in floc
  • Bentonite clay: added to very low-turbidity water to provide nuclei for floc
Optimal pH for alum: 6.5-7.5 | Ferric: 4-7 | PAC: wider range

3.3 FILTRATION (SSF, RSF, Pressure Filter)

Parameter Slow Sand Filter (SSF) Rapid Sand Filter (RSF)
Filtration rate 0.1-0.4 m/hr 4-8 m/hr (high-rate: 12-15)
Mechanism Biological (schmutzdecke) Physical (straining, adsorption)
Pre-treatment None needed (turbidity <5 NTU) Coagulation-flocculation MANDATORY
Media effective size 0.15-0.35 mm 0.45-0.55 mm
Media depth 0.6-1.2 m 0.6-0.75 m
Cleaning Scrape top 1-2 cm every 1-3 months Backwash every 24-72 hours
Pathogen removal >3-log Giardia/Crypto; 99% bacteria Moderate (depends on coagulation)
Schmutzdecke ("dirt layer"): Biological film on top 1-2 cm of SSF sand - algae, protozoa, bacteria, rotifers. Responsible for predation and biological degradation. Filter Media Specifications:
  • Effective Size (ES) = d₁₀ (sieve size passing 10% by weight)
  • Uniformity Coefficient (UC) = d₆₀/d₁₀; RSF needs UC < 1.7
  • UC close to 1.0 = uniform; UC > 2.0 = stratification problem
Dual-media Filter: Anthracite (SG ~1.5, ES 1.0-1.5 mm) on top + sand (SG ~2.65, ES 0.45-0.55 mm) below. After backwash, natural stratification (lighter stays on top). Benefits: coarser anthracite penetrates floc deeper, longer runs (2-3×), higher throughput. Triple-media adds garnet (SG ~4.0) at bottom. Pressure Filter: Enclosed vessel (3-5 bar), pump-pressurised flow, rates 10-20 m/hr. Advantages: compact, installable at any elevation, no gravity head needed. Common in industrial water supply.

3.4 BACKWASHING & FILTER RUNS

Backwash Parameters:
  • Target: 20-50% bed expansion
  • Water rate: 25-50 L/m²/min (0.025-0.050 m/s upward)
  • Air scour: 15-20 L/m²/min (vigorous scrubbing)
  • Duration: 5-10 minutes
  • Sequence: Air first, then water wash
Terminal Head Loss (THL) - Backwash Trigger:
  • Clean filter head loss: 0.3-0.6 m
  • THL trigger: 1.5-3 m
  • Turbidity breakthrough: 0.5-1 NTU in filtered water
  • Filter run: typically 24-48 hours
Filter Ripening Period: 15-60 min post-backwash with elevated turbidity. Initial filtrate has: (1) Fines from backwash water, (2) Unstable forming floc. Filter-to-Waste (FTW): divert initial filtrate to waste until turbidity < 0.3 NTU. Critical for Cryptosporidium oocyst removal (USEPA LT2ESWTR). Mudballs: Coagulated floc + media aggregates (1-5 cm) from inadequate backwash. Remediation: increase backwash rate, add air scour, surface wash (rotary nozzles 0.5-1.5 m/s), manual raking, media replacement.

3.5 DISINFECTION (Chlorination, Ozone, UV)

Chlorine Chemistry:
Cl₂ + H₂O → HOCl + HCl | HOCl ⇌ H⁺ + OCl⁻ (pKa ~7.5)
  • At pH 6.5: ~90% HOCl (germicidal)
  • At pH 7.5: 50% HOCl, 50% OCl⁻
  • At pH 8.5: ~10% HOCl, 90% OCl⁻
  • HOCl is 80× more germicidal than OCl⁻ (neutral vs charged)
Breakpoint Chlorination Curve:
  1. Phase 1: Chloramines form with NH₃ (combined residual)
  2. Phase 2: Chloramines oxidised by excess Cl₂ → residual drops to MINIMUM (breakpoint)
  3. Phase 3: Beyond breakpoint, free residual (HOCl + OCl⁻) increases linearly
Breakpoint dose: ~7.6 mg Cl₂ per mg NH₃-N
CT Concept (Chick-Watson model): Inactivation = f(C × T)
C = residual disinfectant (mg/L); T = contact time (min)
CT = 15 mg·min/L can be achieved by: 1.5 mg/L × 10 min OR 3 mg/L × 5 min OR 0.5 mg/L × 30 min

CT Values for 3-log Inactivation (at 5°C, pH 7):
Giardia (free Cl₂): ~165 mg·min/L
Cryptosporidium (free Cl₂): >7200 mg·min/L (impractical!)
Cryptosporidium (Ozone): 5-10 mg·min/L
Cryptosporidium (UV): 10-12 mJ/cm² for 3-log
Pre- vs Post-Chlorination:
  • Pre-chlorination: Kills algae, controls taste/odour; BUT forms THMs/HAAs with NOM (carcinogenic DBPs)
  • Post-chlorination: Minimal THM formation (NOM removed); provides distribution residual
  • Best practice: O₃/UV as primary disinfectant + Cl₂ only for residual
UV Disinfection (254 nm):
  • Mechanism: Pyrimidine dimer formation in DNA/RNA → replication blocked
  • Giardia/Crypto: very sensitive (3-log at 10-12 mJ/cm²)
  • Adenovirus: resistant (>186 mJ/cm² for 4-log)
  • Limitations: No residual (Cl₂ still needed); turbidity interference (<0.5-1 NTU); photoreactivation
Ozonation:
  • Half-life: 10-30 min at 20°C, pH 7
  • Must be generated on-site
  • No distribution residual
  • Bromate (BrO₃⁻) issue: O₃ + Br⁻ → HOBr → BrO₃⁻ (suspected carcinogen, IS limit 0.01 mg/L)
  • Control: ammonia addition, pH reduction, H₂O₂ quenching
Advanced Oxidation Processes (AOP): O₃+H₂O₂, UV+H₂O₂, O₃/UV → generates hydroxyl radicals (OH•, E⁰ = 2.8 V) - non-selective destruction of micropollutants (pharmaceuticals, pesticides, endocrine disruptors). Essential for potable reuse.

3.6 SOFTENING (Lime-Soda, Zeolite, Ion Exchange)

Lime-Soda Softening Reactions:
Temp. Ca: Ca(HCO₃)₂ + Ca(OH)₂ → 2CaCO₃↓ + 2H₂O (lime only)
Temp. Mg: Mg(HCO₃)₂ + 2Ca(OH)₂ → 2CaCO₃↓ + Mg(OH)₂↓ + 2H₂O
Perm. Ca: CaSO₄ + Na₂CO₃ → CaCO₃↓ + Na₂SO₄ (SODA ASH needed)
Perm. Mg: MgSO₄ + Ca(OH)₂ → Mg(OH)₂↓ + CaSO₄ (then treat as above)

Residual hardness limit: 50-85 mg/L as CaCO₃
(Limited by CaCO₃ Ksp = 3.3×10⁻⁹ and Mg(OH)₂ Ksp = 5.6×10⁻¹²)
Recarbonation: Excess lime removal by CO₂:
  • Stage 1: Ca(OH)₂ + CO₂ → CaCO₃↓ + H₂O (removes excess lime, pH 11→10)
  • Stage 2: CaCO₃ + CO₂ + H₂O → Ca(HCO₃)₂ (stabilisation, pH 8.3-8.5)
Split Treatment: Treat portion (60%) with full excess lime; bypass remainder (40%) untreated. Blend to achieve target hardness. Saves lime, sludge, cost. Zeolite/Ion Exchange Softening:
Softening: Ca²⁺ + Na₂Ze → CaZe + 2Na⁺
Regeneration: CaZe + 2NaCl (10% brine) → Na₂Ze + CaCl₂
  • Advantages: removes both temporary AND permanent hardness completely; no sludge
  • Disadvantages: adds Na⁺ (hypertension concern); turbidity/iron fouls resin; brine disposal

3.7 AERATION & IRON/MANGANESE REMOVAL

Purpose of Aeration:
  1. Oxidise Fe²⁺ (soluble) to Fe³⁺ → Fe(OH)₃ ↓ (brown precipitate, filterable)
  2. Strip CO₂ (raises pH, reduces corrosivity)
  3. Remove H₂S, CH₄, VOCs (taste/odour)
  4. Raise DO from near-zero (anaerobic groundwater) to saturation
Aerator Types:
  • Cascade: Best for CO₂, H₂S removal (gravity, no moving parts); less effective for DO
  • Spray: Fine droplets (1-3 mm), large SA; effective for DO addition; issues: wind drift, nozzle clogging
  • Diffused air: Compressed air through porous diffusers; controlled DO addition
Iron Removal (Fe²⁺ oxidation): Rate ∝ [Fe²⁺][O₂][OH⁻]². EXTREMELY pH-dependent - doubling pH = 100× faster.
  • pH 6: slow (hours) | pH 7: minutes | pH 8: seconds
  • Minimum pH 6.5 required; desirable pH > 7.0
Manganese Removal: Much harder than Fe. O₂ oxidation of Mn²⁺ is negligible below pH 9.5.
  • KMnO₄ oxidation: 1.92 mg KMnO₄ per mg Mn → MnO₂ ppt
  • Cl₂ oxidation: 1.3 mg Cl₂/mg Mn at pH > 7.5
  • Greensand filter: MnO₂-coated media (autocatalytic)
  • Biological oxidation: Mn-oxidising bacteria (most cost-effective)

3.8 FLUORIDATION & DEFLUORIDATION

Fluoride Health Thresholds:
  • < 0.5 mg/L: Dental caries risk
  • 0.5-1.5 mg/L: Dental protection (optimal 0.6-1.0 mg/L, climate-adjusted)
  • > 1.5 mg/L: Dental fluorosis (IS 10500 permissible limit)
  • > 3.0 mg/L: Severe dental fluorosis (pitting, brown staining)
  • > 3-6 mg/L: Skeletal fluorosis (joint pain, spine deformity)
  • > 10 mg/L: Crippling skeletal fluorosis
India: 17 states have endemic fluorosis from naturally high-F groundwater. Nalgonda Technique (NEERI Nagpur):
  1. Add lime (raises pH, adds alkalinity)
  2. Add alum (200-800 mg/L large dose) + bleaching powder
  3. Al(OH)₃ floc forms → F⁻ adsorbs on floc
  4. Sedimentation → Filtration
Efficiency: 70-90% removal. Limitations: large alum dose (residual Al³⁺), high sulphate, sludge disposal, ineffective for F > 10 mg/L. Alternative Methods:
  • Activated alumina adsorption: 92-97% efficient
  • Reverse osmosis: 90-98% rejection
  • Fluoride measurement: SPADNS colorimetric (Zr-dye bleaching) or Ion-Selective Electrode

3.9 DESALINATION (RO, Electrodialysis)

Osmotic Pressure: π = MRT (Van't Hoff)
Seawater (35,000 mg/L TDS): π ≈ 25-27 bar
Brackish water (3,000 mg/L): π ≈ 2.5 bar
RO Operating Pressure: SWRO 55-80 bar | BWRO 10-20 bar
Parameter SWRO (Seawater) BWRO (Brackish)
TDS feed 35,000-45,000 mg/L 1,000-8,000 mg/L
Operating pressure 55-80 bar 10-20 bar
Recovery rate 35-50% 75-85%
Energy 3-5 kWh/m³ (with ERD) 0.5-1.5 kWh/m³
Salt rejection >99.5% >99%
Electrodialysis (ED): DC electric field drives ions through alternating cation/anion-exchange membranes. Economical for brackish water (1000-5000 mg/L TDS). Advantages: less pre-treatment, higher recovery (85-95%), handles variable TDS. Disadvantages: inefficient for high TDS; doesn't remove uncharged species (organics, silica, bacteria). Multi-Stage Flash (MSF): Seawater heated to 90-120°C; flashed through stages at progressively lower pressure. Energy: 10-20 kWh/m³ (vs RO 3-5). Used in Middle East; 100,000-500,000 m³/day plants.

SECTION 4: DISTRIBUTION SYSTEM

Pipe networks, reservoirs, valves, hydrants, and leakage management. Covers Hardy Cross analysis and EPANET modelling.

4.1 PIPE NETWORKS & HARDY CROSS METHOD

Hardy Cross Iterative Correction:
ΔQ = -ΣhL / (n × Σ|hL/Q|)
n = 1.852 (Hazen-Williams) or n = 2 (Darcy-Weisbach)
Sign convention: Clockwise (+), Counter-clockwise (-)
Iterate until |ΔQ| < 0.01 L/s (3-5 iterations typically)

Hazen-Williams Equation:
hL = 10.67 × L × Q1.852 / (C1.852 × D4.87)
C values: New CI/steel 130-140 | Old corroded 80-100 | PVC/HDPE 140-150 | AC 120-140

Darcy-Weisbach: hL = fLV²/(2gD)
Dead-end vs Grid Networks:
  • Dead-end (tree): Single path; pipe failure cuts all downstream supply; stagnation causes chlorine depletion & bacterial regrowth
  • Grid/Loop: Multiple paths; redundancy; more uniform pressure; CPHEEO recommended for towns/cities
EPANET (US EPA): Solves simultaneously:
  1. Nodal continuity: Σ inflows = Σ outflows + demand at every node
  2. Energy equation: ΣhL around every loop = 0
Uses Global Gradient Algorithm (sparse matrix). Also simulates chlorine decay, water age, pump curves, PRVs, storage tanks, pressure-dependent demand.
CPHEEO/IS 1172 Minimum Residual Pressure:
Single-storey: 7 m (0.7 bar) | Two-storey: 12 m | Three-storey: 17 m
Maximum in mains: 70 m (to limit leakage/stress)
Fire hydrant: minimum 10.5 m (1.5 kg/cm²) during firefighting

4.2 SERVICE RESERVOIRS (ESR & GLSR)

Elevated Storage Reservoir (ESR / Overhead Tank):
  • Equalisation: pump operates at constant rate, ESR supplements peak
  • Gravity supply: constant pressure independent of pump operation
  • Emergency storage during pump failure
  • Capacity = Equalisation (⅓ max daily) + Fire + Emergency + Operational
  • Height: designed for min 7 m residual pressure at farthest/lowest point
Ground Level Service Reservoir (GLSR/Clear Water Reservoir):
  • Chlorine contact chamber (30+ min CT requirement, serpentine baffling)
  • Balancing storage between WTP output and variable distribution pumping
  • Emergency reserve + suction source for distribution pumps
  • Capacity: 1-2 days average daily demand
  • Dual compartments for maintenance without supply interruption
Economic Velocity in Water Mains: 0.6-1.5 m/s (optimal 0.9-1.2 m/s)
  • Below 0.6 m/s: sedimentation, biological growth, staleness
  • Above 1.5-3 m/s: high head losses, pipe erosion, water hammer
  • Large transmission mains: up to 3 m/s acceptable
Minimum Distribution Main Diameter (CPHEEO/IS 1742): 100 mm (4 inches)
  • Sub-mains: 150-200 mm | Trunk mains: 300-600+ mm
  • Service connections (laterals): 12.5-25 mm

4.3 VALVES, METERS & HYDRANTS

Valve Types & Applications:
  • Gate (sluice): Full bore open, full obstruction closed. NOT for throttling. Isolation use.
  • Butterfly: Quarter-turn disc. Suitable for throttling. Flow control in large mains.
  • Globe: Excellent throttling, higher head loss
  • Check (non-return): Prevents reverse flow
  • Pressure Reducing Valve (PRV): Pressure management in DMAs
Scour/Blow-off Valve: Installed at LOW points (valleys). Drains main for maintenance, flushes sediment, emergency dewatering. Size: ¼ to ⅓ of main diameter. Discharges to storm drain (NOT sewer). Air Valve: Installed at HIGH points. Releases trapped air during filling (prevents air lock, water hammer). Water Meters:
  • Positive Displacement (PD): Nutating disc/oscillating piston; accurate at low flows; domestic 15-25 mm (most common)
  • Turbine/Velocity: Propeller speed ∝ velocity; for 50-100 mm connections
  • Electromagnetic: No moving parts; industrial/bulk metering (DN > 50 mm)
  • Smart meters: AMR with pulse transmitters + telemetry
  • Standard: IS 779
Venturi Meter Discharge: Q = Cd × A₂/√(1-(A₂/A₁)²) × √(2gh)

4.4 LEAKAGE DETECTION & NRW

Non-Revenue Water (NRW) - IWA Water Balance:
  • Apparent losses: meter under-registration, billing errors, illegal connections
  • Real losses: pipe leakage (main breaks + background seepage + reservoir overflow)
  • Unbilled authorised: firefighting, flushing, parks
  • India NRW: typically 30-55% (target <15-20%); Delhi ~35-40%
Minimum Night Flow (MNF) Analysis: Measure DMA inlet flow at 2-4 AM (minimum demand). MNF minus night use = background leakage. DMA = District Metered Area (5000-7000 connections, single metered inlet). Acoustic Leak Detection: Two sensors on valves/hydrants. Cross-correlation of signals → time delay Δt → leak distance: x = (L - c·Δt)/2. Sound speed c ~1000-1500 m/s in pipes. Effective for metal pipes; harder for plastic. Hydrostatic Pressure Testing (IS 2065/CPHEEO):
  • Test pressure: 1.5 × working pressure (min 6 kg/cm² for class B-C)
  • Duration: 30-60 minutes
  • Essentially zero visible leakage allowed
  • Escaping water test: L = ND√P / 125,000 (L = L/hr, N = joints, D = mm, P = m)
Pressure-Leakage Relationship: L = L₀ × (P/P₀)N1
  • N1 = 0.5 for orifice leaks; 1.0-1.5 for elastic pipe cracks
  • Reducing pressure 20% → leakage reduction 20-30%
  • 30-40% NRW reduction possible with pressure management
Cathodic Protection: Prevents galvanic corrosion of buried CI/DI/steel pipes.
  • Sacrificial anode: Mg, Zn, Al connected to pipe (pipe becomes cathode)
  • Impressed current: DC rectifier (10-30 mA/m²)
  • PVC/PE: no CP needed (non-metallic)

GATE-specific topics not in UPPCB syllabus but valuable foundation. Covers hydrology, groundwater, oxygen sag, lake dynamics, and reactor design.

5.1 HYDROLOGICAL CYCLE & WATER BALANCE

Water Balance: P = R + ET + ΔS + GW recharge
Example: P=800, R=300, ET=420 → ΔS = +80 mm (storage increase)

Rational Method (Peak Runoff): Q = C·i·A/360 (A in hectares, i in mm/hr)
C = runoff coefficient: impervious 0.85-0.95 | forest 0.1-0.3 | agricultural 0.2-0.5

Rating Curve: Q = a(h - h₀)b (power function; loop curves show flood hysteresis)
Hydrological Processes:
  • Interception loss: Rainfall caught on vegetation that evaporates directly
  • Transpiration: Water via plant roots → stomata
  • Evapotranspiration (ET) = transpiration + soil evaporation + interception
  • PET estimated by Penman-Monteith or Thornthwaite
Unit Hydrograph (Sherman 1932) - 3 principles:
  1. Linearity: DRH proportional to effective rainfall depth
  2. Superposition: Multiple-period DRH can be summed
  3. Time invariance: Same UH for same-duration storms
Base Flow Separation Methods:
  • Constant slope method
  • N-day method: N = A^0.2 (A in km²)
  • Straight-line method
  • Base flow recession: Q(t) = Q₀·e^(-kt)

5.2 GROUNDWATER HYDRAULICS

Darcy's Law: Q = K·i·A (K = hydraulic conductivity)
Darcy velocity (flux): v = K·i | Seepage velocity = v/n (n = porosity)
Valid for laminar flow (Re < 1-10 in porous media)

K typical values: Gravel 10⁻² to 10² m/day | Sand 10⁻³ to 10⁰ | Clay <10⁻⁴

Transmissivity: T = K × b (b = saturated thickness; units m²/day)
Storage Coefficient: S = Ss × b (10⁻⁵ to 10⁻³ for confined)
Specific Yield: Sy = 0.1-0.3 for unconfined

Thiem Equation (steady-state, confined):
Q = 2π·T·(h₂-h₁) / ln(r₂/r₁)

Unconfined (steady-state): Q = π·K·(h₂² - h₁²) / ln(r₂/r₁)
Transient (Theis): Q = (4πT·s) / W(u)
Specific Capacity (Well): Sc = Q/s (m³/hr/m or L/s/m)
  • Higher Sc = better-performing well
  • Rough field approximation: T ≈ Sc × 2000
  • Declining Sc over time → biofouling, incrustation, screen clogging
  • Well efficiency = theoretical drawdown / actual drawdown × 100%

5.3 OXYGEN SAG CURVE (Streeter-Phelps)

Streeter-Phelps Equation:
D(t) = [kd·L₀/(kr-kd)]·(e^(-kdt) - e^(-krt)) + D₀·e^(-krt)

At Critical Point (Sag): dD/dt = 0 → kd·Lc = kr·Dc
Dc = kd·Lc/kr
tc = [1/(kr-kd)] · ln[(kr/kd)·(1 - D₀·(kr-kd)/(kd·L₀))]

O'Connor-Dobbins Reaeration: kr = (DL·u)^0.5 / H^1.5
Temperature correction: kr(T) = kr(20) × 1.024^(T-20)
Self-Purification Zones (downstream order):
  1. Zone of Degradation: Turbid, DO drops, BOD high, H₂S, fish absent
  2. Zone of Active Decomposition: Lowest DO (critical point), fungi, sludgeworms, no fish
  3. Zone of Recovery: DO rising, algae appear, rough fish return
  4. Clean Zone: DO restored to saturation, diverse biota
DO Saturation vs Temperature:
  • 15°C: 10.1 mg/L | 20°C: 9.1 mg/L | 25°C: 8.3 mg/L | 30°C: 7.5 mg/L
  • Decreases with salinity and altitude
Initial Deficit: D₀ = DOsat - DOactual

5.4 EUTROPHICATION & LAKE STRATIFICATION

Limiting Nutrient (Liebig's Law): Phosphorus in freshwater; Nitrogen in marine
  • Redfield ratio C:N:P = 106:16:1 by atoms
  • N:P > 16 → P limiting | N:P < 16 → N limiting
  • Carlson TSI uses Secchi depth, Chl-a, Total P
Thermal Stratification (3 layers):
  1. Epilimnion: Warm, wind-mixed, high DO (surface)
  2. Thermocline (metalimnion): Steepest T gradient (>1°C/m) - density barrier
  3. Hypolimnion: Cold, stagnant, DO depletion in productive lakes
Seasonal turnover (spring/autumn) mixes layers. Secchi Disk (20 cm white): Depth at which disk just disappears. TSI(SD) = 60 - 14.41·ln(SD in m)
  • Oligotrophic: SD > 4 m (clear, nutrient-poor)
  • Mesotrophic: 2-4 m
  • Eutrophic: < 2 m (algal blooms, low DO, fish kills)
Internal Loading (Legacy P): Under anoxic hypolimnion, Fe(III)-P reduces to Fe(II), releasing dissolved P. Perpetuates eutrophication even after external loading control.
  • Control: Alum treatment, hypolimnetic aeration, dredging, Phoslock (lanthanum-bentonite)

5.5 REACTOR DESIGN & KINETICS

First-Order Reaction (-r = k·C):

Batch Reactor: -ln(C/C₀) = k·t
Plug Flow Reactor (PFR): τ = -ln(C/C₀)/k (same as batch)
Continuous-Mixed Flow Reactor (CMFR/CSTR): τ = (C₀-C)/(k·C)
Rearranged: C/C₀ = 1/(1 + k·τ)

For 90% conversion: CMFR needs τ = 9/k | PFR needs τ = 2.3/k (4× smaller!)

Damköhler Number: Da = k·τ
Da >> 1 → high conversion (reaction dominates)
CMFR: C/C₀ = 1/(1+Da) | PFR: C/C₀ = e^(-Da)
WORKED EXAMPLE - CMFR vs PFR:
C₀ = 200 mg/L BOD, effluent = 20 mg/L, k = 0.25/hr
CMFR: τ = (200-20)/(0.25 × 20) = 180/5 = 36 hr
PFR: τ = -ln(20/200)/0.25 = ln(10)/0.25 = 9.2 hr
CMFR needs 4× larger volume! PFR preferred for high removal.
Mass Balance (CMFR Steady State):
Rate in - Rate out - Rate consumed = 0
Q·C₀ - Q·C - k·C·V = 0 → C = C₀/(1 + k·τ) Second-Order Reaction (-r = k·C²):
  • Batch/PFR: τ = (1/k)·(1/C - 1/C₀)
  • CMFR: τ = (C₀ - C)/(k·C²)
  • Performance gap widens for higher-order reactions
RTD Tracer Studies: Inject conservative tracer (NaCl, rhodamine B, fluoride).
  • Ideal CMFR: E(t) = (1/τ)·e^(-t/τ) (exponential decay)
  • Ideal PFR: Dirac delta at t = τ
  • Early peak = short-circuiting | Long tail = dead zones

5.6 ENVIRONMENTAL HYDRAULICS

Manning's Equation: V = (1/n)·R^(2/3)·S^(1/2)
R = A/P (hydraulic radius)
Half-full circular pipe: R = D/4 (same as full pipe!)
Max velocity at 93% full; max discharge at 94% full

Froude Number: Fr = V/√(g·y)
Fr < 1 subcritical | Fr = 1 critical (Emin) | Fr > 1 supercritical
Critical depth: yc = (q²/g)^(1/3) for rectangular
Hydraulic jump: y₂/y₁ = (√(1+8Fr₁²) - 1)/2

Weir Formulae:
Broad-crested: Q = 1.71·L·H^1.5 (SI)
Sharp-crested (Francis): Q = 1.84·L·H^1.5
V-notch: Q = (8/15)·Cd·tan(θ/2)·√(2g)·H^2.5
Channel Design Velocities:
  • Non-silting (minimum): > 0.6 m/s
  • Non-scouring (maximum): earthen 0.6-0.9 | grassed 1.2-1.5 | stone/brick 1.5-2.0 | concrete 2.5-6 m/s
Regime Theory (Lacey):
  • P = 4.75·√Q | R = 0.47·(Q/f)^(1/3) | S = f^(5/3) / (3340·Q^(1/6))
  • Used for unlined alluvial channels
Muskingum Channel Routing: S = K[x·I + (1-x)·O]
  • K = travel time of flood wave (hr)
  • x = weighting factor (0 = pure attenuation; 0.5 = pure translation)
  • Natural streams: x = 0.2-0.3
SCS Curve Number Method: Q = (P - Ia)² / (P - Ia + S)
  • S = 25400/CN - 254 (mm) | Ia = 0.2S
  • Depends on land use, Hydrological Soil Group (A to D), AMC
  • CWC India uses for design floods

CRITICAL EXAM-DAY TIPS:

  • ✓ Turbidity Units: Always NTU (Nephelometric Turbidity Units), NOT FTU or JTU. Desirable = 1, Permissible = 5.
  • ✓ Hardness Conversion: Always convert to CaCO₃ equivalent. Multiply by (50/EW) where EW = atomic wt ÷ valency.
  • ✓ SOR vs Detention Time: SOR is the PRIMARY design parameter for settling tanks. Don't confuse Q/A with Q/V.
  • ✓ Population Methods: Arithmetic = lowest; Geometric = highest; Logistic = most realistic long-term. Select based on growth pattern in data.
  • ✓ Fire Demand: P must be in THOUSANDS for Kuichling/Freeman formulae. Check units carefully!
  • ✓ Chlorine pH: Remember pKa of HOCl ≈ 7.5. Operating at pH < 7.5 gives better disinfection (more HOCl).
  • ✓ Filter Comparison: SSF vs RSF - mechanism (biological vs physical), pre-treatment (none vs coagulation), rate (0.1 vs 4 m/hr).
  • ✓ Hardy Cross: Sign convention matters! CW flows positive, CCW negative. Apply correction with same sign convention.
  • ✓ CMFR vs PFR: For same conversion, CMFR always needs LARGER volume (4-10× for high conversions). Exception: zero-order reactions.
  • ✓ Time Management: Unit 1 is HIGHEST weight (15-18 Qs, ~25% of paper). Allocate 25-30 min here.
  • ✓ Marking Strategy: Skip if < 60% confident. +4 correct, -1 wrong → risk/reward 1:4, so mark confidently or skip.
  • ✓ Unit Verification: BEFORE finalising, check units. Common traps: m/day vs m/hr for SOR; mg/L vs μg/L; thousands vs actual for population.
