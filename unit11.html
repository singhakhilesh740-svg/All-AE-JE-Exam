SECTION 1: EIA — CONCEPT, HISTORY & PROCESS

Environmental Impact Assessment is the formal process to predict the environmental consequences of a proposed project, recommend mitigation, and decide on clearance. Largest section in this unit — expect 2–3 Qs.

1.1 DEFINITION & OBJECTIVES

EIA (definition): a systematic, reproducible, multidisciplinary procedure for identifying, predicting, evaluating and mitigating biophysical, social and other relevant effects of development proposals before major decisions are made and commitments given.
Objectives:
  • Identify foreseeable environmental impacts (positive & negative) of a project
  • Predict magnitude, extent and significance of impacts
  • Suggest mitigation measures & alternatives
  • Inform decision-makers and the public
  • Ensure compliance with environmental regulations & standards
  • Promote sustainable development

1.2 HISTORICAL EVOLUTION

YearMilestone

1.3 EIA NOTIFICATION 2006 — STRUCTURE

Project categorisation:
  • Category A: appraised by EAC (Expert Appraisal Committee) at central level (MoEFCC); EC issued by MoEFCC
  • Category B: appraised by SEAC (State EAC) at state level; EC issued by SEIAA; further split into:
    • B1: full EIA + public hearing required
    • B2: no EIA, no public hearing — only EMP & basic data
Coverage: 8 sectors / 39 categories of projects (mining, thermal/nuclear power, river valley, infrastructure, manufacturing, building & construction, township, etc.).

1.4 EIA PROCESS — 4 STAGES

Stage 1: SCREENING
↓ (Determine: Cat A, B1, or B2 by reference to Schedule of EIA Notification 2006)

Stage 2: SCOPING
↓ (EAC/SEAC issues Terms of Reference (ToR) within 60 days of Form 1 submission)

Stage 3: PUBLIC CONSULTATION
↓ (Public hearing by SPCB at site within 45 days; written comments via website 30 days)

Stage 4: APPRAISAL
↓ (EAC/SEAC examines Final EIA + public hearing proceedings; recommends grant/reject within 60 days)

Decision: by MoEFCC (Cat A) / SEIAA (Cat B) within 45 days of EAC/SEAC recommendation

1.5 SCREENING DETAILS

  • Promoter submits Form 1 + pre-feasibility report (and Form 1A additionally for buildings/area development/townships)
  • Determines if EIA is required and category (A/B1/B2)
  • Threshold criteria from Schedule (e.g., thermal power > 500 MW = A; < 500 MW = B; mining > 50 ha = A; 5–50 ha = B)
  • Cat B projects within 5 km of protected area / critically polluted area / inter-state or international boundary are upgraded to Cat A ("General Condition")

1.6 SCOPING — TERMS OF REFERENCE (ToR)

  • EAC/SEAC determines the scope of EIA study
  • ToR specifies what impacts to study, baseline data, methods, modelling required, alternatives, public hearing locations
  • Standard ToR available for many sectors
  • For Cat B2 — no scoping needed
  • Validity of ToR: 4 years (extendable by 2 years)

1.7 BASELINE DATA & IMPACT PREDICTION

Baseline study (3 seasons mandatory excluding monsoon, for at least 1 yr):
  • Air quality (NAAQS pollutants); meteorology (wind rose, mixing height)
  • Water quality (surface & groundwater); hydrogeology
  • Soil quality (heavy metals, fertility)
  • Noise (Leq, day/night by zone)
  • Land use, topography (Survey of India toposheet)
  • Biological environment (flora, fauna, threatened species, protected areas within 10 km)
  • Socio-economic (population, occupation, infrastructure, R&R if applicable)
  • Traffic, archaeology, geology
Study area: usually 10 km radius from project boundary (5 km for buildings).

1.9 EIS & EMP

EIS (Environmental Impact Statement) — final document submitted to authority. Standard contents (per EIA Notification 2006, Appendix III):
  1. Project description
  2. Description of environment (baseline)
  3. Anticipated environmental impacts & mitigation
  4. Analysis of alternatives (technology & site)
  5. Environmental Monitoring Programme
  6. Additional studies (Risk & Disaster Mgmt, public consultation)
  7. Project benefits
  8. Environment Cost Benefit Analysis
  9. EMP — Environment Management Plan (the most important section)
  10. Summary & conclusion
  11. Disclosure of consultants engaged (NABET-accredited mandatory since 2010)
EMP components: mitigation measures (with cost & schedule); monitoring plan (parameters, frequency, locations); institutional arrangements; reporting protocols; budgetary allocations; emergency preparedness; CSR/CER provisions.

1.10 PUBLIC CONSULTATION

  • Conducted by SPCB / UTPCC at project site within 45 days of receiving Draft EIA
  • Two components: (a) public hearing on-site, (b) written responses via website (30 days)
  • Notice in 1 major national newspaper + 1 regional language newspaper at least 30 days in advance
  • Exempt categories: all Cat B2; modernisation of irrigation projects (< 10% increase); units in industrial estates with prior EC; expansion of roads & highways; building & construction (defined small projects); offshore oil & gas; defence & strategic projects
  • Proceedings recorded; transmitted to EAC/SEAC with Final EIA

1.11 APPRAISAL & EC VALIDITY

Validity of Environmental Clearance:
  • River valley projects: 13 years (project life of construction)
  • Mining projects: 30 years (or as per mining lease, max 50 yr)
  • Area development & townships: 10 years
  • All other projects (industries, infrastructure): 5 years
Post-EC compliance: 6-monthly compliance report to MoEFCC/SEIAA; uploaded on company & ministry website.

1.12 STRATEGIC ENVIRONMENTAL ASSESSMENT (SEA) (GATE ADD)

SEA = systematic process for evaluating environmental consequences of policies, plans and programmes (PPP) at higher level than individual projects. EIA is for projects; SEA is for PPPs.
  • Originated EU Directive 2001/42/EC (in force 2004)
  • Applied to land use plans, transport plans, energy plans, sectoral policies
  • Advantages over EIA: deals with cumulative impacts; allows alternatives at strategic level; integrates environment early in decision-making
  • India: not formally adopted; some states use it for industrial corridor planning

1.13 LIFE CYCLE ASSESSMENT (LCA) (GATE ADD)

LCA (ISO 14040:2006 & 14044:2006): "cradle-to-grave" analysis of environmental aspects & potential impacts of a product / process / service across its entire life cycle.
  • Stages: raw material extraction → processing → manufacture → distribution → use → end-of-life (reuse/recycle/disposal)
  • 4 phases (ISO 14040):
    1. Goal & Scope definition (system boundary, functional unit, e.g., 1 kg cement, 1 km travelled)
    2. Inventory analysis (LCI) — inputs (energy, materials) & outputs (emissions, waste) at each stage
    3. Impact Assessment (LCIA) — characterise impacts (GWP, AP, EP, ODP, HTP); methods: CML, ReCiPe, TRACI
    4. Interpretation — sensitivity analysis; conclusions; recommendations
  • Variants: Cradle-to-gate, Cradle-to-cradle (closed loop), Well-to-Wheel (transport fuels)
  • Software tools: SimaPro, GaBi, OpenLCA, Umberto
  • Used by businesses for product comparison, eco-labels (Type III EPDs), green procurement

1.14 HUMAN HEALTH RISK ASSESSMENT (HHRA) (GATE ADD)

HHRA = process to estimate the nature & probability of adverse health effects in humans exposed to chemicals in environmental media. 4 steps (USEPA framework):
  1. Hazard Identification: determine if a chemical is causally linked to adverse health effect (carcinogenic / non-carcinogenic)
  2. Dose-Response Assessment: quantify relationship between exposure dose & severity. Outputs:
    • Carcinogens: Slope Factor (SF) [(mg/kg-d)⁻¹]
    • Non-carcinogens: Reference Dose (RfD) [mg/kg-d]
  3. Exposure Assessment: magnitude, frequency, duration of exposure for each pathway (ingestion, inhalation, dermal). CDI = Chronic Daily Intake (mg/kg-d)
  4. Risk Characterisation:
    • Cancer Risk = CDI × SF (target: 10⁻⁶ to 10⁻⁴)
    • Non-cancer Hazard Quotient HQ = CDI / RfD; HQ ≤ 1 = safe; > 1 = potential risk
    • Hazard Index (HI) = ΣHQ (multiple chemicals/pathways)
Standard CDI for ingestion:
CDI = (C × IR × EF × ED) / (BW × AT)
C = concentration in medium (mg/L water, mg/kg soil/food)
IR = intake rate (L/d, kg/d)
EF = exposure frequency (d/yr); ED = exposure duration (yr)
BW = body weight (kg, default adult 70 kg, child 15 kg)
AT = averaging time (d) — for cancer = 70 yr × 365; for non-cancer = ED × 365

2.2 ISO 14001:2015 — EMS REQUIREMENTS

Built on PDCA (Plan-Do-Check-Act) cycle — also called "Deming cycle".
  • PLAN: establish environmental policy, identify aspects & impacts, set objectives & targets, identify legal requirements
  • DO: implement controls (training, communication, document control, operational control, emergency preparedness)
  • CHECK: monitor, measure, evaluate compliance, internal audit, corrective & preventive action
  • ACT: management review; continual improvement
2015 revision changes: High Level Structure (Annex SL — common for ISO 9001/45001/27001); risk-based thinking; leadership & commitment from top management; consideration of life cycle perspective; broader focus on context of organisation & stakeholders.

2.3 ENVIRONMENTAL ASPECT vs IMPACT

  • Aspect: element of an organisation's activities/products/services that can interact with the environment (e.g., emission of SO₂, generation of solid waste, water consumption)
  • Impact: any change to environment (adverse or beneficial) resulting from an aspect (e.g., acid rain, soil contamination, depletion of water table)
Significance criteria for ranking: severity, frequency, duration, regulatory exposure, stakeholder concern.

2.4 ENVIRONMENTAL AUDIT

Definition: systematic, documented, periodic and objective evaluation of how well the organisation, management & equipment are performing with the aim of contributing to safeguarding the environment (ICC, 1989).
  • Compliance audit: against legal requirements (e.g., consent conditions, NAAQS, NGT orders)
  • Management system audit: ISO 14001 conformance
  • Process / operational audit: energy, water, waste, raw material efficiency
  • Site audit (Phase I/II): contamination assessment; due diligence in M&A
  • Supplier audit: supply chain ESG
Audit frequency: 1st party (internal): quarterly to annual; 2nd party (customer): annual; 3rd party (certification): annual surveillance, 3-yearly recertification.
Indian regulation: Environmental Statement (Form V) mandatory under Environment (Protection) Rules 1986 — every industry holding CTO must submit by 30 September each year to SPCB for previous financial year.

SECTION 3: ENVIRONMENTAL INDICES

Composite indices simplify complex environmental data into single comparable values. WQI & AQI are most asked.

3.1 WATER QUALITY INDEX (WQI)

Definition: single dimensionless number expressing overall water quality based on selected parameters & their weights.
Common WQI methods:
  • NSF-WQI (National Sanitation Foundation, USA, 1970): 9 parameters — DO, FC, pH, BOD₅, NO₃, PO₄, ΔT, turbidity, TS
  • CCME WQI (Canadian Council)
  • CPCB WQI (India): uses subset of parameters
NSF-WQI (weighted arithmetic):
WQI = Σ (wi · qi) / Σ wi

wi = unit weight (DO 0.17, FC 0.16, pH 0.11, BOD 0.11, NO₃ 0.10, PO₄ 0.10, ΔT 0.10, turbidity 0.08, TS 0.07)
qi = quality rating sub-index (0–100) from rating curve

NSF-WQI categories:
91–100 = Excellent | 71–90 = Good | 51–70 = Medium | 26–50 = Bad | 0–25 = Very Bad

Geometric form (Bhargava, used in India):
WQI = Π qiwi (sensitive to low single values)

3.2 AIR QUALITY INDEX (AQI) — INDIA

CPCB AQI launched 17 September 2014 under Swachh Bharat Mission. Reports air quality on a 0–500 scale with 6 categories.
Uses 8 pollutants: PM₁₀, PM₂.₅, SO₂, NO₂, O₃, CO, NH₃, Pb.
AQICategoryColourHealth implications
0–50GoodDark greenMinimal impact
51–100SatisfactoryLight greenMinor breathing discomfort to sensitive
101–200ModerateYellowBreathing discomfort to people with lung/heart disease, children & older adults

AQI calculation (sub-index method):
For each pollutant compute sub-index Ip using linear interpolation between breakpoints:
Ip = [(IHI − ILO)/(BPHI − BPLO)] × (Cp − BPLO) + ILO

AQI = max(Ip) [worst-of-pollutants approach]

Reported only if data for ≥ 3 pollutants available with mandatory inclusion of one of PM₁₀ or PM₂.₅.

3.3 ECOLOGICAL FOOTPRINT & CARRYING CAPACITY

Ecological Footprint (Wackernagel & Rees, 1990s): measure of human demand on Earth's ecosystems — area of biologically productive land & water needed to provide resources consumed and absorb wastes generated.
  • Unit: global hectares (gha) per capita
  • India: ~1.2 gha/cap (2022); World avg: ~2.7 gha/cap
  • Earth's biocapacity: 1.6 gha/cap (decreasing)
  • Earth Overshoot Day: when humanity has used up nature's "annual budget" — 2024 was 1 August
Carrying capacity: maximum population an environment can sustainably support given food, habitat, water and other resources. Logistic growth model: dN/dt = rN(1 − N/K).

3.4 OTHER ENVIRONMENTAL INDICES

  • EPI (Environmental Performance Index): Yale-Columbia; 180 countries; 40 indicators in 11 categories. India ranked 176/180 in 2024 EPI.
  • NDVI (Normalised Difference Vegetation Index): remote sensing — (NIR − RED)/(NIR + RED); range −1 to +1; vegetation density
  • HDI (Human Development Index): UNDP — life expectancy + education + income (geometric mean)
  • SDG Index: by SDSN; tracks 17 SDGs; India ranked 109/166 in 2024
  • Climate Change Performance Index (CCPI): Germanwatch
  • UV Index (UVI): WHO; 0–11+; risk of UV exposure
  • Sustainability Index, Genuine Progress Indicator

SECTION 4: CSR, EPR & GREEN INITIATIVES

Sustainability instruments & certifications. Mostly factual recall.

4.1 CORPORATE SOCIAL RESPONSIBILITY (CSR)

India's first mandatory CSR law in the world — Section 135, Companies Act 2013 (in force April 2014).
  • Applies to companies with: net worth ≥ ₹500 cr OR turnover ≥ ₹1000 cr OR net profit ≥ ₹5 cr (in immediately preceding FY)
  • Spend: 2% of average net profit of preceding 3 years
  • Must constitute CSR Committee of board
  • Schedule VII activities: eradicating hunger/poverty, education, gender equality, env sustainability, art/culture, armed forces veterans, sports, PM National Relief Fund, technology incubators in academic institutions, rural development, slum development, disaster relief
  • Unspent amount to be transferred to fund (e.g., PM CARES, Swachh Bharat Kosh) within 30 days; if for ongoing project, to "Unspent CSR Account" within 30 days & spent in 3 years
  • 2021 amendment: impact assessment mandatory for CSR projects ≥ ₹1 cr (if total CSR ≥ ₹10 cr)
CER (Corporate Environmental Responsibility): additional spend mandated by EAC/SEAC during EIA appraisal — typically 0.5–2.5% of project cost based on size; conditions of EC.

4.2 EXTENDED PRODUCER RESPONSIBILITY (EPR)

EPR: producer responsible for end-of-life management of product placed on market. Reverses "polluter pays" — producer pays for collection, recycling, disposal.
  • Plastic Waste Mgmt Rules 2016 (amended 2022): EPR for 4 categories of plastic packaging (rigid, flexible, multilayered, compostable); tradable EPR certificates issued by CPCB; targets phased
  • E-Waste Mgmt Rules 2022: EPR with tradable certificates; 60% (FY 2023-24) → 80% (FY 2026-27 onward)
  • Battery Waste Mgmt Rules 2022: EPR with tradable certificates; targets up to 90% by FY 2026-27
  • Used Oil / Lubricants: EPR through HOWM Rules
  • EPR may be implemented as: take-back schemes, deposit-refund, recycling fees, PRO (Producer Responsibility Organisation)

4.4 CARBON FOOTPRINT & SCOPE EMISSIONS (GHG PROTOCOL)

GHG Protocol (WRI & WBCSD, 1998) — most widely used GHG accounting framework for organisations.
  • Scope 1 (Direct): emissions from sources owned/controlled by organisation — boilers, furnaces, vehicles, fugitive HFC leaks, process emissions (cement clinker)
  • Scope 2 (Indirect — energy): emissions from purchased electricity, steam, heating, cooling — generated off-site but used by organisation
  • Scope 3 (Indirect — value chain): 15 categories — purchased goods/services, capital goods, business travel, employee commuting, upstream/downstream transport, use of sold products, end-of-life of sold products, investments, franchises (often largest in services/finance)
Carbon footprint unit: tonnes of CO₂-equivalent (tCO₂e) using GWP factors (IPCC AR6).

4.5 ECO-LABELLING (ISO 14020 series)

  • Type I (ISO 14024): third-party certified, multi-criteria — Ecomark (India, BIS 1991, lotus logo); EU Ecolabel (flower); Blue Angel (Germany); Green Seal (USA)
  • Type II (ISO 14021): self-declared (e.g., "recyclable", "biodegradable") — manufacturer's claim
  • Type III (ISO 14025): Environmental Product Declaration (EPD); LCA-based; quantitative

4.6 INDIAN GREEN POLICY INITIATIVES

  • National Action Plan on Climate Change (NAPCC, 2008): 8 missions — Solar, Enhanced Energy Efficiency (NMEEE), Sustainable Habitat, Water, Sustaining Himalayan Ecosystem, Green India, Sustainable Agriculture, Strategic Knowledge for Climate Change
  • State Action Plans on Climate Change (SAPCC): all states
  • PAT Scheme (Perform, Achieve, Trade): under NMEEE; energy efficiency targets for designated consumers; tradable ESCerts
  • Carbon Credit Trading Scheme (CCTS) 2023: notified June 2023; India's domestic carbon market under EC (Amendment) Act 2022
  • Mission LiFE (Lifestyle for Environment): launched at COP27 (2022) by India; pro-planet behaviour change
  • Swachh Bharat Mission (2014); Namami Gange (2014); Smart Cities Mission (2015)
  • Atal Mission for Rejuvenation & Urban Transformation (AMRUT) 2015
  • National Solar Mission: 500 GW non-fossil by 2030
