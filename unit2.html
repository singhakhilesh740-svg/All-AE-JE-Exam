UNIT 4: SOLID WASTE & HAZARDOUS WASTE MANAGEMENT

UPPCB AEE 2026 | 8-10 Questions | 32-40 Marks | Comprehensive Revision Notes (+ GATE ES Additions)

SECTION 1: MUNICIPAL SOLID WASTE (MSW)

Definition, sources, composition, characterisation, and SWM Rules 2016. Foundation for all SWM-related questions.

1.1 DEFINITION & SOURCES

MSW (per SWM Rules 2016): solid or semi-solid domestic, sanitary, commercial, institutional, catering and market waste — and other non-residential waste; street sweepings, silt removed/collected from surface drains, horticulture waste, agriculture & dairy waste; excludes industrial hazardous waste, biomedical waste, e-waste, lead-acid batteries, radioactive waste.
India MSW generation (CPCB 2023):
  • Total: ~1,70,000 TPD (~62 MT/year)
  • Per capita: 200–600 g/day (urban); 100–200 (rural)
  • Collection efficiency: ~95%; Processed: ~50%; Landfilled: ~22%; Unaccounted: ~28%
  • Largest generators: Maharashtra, Tamil Nadu, UP, Karnataka, Delhi

Key observation: Indian MSW has high organic + high moisture + high inerts → low calorific value (800–1500 kcal/kg) → makes mass-burn incineration difficult; favours composting + RDF + biomethanation.

1.3 CHARACTERISATION

Physical properties:
  • Density: Loose 100–200 kg/m³; in compactor truck 350–500; in landfill (compacted) 600–900 kg/m³
  • Moisture content: M = (Wwet − Wdry)/Wwet × 100%; affects calorific value & biological treatment
  • Particle size distribution: for shredding, screening
Chemical properties:
  • Proximate analysis: moisture, volatile combustible matter, fixed carbon, ash
  • Ultimate analysis: C, H, O, N, S, ash (used to compute calorific value, theoretical air for combustion)
  • Calorific value: bomb calorimeter (HHV vs LHV)
  • C:N ratio: < 25 = inadequate C; > 35 = N-deficient (slow composting); optimum 25–30:1
Modified Dulong's formula (HHV):
HHV (kJ/kg) = 337C + 1419(H − O/8) + 93S + 23N
(C, H, O, S, N in % by mass, dry basis)

Tchobanoglous LHV (kJ/kg):
LHV = HHV − 24.42 × (9H + W) where W = moisture %

1.4 SWM RULES 2016 — KEY PROVISIONS

SWM Rules 2016 (notified by MoEFCC, 8 April 2016):
  • Source segregation mandatory: 3 streams
  •   • Green bin = wet/biodegradable
  •   • Blue bin = dry/recyclable
  •   • Black bin = domestic hazardous (sanitary, expired meds)
  • Generator responsibility: segregation at source, user fee, no littering
  • Bulk generators (> 100 kg/day OR housing > 5000 m²): on-site composting/biomethanation
  • Local body responsibility: door-to-door collection, transport, processing, scientific landfill
  • Waste-to-Energy (WtE): non-recyclable high CV (> 1500 kcal/kg) waste shall be used as RDF or for power generation
  • Landfilling: only inert & pre-treated rejects (residual after processing); < 20% of total waste
  • Buffer zone for landfills: 100 m from habitation; not within 200 m of water bodies, 200 m of HFL, 20 km from airport (or 10 km with DGCA NOC)
  • Disposal of legacy waste: bio-mining within 5 years

1.5 3R HIERARCHY & INTEGRATED SWM (GATE ADD)

Waste hierarchy (most preferred to least):
  1. Prevention (avoid generation)
  2. Minimisation (reduce at source)
  3. Reuse (without reprocessing)
  4. Recycling (process into new product)
  5. Recovery (energy / composting / biogas)
  6. Disposal (sanitary landfill — last resort)
3R = Reduce, Reuse, Recycle (extended: 5R = + Recover, Refuse / Rot).

SECTION 2: COLLECTION & TRANSPORT

2.1 COLLECTION SYSTEMS

  • Door-to-door: primary; tricycle / pushcart / mini-truck; coverage indicator under SBM
  • Community bin (DCC — Direct Container Collection): drum/skip system; older, less hygienic
  • Vacuum / pneumatic collection: underground pipes (Stockholm, parts of Singapore)
Transport vehicles: Hopper tipper (1.5–7 t), compactor truck (4:1 to 6:1 compaction), refuse collector (RC) trucks.
Transfer station: small primary vehicles → large secondary vehicles (long-haul to landfill); economical when haul distance > 20 km.

2.2 ROUTE OPTIMISATION

Heuristic rules (McGhee):
  • Routes should not fragment or overlap
  • Start route at top of hill (loaded truck downhill)
  • Heavily travelled streets serviced in early morning
  • For one-way streets, start at upper end
  • U-turns and dead-end streets minimised

SECTION 3: TREATMENT & DISPOSAL

Heart of SWM — composting, anaerobic digestion, RDF, incineration, sanitary landfill. Multiple Qs from this section.

3.1 COMPOSTING

Definition: aerobic biological decomposition of organic waste under controlled conditions to produce a stable, humus-like product (compost).
Generic reaction:
Organic + O₂ + microbes → CO₂ + H₂O + Heat + Compost (humus) + new microbial cells
Optimal conditions:
  • C:N ratio: 25–30:1 (initial); final compost ~10–15:1
  • Moisture: 50–60% (squeeze test: should hold shape but not drip)
  • Temperature stages: Mesophilic (20–45°C, 1–2 days) → Thermophilic (45–70°C, 1–2 weeks; pathogen kill) → Cooling (40–25°C) → Maturation (ambient, weeks)
  • pH: initial 5.5–6.5 (acidic); rises to 8.0–8.5 (alkaline) at maturity
  • Particle size: 25–75 mm
  • O₂: > 5–10% in pile (turn / aerate)
  • Pathogen kill: 55°C × 3 days OR 60°C × 1 day (US EPA Class A biosolids)

3.3 ANAEROBIC DIGESTION (BIOMETHANATION) OF MSW

  • Organic fraction → biogas (CH₄ 50–65%, CO₂ 30–40%) under anaerobic conditions
  • 4 stages: hydrolysis → acidogenesis → acetogenesis → methanogenesis
  • Mesophilic (35°C) or thermophilic (55°C) reactors
  • HRT: 15–30 days (mesophilic); 10–20 days (thermophilic)
  • Biogas yield: 100–150 m³/t MSW; ~600 kWh/t electricity
  • Indian examples: Pune, Indore, Mahindra World City, Mumbai

3.4 INCINERATION

Definition: controlled combustion of waste at high temperature (850–1200°C) with excess air. Reduces volume by 80–90%, mass by 70–75%.
Types:
  • Mass-burn: unsorted MSW; moving grate; needs CV > 1700 kcal/kg
  • Modular incinerator: small scale (5–100 t/d); 2-chamber controlled-air
  • Fluidised Bed Combustor (FBC): sand bed at 800–900°C; uniform combustion; low NOₓ
  • Rotary kiln: hazardous & biomedical waste; long residence time
Key combustion conditions:
  • Primary chamber: 850–950°C; residence time 1–2 s
  • Secondary chamber: ≥ 1050°C; residence time ≥ 2 s (BMW Rules); destroys dioxins/furans
  • Excess air: 50–100%
  • Rapid quenching of flue gas from 850 → 200°C to prevent dioxin re-formation (de novo synthesis at 250–400°C)
Dioxins & furans (PCDD/PCDF): formed from chlorinated organics + Cu/Fe catalyst at 250–450°C; extremely toxic; 2,3,7,8-TCDD is most toxic; emission limit: 0.1 ng TEQ/Nm³.

3.5 REFUSE-DERIVED FUEL (RDF)

Process: shredding → screening → magnetic separation → air classifier → drying → pelletisation. Removes inerts, moisture, metals.
Specifications: CV 3000–4500 kcal/kg; moisture < 25%; ash < 20%; chlorine < 0.8%.
Uses: co-firing in cement kilns (most common in India), dedicated WtE plants, industrial boilers.
Indian RDF projects: Delhi (Okhla, Ghazipur, Narela-Bawana), Hyderabad (Jawahar Nagar), Indore.

3.6 MATERIALS RECOVERY FACILITY (MRF)

Sorts and separates recyclables: paper, cardboard, plastic (PET, HDPE, PP, LDPE), glass, ferrous (magnetic), non-ferrous (eddy current). Outputs are baled and sold to recyclers. SWM Rules 2016 mandate MRF in every ULB.

3.7 SANITARY LANDFILL

Definition: engineered facility for disposal of solid waste with environmental safeguards (liner, leachate collection, gas extraction, daily cover, post-closure monitoring).
Methods:
  • Trench method: excavated trench; suitable for flat terrain with deep groundwater
  • Area method: waste placed on natural surface; cells built up; for high water table or rocky terrain
  • Ramp method: hybrid; partial excavation + ramp build-up
Components (CPCB / SWM Rules 2016):
  1. Liner system (composite): bottom up — compacted clay (≥ 90 cm, k ≤ 10⁻⁷ cm/s) + HDPE geomembrane (≥ 1.5 mm) + protective geotextile
  2. Leachate collection & removal system (LCRS): drainage layer (gravel/geonet) + perforated pipes; slope 1–2%
  3. Daily cover: 15 cm soil at end of day (controls vectors, odour, fire)
  4. Intermediate cover: 30 cm soil if cell inactive > 7 days
  5. Final cover: ≥ 60 cm clay + drainage + topsoil + vegetation
  6. Gas collection & venting/flare/utilisation: mandatory if > 1 lakh tonnes
  7. Stormwater drainage & monitoring wells (upstream + downstream)
  8. Buffer zone: 100 m from habitation; 200 m from water body
  9. Post-closure monitoring: 15–30 years

Generation rate: depends on rainfall, infiltration, waste moisture, cover; typical 10–30% of rainfall.

3.9 LANDFILL GAS (LFG)

Composition (mature landfill): CH₄ 45–60%; CO₂ 40–55%; N₂ 2–5%; trace H₂S, NMOCs.
5 phases of LFG generation:
  1. Aerobic (hours to days) — O₂ depleted, CO₂ rises
  2. Anaerobic non-methanogenic — H₂, CO₂, organic acids
  3. Anaerobic methanogenic unsteady — CH₄ rises, CO₂ falls
  4. Anaerobic methanogenic steady — CH₄ ~50%, CO₂ ~50%; lasts 20–30 yr
  5. Maturation — gas production declines
USEPA LandGEM model (1st-order decay):
QCH4 = Σ k·L₀·Mi·e−k·t
k = methane generation rate (yr⁻¹); L₀ = methane potential (m³/Mg); M = mass; t = age

Theoretical LFG yield (Buswell):
Substrate CnHaOb + (n − a/4 − b/2)H₂O → (n/2 + a/8 − b/4)CH₄ + (n/2 − a/8 + b/4)CO₂
Methane is 28× more potent GHG than CO₂. Indian large landfills (> 1 lakh t) must collect & flare/utilise LFG (SWM Rules 2016). CDM/CER projects historically big.

SECTION 4: HAZARDOUS WASTE

Hazardous & Other Wastes (Management & Transboundary Movement) Rules 2016. High exam weightage — multiple factual Qs.

4.1 DEFINITION & CLASSIFICATION

Hazardous waste (HOWM Rules 2016): waste which by reason of its physical, chemical, biological, reactive, toxic, flammable, explosive or corrosive characteristics causes danger or is likely to cause danger to health or environment, whether alone or with other waste/substance.
Characteristics (USEPA "ICRT" + Indian schedules):
  • Ignitability (I): flash point < 60°C
  • Corrosivity (C): pH < 2 or > 12.5; corrodes steel > 6.35 mm/year
  • Reactivity (R): unstable; reacts violently with water; explosive
  • Toxicity (T): TCLP (Toxicity Characteristic Leaching Procedure) — leachate exceeds limits for 8 metals + 6 pesticides + 14 organics
HOWM Rules schedules:
  • Schedule I: 36 process-based hazardous wastes (e.g., petroleum refining, pesticide manufacturing, electroplating)
  • Schedule II: waste constituents with concentration limits (heavy metals, organics)
  • Schedule III: Part A (waste prohibited for import), Part B (allowed with permission), Part C (allowed without permission for recycling — 25 categories)
  • Schedule IV: recyclable hazardous wastes
  • Schedule VI: Annexure for transboundary movement (Basel)

4.2 COMMON HAZARDOUS WASTES IN INDIA

  • Fly ash from thermal power: 270 MT/yr; 90% utilisation target by Fly Ash Utilisation Rules 2009
  • ETP sludge: from textile, tannery, electroplating units
  • Spent solvents: from pharma, paint, dye industries
  • Spent acids/alkalis: from electroplating, batteries
  • Heavy metal sludges: Cr, Ni, Cd, Pb from electroplating
  • Phenolic wastes: from refineries, plastics
  • Oil contaminated soil/cotton waste: from refineries, garages

4.3 BASEL CONVENTION (1989; in force 1992)

Full name: Basel Convention on the Control of Transboundary Movements of Hazardous Wastes and their Disposal.
Key principles:
  • PIC (Prior Informed Consent): exporting country must obtain written consent of importing country before shipment
  • ESM (Environmentally Sound Management): mandates waste managed to protect human health & environment
  • Ban on export of hazardous waste from OECD to non-OECD (Ban Amendment, in force Dec 2019)
  • India ratified in 1992; lead implementation by MoEFCC
Related: Rotterdam Convention (1998) — PIC for chemicals/pesticides trade; Stockholm Convention (2001) — POPs (initial 12 — "dirty dozen": aldrin, chlordane, DDT, dieldrin, endrin, heptachlor, mirex, toxaphene, HCB, PCBs, dioxins, furans).

4.4 HAZARDOUS WASTE TREATMENT & DISPOSAL

Treatment options:
  • Physical: sedimentation, filtration, distillation, solvent extraction
  • Chemical: neutralisation, oxidation/reduction (Cr⁶⁺ → Cr³⁺ by NaHSO₃), precipitation (heavy metals as hydroxides at pH 8–10)
  • Biological: bioreactors, bioaugmentation (specialised microbes)
  • Thermal: incineration in rotary kiln (1100–1400°C); cement kiln co-processing (most common in India — 45+ cement plants accept HW)
  • Stabilisation/Solidification: mix with cement, lime, fly ash to immobilise contaminants
  • Vitrification: melt with glass-formers at 1200–1500°C → glass-like inert solid (for radioactive HLW)

4.5 SECURE LANDFILL (HW LANDFILL) & TSDF

Secure landfill (HW landfill) — additional requirements over MSW landfill:
  • Double composite liner: 2 HDPE geomembranes (each ≥ 2 mm) with leakage detection between
  • Double clay layer: ≥ 90 cm each
  • Leachate collection & leak detection: primary + secondary LCRS
  • Cover: ≥ 60 cm clay + geomembrane + drainage + topsoil
  • Buffer: ≥ 500 m from habitation; ≥ 1000 m from National Park / Wildlife Sanctuary
  • Pre-treatment (stabilisation) compulsory for liquid & reactive wastes
  • Restricted access: fenced, manned, weighbridge, manifest system
  • Post-closure monitoring: 30 years
TSDF (Treatment, Storage & Disposal Facility): Common facility for cluster of HW generators; concept introduced in HW Rules 2003. India has ~40 CHWTSDFs (e.g., Vapi, Ankleshwar, Hyderabad, Chennai, Roorkee, Taloja).

4.6 GROUNDWATER CONTAMINATION FROM HW (GATE ADD)

NAPLs (Non-Aqueous Phase Liquids):
  • LNAPLs (Light NAPLs, density < 1): petroleum products (gasoline, diesel, jet fuel, BTEX); float on water table; form a thin layer on top of saturated zone
  • DNAPLs (Dense NAPLs, density > 1): chlorinated solvents (TCE, PCE, carbon tet, PCBs); sink to bottom of aquifer; very difficult to remediate; pool on aquitards
Fate & transport: advection, dispersion, sorption (Koc), volatilisation, biodegradation (half-lives), retardation factor R = 1 + (ρb·Kd/n).
Remediation:
  • Pump & treat (oldest, slow)
  • Air sparging + SVE (volatile contaminants)
  • In-situ chemical oxidation (ISCO) — H₂O₂, KMnO₄, persulphate
  • Bioremediation (intrinsic, enhanced, bioaugmentation)
  • Permeable reactive barriers (zero-valent iron for DNAPLs, U)
  • Phytoremediation (slow; metals, organics)

SECTION 5: BIO-MEDICAL WASTE (BMW)

BMW Management Rules 2016 (amended 2018, 2019) — high probability factual Qs (colour codes, treatment).

5.1 BMW DEFINITION & CATEGORIES

BMW: any waste generated during diagnosis, treatment, or immunisation of humans/animals, in research, or in production/testing of biologicals — including sharps, body fluids, expired drugs, microbiological cultures, animal carcasses, anatomical waste.
India BMW generation: ~743 TPD (CPCB 2023); ~2 lakh HCFs.

5.3 BMW TREATMENT TECHNOLOGIES

  • Autoclaving: 121°C, 15 psi, 30–60 min (steam sterilisation); for Red & White category
  • Microwaving: microwave heating with moisture; faster than autoclave
  • Hydroclaving: rotating chamber + steam (more efficient than autoclave)
  • Incineration: primary chamber 800–900°C; secondary chamber ≥ 1050°C, residence ≥ 2 s; for Yellow category; PM 50 mg/Nm³, dioxins/furans 0.1 ng TEQ/Nm³
  • Shredding: mandatory after autoclave for Red & White (prevents reuse)
  • Deep burial: only allowed in towns < 5 lakh population & rural areas; ≥ 2 m depth + 50 cm soil cover + lime layer + fenced
CBWTF (Common Bio-Medical Waste Treatment Facility): common facility for cluster of HCFs (max 75 km radius); GPS tracking, bar-coding mandatory; one CBWTF per district per BMW Rules 2016.
India: ~200 CBWTFs operational (CPCB 2023).

SECTION 6: E-WASTE & PLASTIC WASTE

6.1 E-WASTE (MANAGEMENT) RULES 2022

Notified: 2 November 2022; effective 1 April 2023; supersedes E-Waste Rules 2016.
Coverage: 21 categories of EEE (extended from 21 to 106 in Schedule I).
Key features:
  • EPR (Extended Producer Responsibility): producer responsible for end-of-life collection & recycling
  • EPR Targets: 60% (2023–24), 70% (2024–25), 80% (2026–27 onwards) of quantity placed on market
  • EPR certificates: tradable; issued by registered recyclers; producers must obtain & surrender certificates equal to target
  • Centralised online portal by CPCB for registration & reporting
  • Mandatory authorisation for manufacturers, producers, refurbishers, recyclers
  • Restrictions on hazardous substances in EEE: Pb, Hg, Cd, Cr⁶⁺, PBB, PBDE (similar to EU RoHS)
India e-waste generation: ~1.6 MT/year (2022); ~95% handled by informal sector historically.

6.2 PLASTIC WASTE MANAGEMENT RULES 2016 (amended 2021, 2022)

  • Carry bag thickness: minimum 120 microns (raised from 75 µm in Sep 2021; from 50 µm originally)
  • Single-Use Plastics (SUP) ban (1 July 2022): 19 items including straws, cutlery, plates, ear buds, balloons sticks, candy/ice-cream sticks, thermocol decorations, plastic flags, cigarette packets film, plastic banners < 100 µm
  • EPR for plastic packaging: 4 categories (rigid, flexible, multilayered, compostable); targets phased
  • Compostable plastic: certified by CPCB (IS/ISO 17088)
  • No littering of plastic waste; segregation at source
  • Municipal authority responsible for collection, segregation, recycling, disposal
  • Use of plastic in road construction (CPCB / Ministry of Road Transport guideline)

6.3 C&D WASTE MANAGEMENT RULES 2016

  • Generators (> 20 t in single load OR > 300 t/month) must submit waste mgmt plan to ULB
  • Segregation into 4 categories: concrete, soil, steel/wood/plastics, bricks/masonry
  • ULB must set up C&D processing facility within 1 km radius of every 50 km² OR for cities > 1 million population
  • Use of recycled aggregates: ≥ 10% in non-structural applications mandated
  • India C&D waste: ~150–500 MT/year (estimated; under-reported)

  • ✓ Landfill buffer: 100 m hab / 200 m water / 20 km airport — these 3 are commonly asked together.
  • ✓ Liner thickness: clay ≥ 90 cm + HDPE ≥ 1.5 mm (MSW); double liner with 2 mm HDPE for HW (secure landfill).
  • ✓ Plastic ≥ 120 microns: raised from 75 µm in Sept 2021; before that 50 µm. Always cite 120 µm in current answer.
  • ✓ Dioxin formation T: 250–450°C (de novo synthesis) — quench fast to avoid.
  • ✓ India MSW CV LOW (~800–1500): hence mass-burn struggles; favour composting + RDF + biomethanation.
  • ✓ EPR + tradable certificate: NEW concept under E-Waste Rules 2022 & Plastic 2022 amendment; common framing.
  • ✓ Marking strategy: Unit 4 = 8–10 Qs; Rules-based factual Qs are the bulk — memorise rule names & key thresholds.
  • ✓ Trap: SUP ban "19 items" vs "26 items" — official notification (Aug 2021, effective 1 July 2022) lists 19. Do not confuse.
