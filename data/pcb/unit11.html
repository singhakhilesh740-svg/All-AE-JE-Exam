SECTION 1: FLUID PROPERTIES & HYDROSTATICS

Fluid: Substance that continuously deforms under shear stress; includes liquids and gases
Key Fluid Properties
Property Symbol SI Unit Definition / Significance
Density ρ kg/m³ Mass per unit volume; water ≈ 1000 kg/m³ @ 4°C
Specific Weight γ = ρg N/m³ Weight per unit volume; γwater ≈ 9810 N/m³
Relative Density d = ρfluid/ρwater Dimensionless Density ratio; d=1 for water
Viscosity (Dynamic) μ Pa·s (N·s/m²) Resistance to flow; water @ 20°C ≈ 0.001 Pa·s
Viscosity (Kinematic) ν = μ/ρ m²/s Dynamic viscosity / density; affects flow patterns
Surface Tension σ N/m Force per unit length at liquid surface; capillarity effect
Bulk Modulus K Pa Resistance to compression; water ≈ 2.1×10⁹ Pa
Water @ 20°C: ρ = 1000 kg/m³, γ = 9810 N/m³, μ = 10⁻³ Pa·s, ν = 10⁻⁶ m²/s. These are high-yield constants for numericals.
Pascal's Law & Manometers
Pascal's Law: Pressure at a point in static fluid is same in all directions
P = ρgh
where ρ = density (kg/m³) | g = 9.81 m/s² | h = depth (m)
  • Hydrostatic Pressure: Only vertical component matters; horizontal pressure = 0
  • Absolute Pressure: P_abs = P_atm + P_gauge
  • Gauge Pressure: P_gauge = P_abs − P_atm (pressure above atmospheric)
  • Vacuum Pressure: Negative gauge pressure (below atmospheric)
Manometer Type Application Pressure Range
U-tube Manometer Pressure difference measurement; simple 0–50 kPa
Single-leg Manometer Low pressures; gauge pressure only 0–10 kPa
Inclined Manometer Very low pressures; enhanced readability 0–1 kPa
Mercury Barometer Atmospheric pressure; reference (101.325 kPa = 760 mmHg) Atmospheric only
Buoyancy & Metacentric Height
Archimedes' Principle: Buoyant Force = Weight of Displaced Fluid = ρgV
where V = volume of object submerged (m³)
  • For Floating Objects: Buoyant force = Weight of object (equilibrium)
  • Metacentric Height (GM): Distance between center of gravity (G) and metacenter (M); determines vessel stability
  • Stable if: GM > 0 (M above G) → vessel returns to upright after tilt
  • Unstable if: GM < 0 (M below G) → vessel capsizes
  • Formula: GM = BM – BG where BM = metacentric radius, BG = distance from buoyancy center to gravity
High-yield for floating structures (docks, boats, water treatment tanks). If a question asks about stability of storage tanks, check metacentric height.
Flow Classification
Classification Type 1 Type 2 Significance
By Time Steady Flow Unsteady Flow Properties don't change (steady) vs change (unsteady) with time
By Space Uniform Flow Non-uniform Flow Velocity same (uniform) vs different (non-uniform) across section
By Reynolds Number Laminar (Re < 2300) Turbulent (Re > 4000) Smooth/parallel layers vs chaotic eddies
By Energy Grade Ideal Flow Real Flow No friction losses (ideal) vs friction losses (real)
Reynolds Number & Flow Regime
Reynolds Number: Re = ρvD/μ = vD/ν
where ρ = density | v = velocity | D = diameter | μ = dynamic viscosity | ν = kinematic viscosity

Laminar

Re < 2300
Smooth layers; parabolic velocity profile; f = 64/Re

Transition

2300 ≤ Re ≤ 4000
Unstable; neither fully laminar nor turbulent

Turbulent

Re > 4000
Chaotic; rough velocity profile; f from Moody chart
For water in pipes (D < 100 mm, v ≈ 1–2 m/s), typical Re ≈ 10,000–100,000 (turbulent). Laminar rare in practice.
Continuity Equation
Continuity (Conservation of Mass): Q = A₁V₁ = A₂V₂ = constant
where Q = discharge (m³/s) | A = area (m²) | V = velocity (m/s)
Principle: If pipe narrows, velocity increases (to maintain constant flow)
  • Discharge Q: Volume of fluid passing per unit time; Q = A × V
  • If A↓ → V↑ (narrower section = faster flow)
  • If A↑ → V↓ (wider section = slower flow)
Darcy-Weisbach Equation (Friction Losses)
Head Loss (Major Loss): hf = f × (L/D) × (V²/2g)
where f = Darcy friction factor | L = pipe length (m) | D = diameter (m) | V = velocity (m/s) | g = 9.81 m/s²
  • Head Loss increases with: Pipe length L, velocity V², friction factor f
  • Head Loss decreases with: Larger diameter D
  • Friction Factor f: Depends on Reynolds number & pipe roughness (ε)
  • Laminar (Re < 2300): f = 64/Re (independent of roughness)
  • Turbulent (Re > 4000): f from Moody Chart (depends on Re & relative roughness ε/D)
Moody Chart: Graphical plot of Darcy friction factor vs Reynolds number for different relative roughness (ε/D). High-yield for locating f values; may be provided in exam.
Minor Losses (Local Losses)
Minor Loss Formula: hm = K × (V²/2g)
where K = loss coefficient (dimensionless); depends on fitting type
Fitting Type K Value Notes
Sudden Expansion 0.3–0.5 Dependent on area ratio; highest loss
Sudden Contraction 0.04–0.5 Varies with contraction ratio
90° Elbow (flanged) 0.27–0.3 Smooth turns reduce K; threaded = higher
45° Elbow (flanged) 0.16 Less loss than 90°
Gate Valve (open) 0.2 Fully open; partially closed → much higher K
Check Valve 2.0–2.5 High loss; prevents backflow
Exit (pipe to reservoir) 1.0 Full kinetic energy lost
Entrance (reservoir to pipe) 0.5 (sharp) to 0.05 (rounded) Rounded inlets reduce loss
Total head loss = Major loss (Darcy) + Minor loss (local). In short pipes with many fittings, minor losses can dominate. Always account for both in design.
Pipe Networks & Hardy Cross Method
  • Pipe Network: System of interconnected pipes; water distribution systems are networks
  • Hardy Cross Method: Iterative solution for unknown flows in looped networks
  • Principle: At each junction, inflow = outflow (continuity); around each loop, sum of head losses = 0
  • Process: Assume initial flows → calculate head losses → correct flows → repeat until convergence
  • Applications: Water supply distribution, sewage collection, industrial process piping
Branching Network: Tree-like structure (one path between points); simpler than looped networks; only continuity equations needed

Looped (Grid) Network: Multiple paths between points (loops); more complex; Hardy Cross or matrix methods required
Centrifugal Pump Fundamentals
  • Type: Positive displacement pump; uses rotating impeller to impart kinetic energy to fluid
  • Components: Impeller, casing, suction port, discharge port, shaft, bearings
  • Operating Principle: Impeller rotates → throws fluid radially outward → pressure build-up → discharge through outlet
  • Advantages: High discharge, suitable for high flow rates, smooth operation, low maintenance
  • Disadvantages: Cannot self-prime (needs priming), low discharge head for small pumps
Pump Performance Characteristics
Parameter Symbol Definition / Formula
Head (Discharge Head) H Energy per unit weight added by pump (m); measured from discharge minus suction (gauge)
Discharge Q Volume of fluid pumped per unit time (m³/s or l/s)
Power (Hydraulic) P_h P_h = ρgQH (Watts); power transferred to fluid
Power (Brake/Shaft) P_brake Actual input power from motor; P_brake = P_h / η
Efficiency (Overall) η η = P_h / P_brake (typically 0.7–0.85 for centrifugal pumps)
Specific Speed Ns Ns = (N × Q^0.5) / H^0.75 (N in rpm, Q in m³/s, H in m); pump classification
Specific Speed (Ns): Ns = (N√Q) / H^0.75
where N = pump speed (rpm) | Q = discharge (m³/s) | H = head (m)
Ns < 50: Centrifugal (high head, low Q)
Ns = 50–300: Mixed flow
Ns > 300: Axial flow (low head, high Q)
NPSH & Cavitation
Net Positive Suction Head (NPSH): NPSH_available = (P_atm – P_vapor)/ρg – h_f,suction
where P_atm = atmospheric pressure | P_vapor = vapor pressure of liquid | h_f,suction = suction line losses
  • NPSH Required (NPSHR): Minimum suction head required by pump (given by manufacturer)
  • NPSH Available (NPSha): Actual suction head available at site
  • Cavitation Criterion: NPSH_a > NPSH_r (if violated → cavitation occurs)
  • Cavitation: Vapor bubbles form in suction → collapse near impeller → noise, vibration, erosion, performance drop
  • Prevention: Reduce suction lift, minimize suction losses, increase inlet pressure, cool liquid
Cavitation is destructive. Always ensure NPSH_available ≥ NPSH_required with safety margin (typically 0.5 m minimum).
Pump Operating Conditions & Affinity Laws
  • Operating Point: Intersection of pump curve (H vs Q) and system curve (friction losses)
  • Best Efficiency Point (BEP): Optimal discharge on pump curve; highest efficiency
  • Throttling: Reducing flow by closing discharge valve; moves operating point, reduces efficiency
  • Variable Speed Drive: Efficient method to vary flow; adjusts entire pump curve
Affinity Laws (for geometrically similar pumps):
When speed changes from N₁ to N₂:
Q₂/Q₁ = N₂/N₁ (Discharge proportional to speed)
H₂/H₁ = (N₂/N₁)² (Head proportional to speed squared)
P₂/P₁ = (N₂/N₁)³ (Power proportional to speed cubed)
Key insight: Small speed changes dramatically affect power (cubic relationship).
Affinity laws are high-yield for numericals. If pump speed doubles, discharge doubles, head quadruples, power increases 8×. Practice 3–4 problems on this.
Pumps in Series & Parallel
Configuration Effect on Head Effect on Discharge Use Case
Series H_total = H₁ + H₂ (heads add) Q_total = Q₁ = Q₂ (same flow) High head requirement; same discharge
Parallel H_total = H₁ = H₂ (same head) Q_total = Q₁ + Q₂ (flows add) High discharge requirement; same head
Series Pumps: Output of first pump is input to second; useful for very high heads (ex: booster pumps in long distribution lines)

Parallel Pumps: Both pump to same header; useful for high discharge (ex: water supply from multiple sources)
Open Channel Flow Fundamentals
  • Open Channel: Flow with free surface exposed to atmosphere (rivers, canals, sewers with partial flow)
  • Contrast to Closed Pipes: Pressure at surface = atmospheric (constant); slope drives flow
  • Uniform Flow: Depth & velocity constant along channel (Chezy/Manning apply)
  • Non-uniform Flow: Depth/velocity changes; more complex analysis needed
Manning's Equation (Open Channels)
Manning's Equation: V = (1/n) × R^(2/3) × S^(1/2)
Or: Q = (A/n) × R^(2/3) × S^(1/2)
where n = Manning's roughness coefficient | R = hydraulic radius = A/P | A = cross-sectional area | P = wetted perimeter | S = bed slope
Channel Type Manning's n Condition
Concrete (smooth) 0.012–0.015 New, clean
Concrete (rough) 0.018–0.025 Old, algae growth
Natural earth channel 0.025–0.035 Straight, clean
Natural earth (with weeds) 0.035–0.050 Dense vegetation
River (clean) 0.030–0.040 Straight reach
River (meandering) 0.040–0.060 Bends, vegetation
Sewer (concrete pipe) 0.011–0.015 Clean; gravity flow
Manning's n is high-yield parameter. For exam numericals, expect n values given or standard (concrete = 0.015, earth = 0.03). Practice 5 problems on Manning's equation.
Chezy's Formula (Alternative)
Chezy's Equation: V = C × √(R × S)
where C = Chezy's roughness coefficient; relationship with Manning: C = R^(1/6) / n
  • Chezy vs Manning: Both empirical for uniform flow; Manning preferred in practice (n values well-documented)
  • Hydraulic Radius R: For rectangular channel: R = (B × D) / (B + 2D) where B = width, D = depth
Critical Flow & Froude Number
Froude Number: F = V / √(g × D_m)
where V = velocity | g = 9.81 m/s² | D_m = hydraulic depth = A/B (mean depth for rectangular channel)

Subcritical (F < 1)

Slower flow
Depth > critical; gravity dominates; upstream disturbances propagate downstream

Critical (F = 1)

Transition point
Depth = critical depth; specific energy minimum

Supercritical (F > 1)

Faster flow
Depth < critical; inertia dominates; upstream disturbances CANNOT travel upstream
Critical Depth (Rectangular Channel): D_c = (q²/g)^(1/3)
where q = discharge per unit width = Q/B
Hydraulic Jump
Hydraulic Jump: Sudden transition from supercritical to subcritical flow; violent, turbulent; significant energy loss
  • Occurs when: Supercritical flow (F > 1) transitions to subcritical (F < 1)
  • Common causes: Spillway toe, downstream constriction, sudden slope change
  • Characteristics: Rapid rise in water surface, large surface waves, air entrainment, noise
  • Energy Loss: Significant; used in dissipators to protect downstream channel
  • Applications: Stilling basin design, spillway energy dissipation, channel protection
Conjugate Depths (Hydraulic Jump Relation):
For rectangular channel: y₂/y₁ = [√(1 + 8F₁²) – 1] / 2
where y₁ = upstream depth, y₂ = downstream depth, F₁ = upstream Froude number
Hydraulic jump is visually striking and frequently tested. Recognize it in diagrams; know it occurs when supercritical → subcritical; causes energy loss (useful for design).
Water Hammer & Surge Tanks
Water Hammer (Surge): Sudden pressure rise/fall due to rapid valve closure or pump shutdown; can damage pipes (explosion or implosion)
  • Cause: Rapid deceleration of water column → momentum transfer → pressure spike
  • Magnitude: Can exceed 2× system pressure; depends on velocity change & closure time
  • Prevention Methods: Slow valve closure, surge tanks, relief valves, air chambers
Protection Device Principle Application
Surge Tank Open reservoir on pipeline; water level rises/falls to absorb pressure changes Hydropower penstocks, long transmission lines
Air Chamber Sealed tank with trapped air; air compresses to absorb surge Pump discharge lines, household water systems
Relief Valve Opens automatically when pressure exceeds threshold; diverts flow Pressure systems, pump discharge
Slow Closure Gradual valve closure (motorized operators); reduces rate of momentum change Check valves, isolation valves on critical systems
High-Frequency Formulae
1. Reynolds Number Re = ρvD/μ = vD/ν Example: Water (ν = 10⁻⁶ m²/s) in pipe D = 0.1 m, v = 1 m/s: Re = 1 × 0.1 / 10⁻⁶ = 100,000 (Turbulent)
2. Continuity Equation Q = A × V
Example: Pipe A = 0.01 m², V = 2 m/s: Q = 0.01 × 2 = 0.02 m³/s = 20 l/s
3. Darcy-Weisbach (Major Loss) hf = f × (L/D) × (V²/2g) Example: Pipe: L = 1000 m, D = 0.5 m, V = 1 m/s, f = 0.02, g = 9.81 m/s²: hf = 0.02 × (1000/0.5) × (1²/(2×9.81)) = 0.02 × 2000 × 0.051 = 2.04 m
4. Minor Loss hm = K × (V²/2g) Example: 90° elbow (K = 0.3), V = 2 m/s: hm = 0.3 × (4/(2×9.81)) = 0.3 × 0.204 = 0.061 m
5. Pump Hydraulic Power P_h = ρ × g × Q × H
where ρ = 1000 kg/m³, g = 9.81 m/s², Q in m³/s, H in m
Example: Q = 0.05 m³/s, H = 50 m: P_h = 1000 × 9.81 × 0.05 × 50 = 24,525 W ≈ 24.5 kW
6. Affinity Laws Q₂ = Q₁ × (N₂/N₁)
H₂ = H₁ × (N₂/N₁)²
P₂ = P₁ × (N₂/N₁)³
Example: Pump @ 1000 rpm: Q = 0.1 m³/s, H = 20 m, P = 20 kW. At 1500 rpm: Q₂ = 0.1 × (1500/1000) = 0.15 m³/s
H₂ = 20 × (1.5)² = 45 m
P₂ = 20 × (1.5)³ = 67.5 kW
7. Manning's Equation V = (1/n) × R^(2/3) × S^(1/2)
Example: Concrete channel n = 0.015, R = 1 m, S = 0.001: V = (1/0.015) × 1^(2/3) × √0.001 = 66.67 × 1 × 0.0316 = 2.1 m/s
8. Froude Number F = V / √(g × D_m)
Example: Rectangular channel: B = 5 m, D = 1 m, Q = 10 m³/s: V = Q / (B×D) = 10 / 5 = 2 m/s
D_m = A/B = (5×1)/5 = 1 m
F = 2 / √(9.81 × 1) = 2 / 3.13 = 0.64 (Subcritical)
Sample Numericals
Q1: Darcy-Weisbach with Multiple Losses
Water flows through a 100 m steel pipe (D = 0.1 m) @ v = 1.5 m/s. Pipe includes one 90° elbow (K=0.3) and entrance loss (K=0.5). Assume f = 0.025. Find total head loss.
Solution:
Major loss: hf = 0.025 × (100/0.1) × (1.5²/(2×9.81)) = 0.025 × 1000 × 0.1145 = 2.86 m
Elbow: hm1 = 0.3 × (1.5²/(2×9.81)) = 0.3 × 0.1145 = 0.034 m
Entrance: hm2 = 0.5 × 0.1145 = 0.057 m
Total = 2.86 + 0.034 + 0.057 = 2.95 m
Q2: Affinity Laws
A centrifugal pump at 1200 rpm delivers Q = 0.08 m³/s and H = 30 m. If speed is increased to 1800 rpm, find new discharge, head, and power ratio. Original power was 25 kW.
Solution:
Speed ratio = 1800/1200 = 1.5
Q₂ = 0.08 × 1.5 = 0.12 m³/s
H₂ = 30 × (1.5)² = 30 × 2.25 = 67.5 m
P₂ = 25 × (1.5)³ = 25 × 3.375 = 84.4 kW
Q3: Manning's Equation (Trapezoidal Channel)
A trapezoidal concrete channel (n = 0.015) with bottom width B = 3 m, side slope 1:1, depth D = 1.5 m, and bed slope S = 0.0005. Find discharge.
Solution:
Area A = (B + mD) × D = (3 + 1.5) × 1.5 = 6.75 m² (for 1:1 slope, m = 1)
Perimeter P = B + 2D√(1+m²) = 3 + 2×1.5×√2 = 3 + 4.24 = 7.24 m
R = A/P = 6.75 / 7.24 = 0.932 m
V = (1/0.015) × 0.932^(2/3) × √0.0005 = 66.67 × 0.953 × 0.0224 = 1.42 m/s
Q = A × V = 6.75 × 1.42 = 9.57 m³/s
Q4: Froude Number & Critical Depth
In a rectangular channel (B = 4 m), water flows @ Q = 12 m³/s with depth D = 0.8 m. Is flow subcritical or supercritical? Find critical depth.
Solution:
V = Q / (B×D) = 12 / (4×0.8) = 3.75 m/s
D_m = 0.8 m
F = 3.75 / √(9.81×0.8) = 3.75 / 2.8 = 1.34 > 1 → SUPERCRITICAL
q = Q/B = 12/4 = 3 m³/s per m width
D_c = (q²/g)^(1/3) = (9/9.81)^(1/3) = 0.945^(1/3) = 0.98 m
Since D = 0.8 m < D_c, flow is supercritical (confirmed).
Q5: Pump Efficiency
A pump delivers Q = 0.05 m³/s against H = 40 m. Motor input power is 22 kW. Find overall efficiency and hydraulic power.
Solution:
P_h = ρgQH = 1000 × 9.81 × 0.05 × 40 = 19,620 W = 19.62 kW
η = P_h / P_brake = 19.62 / 22 = 0.892 = 89.2% (excellent efficiency)

SECTION 3: QUICK DESIGN CHECKLIST

  • Pipe Design: Check Re (laminar/turbulent) → find f (Moody chart or correlation) → calculate hf (Darcy) → add minor losses → total head loss
  • Pump Selection: Required Q and H → read pump curve → check operating point on system curve → verify NPSH available > NPSH required
  • Open Channel: Given Q, slope, Manning's n → solve Manning iteratively for depth D or use nomographs
  • Hydraulic Jump: Check Froude number; if F₁ > 1 and abrupt transition, jump occurs → major energy loss
  • Water Hammer: Rapid valve closure → pressure surge → install relief valve or surge tank if needed

SECTION 4: EXAM STRATEGIES

  1. Formulas to Drill: Reynolds, Continuity, Darcy-Weisbach, Manning, Froude, Pump Power. Know derivations + numerical patterns.
  2. Moody Chart: Understand how to use it; may be provided in exam. Know relative roughness concept.
  3. Affinity Laws: High-frequency numerical topic. Practice speed change scenarios (1.5×, 2×, 0.8×).
  4. Pipe vs Channel: Closed pipes use Darcy; open channels use Manning. Don't mix formulas.
  5. NPSH & Cavitation: Critical for pump questions. NPSH_a > NPSH_r always required.
  6. Hardy Cross: Conceptual; may ask principle but unlikely to solve in exam time.
  7. Hydraulic Jump: Recognize by F₁ > 1 → F₂ < 1 transition; causes energy loss; used in stilling basins.
Common Mistakes to Avoid:
❌ Confusing laminar (f = 64/Re) with turbulent (f from Moody)
❌ Forgetting units in hydraulic power (ρ in kg/m³, g in m/s², Q in m³/s, H in m → Watts)
❌ Using Manning's n for closed pipes (use Darcy only)
❌ Assuming NPSH available without calculating suction losses
❌ Mixing up affinity laws (power cubes; head squares; discharge linear)
❌ Forgetting + signs in continuity for networks (inflow = outflow at junctions)
