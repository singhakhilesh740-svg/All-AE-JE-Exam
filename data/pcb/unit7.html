Sound & Noise Basics
Sound: Acoustic waves causing vibration in air, measurable and predictable
Noise: Unwanted sound that is objectionable, annoying, or harmful to health
  • Frequency: 20 Hz–20 kHz (human hearing range)
  • Infrasound: <20 Hz (ex: earthquakes, volcano rumble)
  • Ultrasound: >20 kHz (ex: bats, medical ultrasound)
  • Sound Pressure Level (SPL): Quantifies acoustic pressure relative to reference pressure (20 µPa)
Decibel (dB) Scale
Sound Pressure Level (SPL) Formula: SPL (dB) = 20 × log₁₀ (P / P₀)
where P = measured sound pressure (Pa) | P₀ = reference pressure (20 µPa)
SPL is logarithmic, not linear. Every 10 dB increase = 10× sound pressure; 3 dB ≈ just noticeable change

A-Weighting (dBA)
  • Why A-weighting? Human ear is not equally sensitive to all frequencies; we hear mid-frequencies (1–5 kHz) louder than very low or very high frequencies
  • A-weighted scale: Emphasizes frequencies 1–5 kHz; de-emphasizes <500 Hz and >6 kHz
  • Notation: dBA or LAeq (for energy-equivalent level)
  • Example: 80 dB @ 100 Hz sounds quieter than 80 dB @ 1 kHz in real life; A-weighting captures this
UPPCB Noise Rules 2000 use dBA for all environmental noise standards—not dB. Always assume dBA for exam questions unless stated otherwise.
Noise Descriptors
Term Full Name Definition & Use
SPL Sound Pressure Level Instantaneous acoustic pressure; rarely used for environmental noise
Leq Energy-Equivalent Level Constant sound level over time T that contains same total energy as actual fluctuating noise; most used in UPPCB
L10 Level Exceeded 10% of Time Sound level exceeded for 10% of measurement period (loud peaks); used for traffic noise
L90 Level Exceeded 90% of Time Background/ambient level (quiet periods); 90% of time noise is above this level
Ldn or Ld/n Day-Night Level 24-hr weighted level; nighttime (10 PM–6 AM) levels penalized +10 dB to account for sleep disruption
Lnite Nighttime Level Average level during night (10 PM–6 AM)
Leq Formula (Simplified):
Leq = 10 × log₁₀ [ΣPᵢ² / Pref² / T]
where i = measurement intervals; T = total duration
Inverse-Square Law
  • Sound intensity decreases with distance: I ∝ 1/r²
  • SPL reduction from point source: For every doubling of distance, SPL drops ~6 dB
  • Formula: SPL₁ – SPL₂ = 20 × log₁₀ (r₂/r₁)
  • Example: 80 dB @ 10 m → 74 dB @ 20 m (6 dB drop)
High-yield topic for numericals. If a question gives SPL @ one distance, ask for SPL @ another—use inverse-square law.

UPPCB Enforcement & Penalties
  • Monitoring: NAMP (National Ambient Monitoring Programme) uses CAAQMS-type networks
  • Non-compliance: Fine up to ₹1 lakh, imprisonment up to 3 months, or both (Environment Act 1986)
  • Residential violation: If industrial zone SPL (75 dB) measured in residential area (limit 55 dB), prosecution under Water Act/EP Act
  • Construction hours: Restricted to 6 AM–10 PM in residential areas (further restricted in silence zones)
Designated Silence Zones (Section 3, Noise Rules 2000):
Areas within 100 m of hospitals, schools, courts, senior citizen homes—no amplified music, DJs, horn honking allowed any time
Festival/Event Exemptions (Partial)
  • Temporary exemptions may be granted for festivals/cultural events
  • Events must NOT operate 10 PM–6 AM in residential areas
  • SPL <75 dBA still enforced in commercial zones during events
  • Zero tolerance in silence zones (hospitals, courts) even during festivals
Noise Control Hierarchy
  1. Elimination: Remove or shut down source (ideal but often impractical)
  2. Substitution: Replace loud equipment with quieter alternatives (ex: electric vehicles vs IC engines)
  3. Reduction at Source: Mufflers, silencers, low-vibration machines
  4. Path Reduction: Barriers, distance, absorption (most practical for existing sources)
  5. Personal Protection: Ear plugs, earmuffs (last resort; personal responsibility)
Noise Reduction Techniques
Technique Mechanism Reduction (dB) Applications
Enclosure Rigid box around source; sound trapped inside 10–30 dBA Compressors, generators, diesel pumps
Muffler/Silencer Absorptive material; expansion chambers 10–20 dBA Vehicle exhaust, air compressor outlets
Damping Viscoelastic coating on vibrating surfaces; energy dissipated as heat 5–15 dBA Metal sheets, engine blocks, ductwork
Isolation (Vibration) Elastic supports (springs, rubber pads) decouple equipment from floor 10–20 dBA Motors, pumps, compressors, machinery mounted on resilient pads
Acoustic Barriers Dense, rigid walls block/reflect sound; gap reduces effectiveness 5–15 dBA per barrier Highway noise barriers, factory perimeter walls
Absorption Porous materials (foam, mineral wool, fiberglass) convert sound to heat 5–20 dBA Acoustic ceiling tiles, wall panels, pipe lagging
Distance/Zoning Inverse-square law: 6 dB per distance doubling 6 dBA per 2× distance Urban planning, industrial zone designation
Transmission Loss (TL) for Barriers: TL = 20 × log₁₀ (2πfm/ρc) + 10 × log₁₀ (S/A)
where f = frequency (Hz) | m = surface mass (kg/m²) | ρc = acoustic impedance | S/A = geometric factor
Practical: Doubling barrier thickness increases TL by ~3 dB
Insertion Loss (IL): Actual SPL reduction achieved by a barrier in real environment (accounts for diffraction, ground effects); typically 5–10 dBA less than TL

Hearing Protection Equipment (HPE)
  • Foam Earplugs: NRR 20–33 dB; foam must be compressed, inserted, held in place 10 sec; single-use
  • Earmuffs: NRR 20–30 dB; reusable; less effective if hair/glasses on seal surface
  • Dual Protection (plugs + muffs): NRR ≈ higher NRR + 5 dB (not additive)
  • Canal Caps: NRR 20–25 dB; intermediate between plugs and muffs
  • Custom-molded earplugs: Higher comfort, NRR 20–30 dB; expensive
NRR (Noise Reduction Rating): Attenuation in dB under ideal lab conditions; real-world reduction is 50–75% of stated NRR (due to improper insertion, movement, etc.)

Atomic Energy Act, 1962 & AERB
  • Atomic Energy Act 1962: Central legislation; authorizes production, use, possession of radioactive materials in India
  • AERB (Atomic Energy Regulatory Board): Statutory body under DAE; sets radiation safety codes, issues licenses, inspects facilities, prosecutes violations
  • Nuclear Power Corporation (NPC): Operates 24 reactors; generates 3.3% of India's electricity
  • BARC (Bhabha Atomic Research Centre): R&D; manages waste, decommissioning
  • Radiation Safety Rules 2004: Prescribe dose limits, contamination levels, waste management protocols
AERB is NOT CPCB. Radiation is federal (DAE/AERB), not state (UPPCB) jurisdiction. But UPPCB exams test basic knowledge of AERB limits & Atomic Energy Act.
High-Frequency Formulae
1. SPL Formula (Basic) SPL (dB) = 20 × log₁₀ (P / P₀) Example: If P = 2 Pa and P₀ = 20 µPa = 2×10⁻⁵ Pa: SPL = 20 × log₁₀ (2 / 2×10⁻⁵) = 20 × log₁₀ (10⁵) = 20 × 5 = 100 dB
2. SPL Difference from Distance (Inverse-Square Law) ΔL = 20 × log₁₀ (r₁ / r₂) Example: 80 dB @ 10 m. Find SPL @ 20 m: ΔL = 20 × log₁₀ (10 / 20) = 20 × log₁₀ (0.5) = 20 × (−0.301) = −6.02 ≈ −6 dB
SPL @ 20m = 80 − 6 = 74 dB
3. Addition of Multiple Sound Sources (SPL) Ltotal = 10 × log₁₀ (ΣAntilog(Li/10))
Or: Ltotal = 10 × log₁₀ (Σ10^(Li/10)) Example: Two machines: 80 dB + 80 dB: Ltotal = 10 × log₁₀ (10^(80/10) + 10^(80/10)) = 10 × log₁₀ (10⁸ + 10⁸) = 10 × log₁₀ (2×10⁸)
= 10 × (8 + 0.301) = 10 × 8.301 = 83.01 dB
Rule: Adding two equal SPLs increases by 3 dB. Two 80 dB sources = 83 dB (not 160!)
4. 3 dB Exchange Rule (Occupational Exposure) For every 3 dB increase in SPL, safe exposure time reduces by 50%: T₁ × 2^((L₂−L₁)/3) = T₂ Example: Safe limit @ 85 dB = 8 hr. Safe limit @ 88 dB? T₂ = 8 × 2^((88−85)/3) = 8 × 2¹ = 16 hours ??? (wrong direction)
Correct: @ 88 dB (3 dB higher), exposure time is HALF: 8 hr / 2 = 4 hr
5. Transmission Loss (Barrier) — Simple Form TL ≈ 20 × log₁₀ (f × m) + constant
where f = frequency (Hz) | m = mass/unit area (kg/m²) Practical Rule: Doubling mass → +3 dB TL. Doubling frequency → +6 dB TL
6. Leq (Energy-Equivalent Level) Leq = 10 × log₁₀ [1/T × ∫₀ᵀ 10^(L(t)/10) dt]
Simplified (discrete intervals): Leq = 10 × log₁₀ [1/n × Σ10^(Li/10)]
Example: Noise measured in 5 consecutive hours: 70, 75, 80, 75, 70 dB: Leq = 10 × log₁₀ [1/5 × (10^7 + 10^7.5 + 10^8 + 10^7.5 + 10^7)]
= 10 × log₁₀ [1/5 × (1×10⁷ + 3.16×10⁷ + 10×10⁷ + 3.16×10⁷ + 1×10⁷)]
= 10 × log₁₀ [1/5 × 18.32×10⁷] = 10 × log₁₀ (3.664×10⁷) = 75.64 dB
Leq gives weighted average that accounts for energy over time. Higher peaks contribute MORE because of exponential nature (10^x).
Sample Numericals
Q1: Inverse-Square Law
A diesel generator produces 90 dB @ 5 m. What is SPL @ 50 m distance?
Solution: ΔL = 20 × log₁₀ (5/50) = 20 × log₁₀ (0.1) = 20 × (−1) = −20 dB
SPL @ 50 m = 90 − 20 = 70 dB
Q2: Addition of Sources
Three identical machines each produce 80 dB. Find combined SPL.
Solution: Ltotal = 10 × log₁₀ (3 × 10^(80/10)) = 10 × log₁₀ (3 × 10⁸)
= 10 × (8 + 0.477) = 10 × 8.477 = 84.77 dB
Rule: Each source adds: first 80 dB, 2nd adds 3 dB → 83 dB, 3rd adds 1.76 dB → 84.76 dB
Q3: Noise Standards Violation
A factory operates @ 80 dB (measured Leq) in a residential zone. Check compliance.
Solution: Residential zone daytime limit = 55 dBA (Noise Rules 2000)
Measured = 80 dB → Exceeds by 25 dB
Verdict: VIOLATION → Fine + closure + improvement notice
Q4: Occupational Exposure
A worker is exposed to 88 dB Leq daily. Safe exposure limit @ 85 dB = 8 hr. How many hours is safe @ 88 dB?
Solution: Difference = 88 − 85 = 3 dB → Time reduces by 50%
Safe time @ 88 dB = 8 × 0.5 = 4 hours
Q5: Dose Equivalent
A worker receives 5 mGy of alpha radiation. Calculate dose equivalent in Sv (QF for alpha = 20).
Solution: Sv = Gy × QF = 0.005 × 20 = 0.1 Sv = 100 mSv
This exceeds annual occupational limit (50 mSv) → Immediate medical monitoring required

🔊 Noise Reduction Quick Tips
  • Inverse-Square Law: 6 dB reduction per doubling of distance
  • Addition Rule: Two equal sources = +3 dB (not 2×). Adding 10 sources ≈ +10 dB
  • Enclosure: 10–30 dB reduction (most effective)
  • Barrier: 5–15 dB (depends on frequency & mass)
  • Muffler: 10–20 dB (typical for exhaust)
  • Time Limit (3 dB rule): +3 dB → ÷2 time; −3 dB → ×2 time
☢️ Radiation Quick Facts
  • Alpha: Stopped by paper; dangerous if ingested/inhaled (QF=20)
  • Beta: Stopped by aluminum; less damaging than alpha (QF=1)
  • Gamma: Requires lead/concrete; most penetrating (QF=1)
  • LLW: Store 6–10 years → decay → regular waste
  • HLW: Deep geological storage required (thousands of years)
  • Public Limit: 1 mSv/yr; Worker: 50 mSv/yr
  • Background: 2–3 mSv/yr (natural; varies by location)
📋 Exam Strategies for Unit 7
  1. Zone Standards: Memorise 4 zones + day/night splits. 80% of MCQs test standard matching.
  2. Inverse-Square Law: Most common numerical. Practice 5 problems on distance-based SPL reduction.
  3. Addition of Sources: Know the 10×log(n) rule for n identical sources.
  4. Occupational Limits: 85 dB safe for 8 hr; 3 dB rule for time reduction.
  5. Radiation Units: Distinguish Gray (absorbed), Sievert (effect), Becquerel (activity).
  6. AERB vs CPCB: Radiation = federal (AERB); Noise = state (UPPCB). Different jurisdictions.
  7. Control Methods: Know Enclosure > Barrier > Isolation > Absorption ranking for reduction.
Common Mistakes to Avoid:
❌ Confusing dB with dBA (A-weighted)
❌ Adding SPLs linearly (80+80 ≠ 160; it's 83)
❌ Forgetting day/night split (Residential = 55/45, not uniform)
❌ Mixing AERB (radiation) with CPCB (air/water)
❌ Thinking noise barriers give full TL (real-world IL is 5–10 dB less due to diffraction)
