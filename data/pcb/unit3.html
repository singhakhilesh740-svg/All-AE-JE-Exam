UNIT 3: AIR POLLUTION & CONTROL TECHNOLOGY

UPPCB AEE 2026 | 10-13 Questions | 40-52 Marks | Comprehensive Revision Notes (+ GATE ES Additions)

SECTION 1: FUNDAMENTALS OF AIR POLLUTION

Structure of atmosphere, classification of pollutants, sources, and criteria pollutants. Conceptual base for dispersion, control, and standards.

1.1 STRUCTURE OF ATMOSPHERE

Layers (bottom to top):
  • Troposphere (0–12 km): T decreases with altitude (DALR ≈ 6.5°C/km); ~75% of atmospheric mass; weather phenomena; air pollution mostly here
  • Stratosphere (12–50 km): T increases with altitude (UV absorption by O₃); contains ozone layer (peak 20–25 km); stable, no vertical mixing
  • Mesosphere (50–85 km): T decreases; coldest layer (~ −90°C at mesopause)
  • Thermosphere (85–600 km): T rises sharply; ionosphere; auroras
  • Exosphere (> 600 km): merges with outer space
Dry air composition (by volume): N₂ = 78.09%, O₂ = 20.95%, Ar = 0.93%, CO₂ ≈ 0.042% (420 ppm, 2024), trace Ne, He, CH₄, Kr, H₂, N₂O, Xe, O₃.
Ozone layer: Peak O₃ = 2–8 ppm at 20–25 km; absorbs 97–99% of UV-B/UV-C; "good ozone" in stratosphere vs "bad ozone" (smog) in troposphere.

1.2 CLASSIFICATION OF POLLUTANTS

By origin:
  • Primary: emitted directly — SO₂, NOₓ (as NO), CO, PM, VOCs, Pb, NH₃
  • Secondary: formed in atmosphere — O₃ (ground-level), PAN, H₂SO₄/HNO₃, sulphate & nitrate aerosols, formaldehyde
By state: Gaseous (SO₂, NOₓ, CO, CO₂, O₃, VOCs, H₂S); Particulate (dust, fumes, mist, smoke, fly ash, smog).
Criteria pollutants — India NAAQS 2009 lists 12:
PM₁₀, PM₂.₅, SO₂, NO₂, O₃, CO, Pb, NH₃, Benzene, BaP, As, Ni.
(USEPA list = 6: PM, O₃, SO₂, NO₂, CO, Pb)

1.3 PARTICULATE MATTER CLASSIFICATION

  • Dust: solid, 1–200 µm; from mechanical disintegration (crushing, grinding)
  • Fumes: solid, 0.03–0.3 µm; from condensation of vapours (metallurgy)
  • Mist: liquid < 10 µm; condensation/atomization
  • Smoke: 0.01–1 µm; incomplete combustion of carbonaceous fuels
  • Fog: liquid > 10 µm; visibility < 1 km
  • Aerosol: general term — solid/liquid suspension in gas
Size-based PM:
TSP < 100 µm; PM₁₀ ≤ 10 µm (inhalable, upper airway); PM₂.₅ ≤ 2.5 µm (respirable, alveoli); PM₁ ≤ 1 µm (ultrafine, can enter bloodstream)

1.4 SOURCES OF POLLUTION

  • Point source: identifiable, localised (thermal stack, refinery vent)
  • Line source: linear (highways, railways)
  • Area source: spread (residential colony, landfill, brick kiln cluster)
  • Mobile source: vehicles, aircraft, ships
  • Fugitive emissions: unintentional leaks (valves, flanges, tanks)
Emission factor (EF): mass of pollutant / unit activity (e.g., kg SO₂ per t coal). Used when stack data is absent. USEPA AP-42 is the standard reference.
GATE ADD — Atmospheric sinks & residence times:
  • Wet deposition: rainout (within cloud) + washout (below cloud)
  • Dry deposition: gravitational settling + surface impaction
  • Chemical transformation: OH• oxidation, photolysis
Residence times: SO₂ ≈ 2–4 d; NOₓ ≈ 1 d; CH₄ ≈ 12 yr; N₂O ≈ 114 yr; CO₂ ≈ 100–1000 yr; CFCs 50–100 yr.

1.5 INDOOR AIR POLLUTION (GATE ADD)

  • Sources: biomass burning (chulha), tobacco smoke, paints/solvents (VOCs), plywood (HCHO), soil (Rn-222), asbestos, mould
  • Key pollutants: PM₂.₅, CO, NO₂, formaldehyde, radon, VOCs
  • Sick Building Syndrome (SBS): headache, irritation, fatigue — poor ventilation, low humidity, VOCs
  • WHO: household air pollution causes ~3.2 million premature deaths/year globally
  • PMUY (Ujjwala): Indian scheme providing LPG to reduce indoor biomass burning

SECTION 2: SPECIFIC POLLUTANTS & HEALTH EFFECTS

Properties, sources, chemistry and health/environment impacts of each criteria pollutant. High-frequency MCQ area (3–4 Qs).

2.1 PARTICULATE MATTER (PM)

Sources: coal/diesel/biomass combustion, road dust, construction, cement & steel, secondary aerosols (sulphate, nitrate, organic).
Health:
  • PM₁₀ → upper airway: bronchitis, cough
  • PM₂.₅ → alveoli → bloodstream; IARC Group 1 carcinogen; lung cancer, CVD, stroke, premature mortality
  • AQLI 2023: long-term PM₂.₅ reduces life expectancy — India ~5.3 yr; Delhi ~11.9 yr
Environmental: reduced visibility (Rayleigh & Mie scattering), building soiling, global dimming, cloud nucleation.

2.2 SULPHUR DIOXIDE (SO₂)

Sources: coal/oil combustion (thermal power, refineries), Cu/Zn/Pb smelting, H₂SO₄ plants, volcanoes.
SO₂ + OH• → HSO₃•
HSO₃• + O₂ → HO₂• + SO₃
SO₃ + H₂O → H₂SO₄ (acid rain)
Residence time ≈ 2–4 days
Health: bronchoconstriction, asthma, eye/throat irritation. Olfactory threshold ≈ 0.5 ppm.
Environmental: acid rain (pH < 5.6); Taj Mahal damage → Taj Trapezium Zone (10,400 km², SC 1996 order); vegetation injury > 0.3 ppm.

2.3 OXIDES OF NITROGEN (NOₓ)

NOₓ = NO + NO₂. NO is primary emission; NO₂ formed by oxidation.
Formation mechanisms:
  • Thermal NOₓ (Zeldovich): N₂ + O₂ → 2NO at > 1500°C (dominant in combustion)
  • Fuel NOₓ: N bound in fuel (coal) oxidises
  • Prompt NOₓ (Fenimore): rapid, in flame front via HCN intermediate
Sources: vehicles (~40% urban), thermal power, industrial boilers, lightning.
Health: NO₂ ~4× more toxic than NO; pulmonary oedema at high conc; chronic bronchitis.
Environmental: HNO₃ (acid rain), precursor to tropospheric O₃ & PAN, brown haze.

2.4 CARBON MONOXIDE (CO)

Sources: incomplete combustion — vehicles (50–60%), biomass burning, tobacco smoke. Highest during cold starts.
Hb + CO → COHb (carboxyhaemoglobin)
Hb affinity for CO is ~240× that for O₂
Effect: hypoxia (tissue O₂ starvation)
Health by COHb%:
  • 2–5%: impaired judgment, reduced exercise capacity (smokers)
  • 10–20%: headache, fatigue, dizziness
  • 30–50%: nausea, confusion, loss of consciousness
  • > 60%: death
Environmental: not a direct GHG but oxidises to CO₂ and depletes OH• radical (indirect warming).

2.5 VOCs & HYDROCARBONS

Definition (CPCB): organics with vapour pressure > 0.01 kPa at 20°C or boiling point ≤ 250°C.
Key VOCs:
  • Benzene (C₆H₆): IARC Group 1; causes leukaemia (AML); NAAQS annual = 5 µg/m³
  • Formaldehyde (HCHO): irritant, carcinogen, from plywood/paints
  • 1,3-Butadiene: carcinogen, from vehicles & tyre industry
  • PAHs: 16 priority PAHs (USEPA); Benzo[a]pyrene — IARC Group 1
Role in smog: VOCs + NOₓ + sunlight → O₃ + PAN.

2.6 LEAD (Pb)

  • Historical source: tetraethyl lead in petrol; India phased out April 2000
  • Current: Pb-acid battery manufacture/recycling, smelting, paints
  • Health: affects CNS in children (reduced IQ), anaemia (inhibits haem synthesis), kidney damage
  • Bioaccumulates in bone (half-life 20–30 yr)
  • WHO BLL of concern: > 5 µg/dL
  • NAAQS: 0.5 µg/m³ (annual)

2.8 ACID RAIN

Definition: pH < 5.6 (pure rain ≈ 5.6 due to dissolved CO₂ → H₂CO₃).
2SO₂ + O₂ → 2SO₃; SO₃ + H₂O → H₂SO₄
4NO₂ + O₂ + 2H₂O → 4HNO₃
~65–70% acidity from H₂SO₄; ~30–35% from HNO₃
Effects:
  • Aquatic: fish kills at pH < 5; release of toxic Al³⁺ from soils into lakes
  • Soil: leaching of Ca²⁺, Mg²⁺, K⁺; mobilisation of Al, Hg
  • Forests: Waldsterben (forest dieback) — Germany; needle damage
  • Buildings: marble/limestone corrosion — Taj Mahal, Parliament House
  • Reaction: CaCO₃ + H₂SO₄ → CaSO₄ + CO₂ + H₂O

Photochemical smog chemistry:
(1) NO₂ + hν (λ < 430 nm) → NO + O•
(2) O• + O₂ + M → O₃ + M
(3) O₃ + NO → NO₂ + O₂ (recycles)
(4) RH + OH• → R• → RO₂• → RO• → aldehydes + PAN (CH₃COO-O-NO₂)
PAN is a powerful eye irritant and plant toxin.
GATE ADD — Stratospheric ozone depletion (Rowland-Molina):
CFCl₃ + hν (UV) → CFCl₂• + Cl•
Cl• + O₃ → ClO• + O₂
ClO• + O• → Cl• + O₂ [Cl• regenerated — chain reaction]
Net: O₃ + O• → 2O₂; 1 Cl destroys ~100,000 O₃ molecules
ODP: CFC-11 = 1.0 (ref); CFC-12 = 1.0; Halon-1301 ≈ 10; HCFCs = 0.02–0.11; HFCs = 0.
Montreal Protocol (1987): phase-out CFCs, halons. Kigali Amendment (2016): adds HFCs (high GWP).

SECTION 3: METEOROLOGY & DISPERSION

Core numerical area. Gaussian plume, plume rise, stability classes. Expect 1–2 calculation Qs.

3.1 LAPSE RATES & STABILITY

Dry Adiabatic Lapse Rate (DALR):
Γd = g/cp = 9.81 / 1005 ≈ 9.8 °C/km ≈ 1°C / 100 m
(rate at which dry air parcel cools as it rises with no heat exchange)

Environmental Lapse Rate (ELR): actual measured profile; ISA avg ≈ 6.5 °C/km
Saturated ALR: ~5–6 °C/km (latent heat released during condensation)
Stability criteria:
  • Super-adiabatic (ELR > DALR): UNSTABLE — strong vertical mixing, good dispersion
  • Adiabatic/neutral (ELR = DALR): NEUTRAL
  • Sub-adiabatic (ELR < DALR): STABLE — restricted mixing
  • Isothermal (dT/dz = 0): very stable
  • Inversion (dT/dz > 0, T rises with height): extremely stable — traps pollutants

3.3 PASQUILL STABILITY CLASSES

Six classes based on surface wind, daytime insolation, night cloud cover:
  • A — extremely unstable (strong sun, light wind)
  • B — moderately unstable
  • C — slightly unstable
  • D — neutral (cloudy day or night)
  • E — slightly stable (night)
  • F — moderately stable (clear night, light wind)
Used to select dispersion coefficients σy, σz in the Gaussian equation.

3.4 INVERSIONS & MIXING HEIGHT

Types:
  • Radiation inversion: ground cools rapidly on clear nights (most common; winter mornings in N. India)
  • Subsidence inversion: sinking air warms adiabatically (high-pressure systems)
  • Frontal inversion: warm air overrides cold along a front
  • Advection inversion: warm air moves over cold surface
Mixing height (Hmix): height to which pollutants can disperse vertically.
Delhi winter Hmix ≈ 100–200 m (severe) vs summer ≈ 2000–3000 m — explains winter AQI spikes.

3.5 GAUSSIAN PLUME MODEL

Assumes steady state, Gaussian distribution in y (cross-wind) and z (vertical).
General Gaussian equation (continuous point source):
C(x,y,z) = [Q / (2π·σy·σz·u)] × exp[−y²/(2σy²)] × { exp[−(z−H)²/(2σz²)] + exp[−(z+H)²/(2σz²)] }

Ground-level centreline (y=0, z=0):
C(x,0,0) = [Q / (π·σy·σz·u)] × exp[−H²/(2σz²)]

Maximum ground-level concentration (occurs at σz = H/√2):
Cmax = 2Q / (π·e·u·H²) × (σy/σz)

Q = emission rate (g/s); u = wind at stack (m/s); H = effective stack height (m)
σy, σz = dispersion coefficients (m) from Pasquill-Gifford curves

3.6 PLUME RISE — HOLLAND'S FORMULA

Δh = (vs·d / u) × [1.5 + 2.68×10⁻³ · P · (Ts − Ta)/Ts · d]

Δh = plume rise (m); vs = exit velocity (m/s); d = stack dia (m); u = wind (m/s)
P = atm. pressure (kPa, ≈ 101.3); Ts, Ta = stack & ambient T (K)

Effective stack height: H = hs + Δh
Plume rise insight: Δh ↑ with exit velocity, stack diameter, (Ts−Ta); Δh ↓ with wind speed. Holland's formula is conservative (underpredicts rise). Briggs' formula more accurate for tall stacks.

3.7 WIND ROSE DIAGRAM

Graphical tool showing frequency of wind direction and speed. Uses:
  • Siting of industries (locate downwind of city)
  • Design of air monitoring networks
  • Inputs for dispersion models
Convention: wind rose shows direction wind is blowing FROM (not TO).
GATE ADD — Topographic & urban effects:
  • Urban Heat Island (UHI): city 2–8°C warmer; concrete/asphalt thermal mass, low vegetation, anthropogenic heat; local circulation traps pollutants
  • Valley effects: cold air drainage at night traps pollutants (Dehradun, Shimla)
  • Coastal effects: sea breeze (day), land breeze (night) — fumigation common
  • Building downwash: rule of thumb — stack height > 2.5× tallest building nearby

SECTION 4: AIR POLLUTION CONTROL EQUIPMENT

Most important numerical section. Deutsch-Anderson (ESP), cyclone cut-size, efficiency calcs. Expect 2–3 Qs including ≥ 1 numerical.

4.1 GRAVITY SETTLING CHAMBER

Principle: large particles settle by gravity at low horizontal gas velocity. Used for > 50 µm as pre-cleaner before ESP/bag filter.
Stokes' settling velocity: vs = g(ρp − ρg)d² / (18µ)

Minimum diameter collected (100% efficiency):
dmin = √[18µ·H / (g·ρp·t)] where t = residence time
Also: dmin = √[18µ·H·Vh / (g·ρp·L)]

Efficiency: η = vs·L / (H·Vh); η = 1 when vs·tres ≥ H
Typical efficiency 50–70% for PM > 50 µm; very poor for < 10 µm. ΔP < 1 cm H₂O.

4.2 CYCLONE SEPARATOR

Principle: centrifugal force throws particles outward; clean gas spirals upward. Best for 5–50 µm; ~80–95% for > 10 µm.
Centrifugal acceleration ≈ 5 to 2500× gravity

Cut-size (Lapple):
dpc = √[9µ·W / (2π·N·V·(ρp − ρg))]
W = inlet width; N = effective turns (~5); V = inlet gas velocity
dpc = particle size collected at 50% efficiency
Advantages: no moving parts, low cost, high T / abrasive dust OK.
Limitations: poor for < 10 µm; ΔP = 5–15 cm H₂O.
Multi-cyclone: parallel array of small cyclones — higher efficiency.

4.3 ELECTROSTATIC PRECIPITATOR (ESP)

Principle: particles pass through high-voltage corona (20–100 kV DC) → acquire negative charge → attracted to grounded collection plates → dislodged by rappers into hopper.
Used for: fly ash (mandatory on Indian thermal plants), cement, steel. Handles huge volumes.
Deutsch-Anderson equation:
η = 1 − exp(−wA/Q)

w = drift velocity (m/s); typical 0.03–0.15 m/s
A = total plate area (m²); Q = gas flow (m³/s)

SCA (Specific Collection Area): SCA = A/Q (m² per m³/s); typical 50–120 for 99%

Modified D-A with non-ideality (Matts-Öhnfeldt):
η = 1 − exp[−(wA/Q)^k]; k ≈ 0.5
Key ESP insights:
  • Efficiency > 99% for PM₁₀; ~95% for PM₂.₅
  • Resistivity optimum: 10⁴ to 10¹⁰ Ω·cm
  • High-resistivity fly ash (low-S coal) hardest to collect → needs SO₃ flue gas conditioning
  • Low ΔP (1–2 cm H₂O) — energy efficient
  • High CAPEX, low OPEX; mandatory on Indian coal plants

4.4 FABRIC FILTER / BAG HOUSE

Principle: dust-laden gas passes through woven/felted bags; dust cake forms on surface & itself acts as filter. Efficiency > 99.9% for PM down to 0.3 µm.
Cleaning mechanisms:
  • Mechanical shaking: simplest, low A/C
  • Reverse air: reverse airflow collapses bag
  • Pulse-jet: high-pressure air pulse (0.5–0.7 MPa); highest A/C; most common modern
Air-to-cloth ratio (A/C, filtration velocity):
A/C = Q / Afabric (m/min)
Shaker: 0.5–1 | Reverse-air: 0.5–2 | Pulse-jet: 2–5

Pressure drop: ΔP = ΔPfabric + K2·W·V
W = dust loading (kg/m²); V = filtration velocity
Bag materials & temperature limits:
  • Cotton < 80°C
  • Polyester < 135°C
  • Nomex (aramid) < 200°C
  • Fibreglass < 260°C
  • PTFE / Teflon < 260°C (acid resistant)
  • P84 < 240°C
Limitations: temperature, moist/sticky dust causes blinding, ΔP 10–20 cm H₂O.

4.5 WET SCRUBBERS

Principle: liquid (water) contacts dust or gas; removes both PM and soluble gases (SO₂, HCl, NH₃).
Types:
  • Spray tower: simplest; PM > 10 µm; low efficiency, low ΔP
  • Packed tower: gas absorption; Raschig rings / Pall rings / structured packing
  • Venturi scrubber: gas through throat at 60–150 m/s; water injected; > 99% for PM > 1 µm; ΔP 50–200 cm H₂O
  • Cyclonic scrubber, impingement-plate scrubber
Contact power theory (Semrau):
Nt = α · PT^β (transfer units vs contact power)
Higher ΔP → higher efficiency for fine PM
Wet scrubber handles sticky/flammable dust and does simultaneous gas + PM removal, but generates wastewater (needs treatment), visible plume, corrosion, freezing.

4.7 GASEOUS POLLUTANT CONTROL

(a) Absorption (gas-liquid): soluble gas transferred from gas to liquid in packed/tray column.
Fick's law: N = −D · (dc/dx)
Two-film theory: 1/KG = 1/kg + H/kL
Henry's law: p = H·x
(b) Adsorption (gas-solid): VOCs, H₂S, odours. Activated carbon most common (surface area 500–1500 m²/g). Silica gel, alumina, zeolites for specific gases.
  • Langmuir isotherm (monolayer): q = qm·K·c / (1 + K·c)
  • Freundlich isotherm: q = Kf·c^(1/n)
(c) Condensation: cool gas below dew point → liquid; used for high-conc VOCs.
(d) Combustion / Afterburners:
  • Thermal oxidiser: direct combustion at 700–980°C; VOC destruction > 99%
  • Catalytic oxidiser: Pt/Pd catalyst; operates at 300–500°C; lower fuel cost
  • Regenerative Thermal Oxidiser (RTO): ceramic heat recovery, efficiency > 95%

4.8 FLUE GAS DESULPHURISATION (FGD)

Purpose: removes SO₂ from flue gas (thermal power plants).
Wet limestone FGD (most common, ~85% of world capacity):
CaCO₃ + SO₂ + ½O₂ + 2H₂O → CaSO₄·2H₂O (gypsum) + CO₂
SO₂ removal efficiency: > 95%
L/G ratio: 5–20 L/m³; Ca/S molar ratio: 1.02–1.05
Byproduct: marketable gypsum (wallboard, cement)
Other FGD types: dry/semi-dry lime (spray dryer absorber), seawater FGD (coastal plants), ammonia scrubbing, regenerable processes (Wellman-Lord).
Indian context: MoEFCC 2015 norms made FGD mandatory for all thermal plants; deadlines extended multiple times; < 40% capacity fitted as of 2025.

4.9 NOₓ CONTROL

Combustion modifications (in-furnace):
  • Low-NOₓ burners (staged combustion)
  • Flue Gas Recirculation (FGR): reduces flame T → less thermal NOₓ
  • Over-Fire Air (OFA): two-stage combustion
  • Water/steam injection
Post-combustion:
  • SCR (Selective Catalytic Reduction): NH₃ + NO + ¼O₂ → N₂ + 1.5H₂O on V₂O₅/TiO₂ catalyst at 300–400°C; efficiency 80–95%
  • SNCR (Selective Non-Catalytic Reduction): NH₃ or urea at 870–1100°C; no catalyst; efficiency 30–70%; cheaper but lower efficiency

4.10 AUTOMOTIVE EMISSION CONTROLS (GATE ADD)

Three-Way Catalyst (TWC) for petrol vehicles (stoichiometric operation):
  • Simultaneously oxidises CO, HC and reduces NOₓ on Pt/Pd/Rh catalyst
  • Requires λ (excess air ratio) close to 1.0 — controlled by O₂ sensor
  • Needs unleaded petrol (Pb poisons catalyst)
Diesel controls:
  • DOC (Diesel Oxidation Catalyst): oxidises CO, HC
  • DPF (Diesel Particulate Filter): traps soot, regenerates by burning at high T
  • SCR: NOₓ reduction with AdBlue (urea 32.5%)
  • EGR (Exhaust Gas Recirculation): reduces combustion T
Fuel quality (BS-VI, since April 2020): sulphur < 10 ppm (vs 50 ppm BS-IV); enables TWC/DPF.

  • Thermal power (installed 2017 onwards): PM 30 mg/Nm³; SO₂ 100; NOₓ 100; Hg 0.03 mg/Nm³
  • Cement plant: PM 30 mg/Nm³; NOₓ 600; SO₂ 100–700 (depends on feed)

  • Brick kiln (zigzag/VSBK): PM 250 mg/Nm³

5.5 MONITORING NETWORKS

  • NAMP (National Air quality Monitoring Programme): manual monitoring of 4 pollutants (SO₂, NO₂, PM₁₀, PM₂.₅); ~830 stations in 344 cities (2023)
  • CAAQMS (Continuous Ambient AQ Monitoring Stations): real-time monitoring of 12 pollutants + meteorology; ~530 stations (2024)
  • SAFAR (System of Air quality & weather Forecasting): IITM Pune — Delhi, Mumbai, Pune, Ahmedabad
  • Sampling height: 3–10 m above ground
  • Sampling duration: 24-hr (manual); continuous for CAAQMS
  • Minimum monitoring: 104 days/yr (NAMP)

CRITICAL EXAM-DAY TIPS:

  • ✓ Unit check in Deutsch-Anderson: w (m/s), A (m²), Q (m³/s) — if Q given in m³/hr, convert.
  • ✓ Gaussian plume: Q in g/s; u in m/s; concentration in µg/m³ requires factor of 10⁶.
  • ✓ Holland's Δh: T in Kelvin; u at stack top (not ground); P ≈ 101.3 kPa unless stated.
  • ✓ NAAQS memory: PM₁₀ annual = 60 & 24-hr = 100; PM₂.₅ = 40 & 60; SO₂ = 50 & 80; NO₂ = 40 & 80.
  • ✓ Stokes' settling: valid only for Re < 1 (small particles); for large particles use intermediate/Newton regime.
  • ✓ Pasquill class: daytime + strong sun + low wind = A (most unstable); clear night + low wind = F.
  • ✓ Plume traps: fumigation (worst ground conc) — inversion ABOVE, unstable below.
  • ✓ DALR vs SALR: dry = 9.8°C/km; saturated < dry because latent heat release slows cooling.
  • ✓ ESP resistivity: too high = back-corona; too low = particle re-entrainment; optimum 10⁴–10¹⁰ Ω·cm.
  • ✓ FGD products: wet limestone → gypsum (marketable); dry lime → mix (waste).
  • ✓ Smog ID: reducing (SO₂ + PM, winter, cold) = London; oxidising (O₃ + PAN, summer, hot) = LA.
  • ✓ Marking: Unit 3 = 10–13 Qs (~15% of paper). Factual (NAAQS, standards) are guaranteed marks — prioritise.
  • ✓ Common trap: criteria pollutants count = 12 (India NAAQS) vs 6 (USEPA) — read question carefully.
