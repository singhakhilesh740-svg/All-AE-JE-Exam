SECTION 1: WATER ANALYSIS METHODS

This section covers analytical methods for water quality parameters. Focus on procedure details, indicators, and standard values.

1.1 DISSOLVED OXYGEN (DO) - WINKLER'S METHOD (Standard APHA)

Procedure:
  1. Add MnSO₄ + alkaline KI to sample → Mn²⁺ oxidised to MnO₂ by DO
  2. Add H₂SO₄ → MnO₂ reacts with KI releasing I₂
  3. Titrate I₂ with Na₂S₂O₃ (sodium thiosulphate) using starch indicator
  4. Endpoint: blue colour (starch-iodine complex) → colourless (all I₂ consumed)
Azide Modification: Removes NO₂⁻ interference (which would oxidise I⁻ to I₂ in acidic medium, giving falsely high DO).
Field Alternative: Clark-type membrane electrode (polarographic sensor):
  • O₂ diffuses through semi-permeable membrane (Teflon)
  • Electrochemically reduced at cathode → current proportional to DO
  • Continuous monitoring, portable, no chemicals, rapid response
  • Requires calibration in air-saturated water
Critical Values: DO saturation at 20°C (freshwater) = 9.1 mg/L
CPCB River Standard: DO > 4 mg/L (indicates healthy stream)
Below 2 mg/L: Lethal to most fish; below 4 mg/L causes stress to aquatic organisms

1.2 BIOCHEMICAL OXYGEN DEMAND (BOD5)

Test Conditions (Critical):
  • Temperature: 20°C (represents average stream temperature)
  • Duration: 5 days (represents 70-75% of ultimate BOD for domestic sewage)
  • Light: DARK incubation (prevents algal photosynthesis producing O₂, which would falsely lower measured BOD)
  • Dilution Water: Aerated, pH 7.2, nutrient-seeded with MgSO₄, CaCl₂, FeCl₃, phosphate buffer
  • Final Conditions: DO depletion should be 2-6 mg/L; residual DO > 1 mg/L after 5 days
BOD Equation: y = L₀(1 - e-kt)
where: y = BOD at time t, L₀ = ultimate BOD, k = rate constant (base e), t = time (days)
Example: k₂₀ = 0.23/day, L₀ = 300 mg/L
BOD₅ = 300 × (1 - e-0.23×5) = 300 × (1 - e-1.15) = 300 × 0.683 = 205 mg/L (≈ 210 mg/L)
Temperature Correction: kT = k₂₀ × 1.047(T-20)
Example: At 10°C: k₁₀ = 0.23 × 1.047-10 = 0.23 × 0.633 = 0.146/day
Biodegradability Assessment using BOD/COD Ratio:
  • > 0.5 = Readily biodegradable (typical domestic sewage 0.5-0.6) → suitable for biological treatment
  • 0.3-0.5 = Moderately biodegradable
  • < 0.3 = Poorly biodegradable → requires physico-chemical or advanced oxidation treatment
  • < 0.1 = Essentially non-biodegradable (industrial effluents with synthetic chemicals, solvents, dyes)
CPCB General Standards (Schedule VI - Inland Surface Water): BOD ≤ 30 mg/L

1.3 CHEMICAL OXYGEN DEMAND (COD) - OPEN REFLUX DICHROMATE METHOD

Test Step Procedure & Details
Oxidant K₂Cr₂O₇ (potassium dichromate) in concentrated H₂SO₄
Catalyst Ag₂SO₄ (promotes oxidation of resistant/straight-chain organics)
Chloride Masking HgSO₄ (mercuric sulphate) complexes Cl⁻ to prevent false high COD. Ratio: 10:1 HgSO₄:Cl⁻ by mass. Cl⁻ would otherwise be oxidised by dichromate (6Cl⁻ + Cr₂O₇²⁻ + 14H⁺ → 3Cl₂ + 2Cr³⁺ + 7H₂O), giving falsely high readings.
Reflux Conditions 150°C for 2 hours (hot, closed-reflux system for better oxidation)
Back-Titration Excess unreacted K₂Cr₂O₇ titrated with standardised FAS (Ferrous Ammonium Sulphate)
Indicator Ferroin (1,10-phenanthroline ferrous complex). Color change: blue-green (oxidised) → reddish-brown (reduced) at endpoint
Calculation COD (mg/L) = [(Blank FAS mL - Sample FAS mL) × N × 8000] / mL sample, where N = normality of FAS
Key Advantages of COD over BOD:
  • Speed: Result in 2-3 hours vs 5 days for BOD
  • Completeness: Measures total oxidisable organic matter (biodegradable + non-biodegradable). BOD measures only biologically degradable organics.
  • Robustness: Not affected by toxic substances that inhibit microbial activity
  • Reproducibility: More consistent results than BOD
  • Inorganic Oxidisable Matter: Oxidises some inorganics (Fe²⁺, sulphides, NO₂⁻)
COD ≥ BOD Always: Because COD includes both biodegradable and non-biodegradable organics.
For domestic sewage: BOD₅/COD ≈ 0.5-0.6
For industrial: can be 0.1-0.3
CPCB General Standards (Schedule VI): COD ≤ 250 mg/L (inland surface water)
pH (also Schedule VI): 5.5-9.0 (new standard; older: 6.0-8.5)
Suspended Solids (SS): 100 mg/L (new: 50 mg/L)

1.4 HARDNESS: EDTA TITRATION

Total Hardness (pH 10 using NH₃-NH₄⁺ buffer):
  • Add EDTA (disodium ethylene diamine tetra-acetic acid)
  • EDTA chelates Ca²⁺ and Mg²⁺ ions: Ca²⁺ + EDTA → CaEDTA²⁻, Mg²⁺ + EDTA → MgEDTA²⁻
  • Indicator: EBT (Eriochrome Black T) forms wine-red/pink complex with Ca²⁺/Mg²⁺
  • Endpoint: EDTA strips last metal ion from EBT → free EBT turns blue
  • Result: Expressed as mg/L CaCO₃
Calcium Hardness Alone (pH 12-13 using NaOH):
  • At high pH, Mg²⁺ precipitates as Mg(OH)₂, doesn't interfere
  • Indicator: Murexide (ammonium purpurate) → pink (with Ca²⁺) to purple (endpoint)
  • Mg Hardness: Calculated by difference: Mg = Total - Ca
Temporary vs Permanent Hardness:
  • Temporary (Bicarbonate): Caused by Ca(HCO₃)₂ and Mg(HCO₃)₂. Removed by boiling: Ca(HCO₃)₂ → CaCO₃↓ + H₂O + CO₂↑. Economical for small quantities.
  • Permanent (Non-carbonate): CaSO₄, MgSO₄, CaCl₂, MgCl₂. NOT removable by boiling. Requires: lime-soda process, zeolite softening, or ion exchange.
  • Total Hardness = Temporary + Permanent
Hardness Classification mg/L as CaCO₃ Health & Technical Impact
Soft 0-75 May be corrosive to pipes; risk of Pb/Cu leaching from plumbing materials
Moderately Hard 75-150 No significant issues; acceptable for most uses
Hard 150-300 Scale formation in boilers/pipes/geysers; high soap consumption; reduces lathering
Very Hard > 300 Severe scaling; significant water softening required for domestic/industrial use
IS 10500:2012 Drinking Water Limits:
Desirable: 200 mg/L as CaCO₃
Permissible (no alternative source): 600 mg/L as CaCO₃

1.5 TURBIDITY, COLOUR, ODOUR, CHLORINE RESIDUAL

Turbidity (Nephelometric):
  • Measured by nephelometer (turbidimeter) → light scattered at 90° angle
  • Unit: NTU (Nephelometric Turbidity Unit)
  • Calibration: Formazin polymer suspension (primary standard)
  • Significance: High turbidity shields pathogens from UV/chlorine disinfection (turbidity blocks light penetration and protects microbes)
  • Sources: Clay, silt, plankton, colloidal particles
True Colour:
  • Measured AFTER filtering through 0.45 μm membrane filter (removes all suspended solids)
  • Compared against platinum-cobalt (Pt-Co) standards
  • Unit: TCU (True Colour Unit) or Hazen units
  • Sources: Dissolved humic/fulvic acids, tannins, Fe, Mn, industrial dyes
Threshold Odour Number (TON):
  • TON = (A + B) / A, where A = mL sample, B = mL odour-free dilution water at threshold dilution
  • TON of 1 = no odour (no dilution needed)
  • TON of 200 = 1 mL sample + 199 mL dilution water = objectionable odour
  • Method: Sensory panel (minimum 5 trained panellists)
  • Sources: H₂S (rotten egg), geosmin (earthy, from actinomycetes/cyanobacteria), chlorophenols (medicinal), industrial chemicals
Residual Chlorine (DPD Method):
  • DPD Reagent: N,N-diethyl-p-phenylenediamine
  • Reaction: Reacts with free chlorine (HOCl/OCl⁻) → pink/magenta colour
  • Measurement: Colorimetric/spectrophotometric at 515 nm. Colour intensity proportional to Cl concentration.
  • DPD-1 Vial: Free chlorine only
  • DPD-3 Vial (with KI): Total chlorine (free + combined)
  • Combined Cl = Total - Free
  • Alternative: Amperometric titration (more accurate for lab use)
  • OLD Method (Phased Out): OTA (o-tolidine) - found to be carcinogenic
IS 10500:2012 Drinking Water Limits:
Turbidity: Desirable 1 NTU; Permissible 5 NTU
Colour: Desirable 5 Hazen; Permissible 15 Hazen
Odour: 'Agreeable' (no numerical limit specified)
Free Chlorine Residual: Minimum 0.2 mg/L at consumer end (ensures protection during distribution)

1.6 JAR TEST

Purpose: Laboratory procedure to determine optimum coagulant dose, pH adjustment chemicals, and mixing conditions for coagulation-flocculation process.
Procedure:
  1. Fill 6 identical beakers with sample water (usually 1 L each)
  2. Add different coagulant doses to each beaker (e.g., 10, 20, 30, 40, 50, 60 mg/L)
  3. Rapid Mix: Stir at ~100 RPM for 1-2 minutes (coagulation - forms microflocs)
  4. Slow Mix: Reduce to ~30-40 RPM for 15-20 minutes (flocculation - combines microflocs into settleable flocs)
  5. Settling: Allow to settle undisturbed for 30 minutes
  6. Analysis: Measure supernatant: turbidity (NTU), colour (Hazen), pH, alkalinity
Interpretation:
  • Optimum Dose: Minimum coagulant concentration achieving acceptable water quality (lowest turbidity/colour with acceptable pH)
  • Over-dosing: Wastes chemicals, may increase turbidity (re-stabilisation)
  • Under-dosing: Insufficient floc formation, poor removal
  • Tests can include: coagulant aids (polyelectrolytes), pH adjustment (lime/acid), temperature effects

SECTION 2: MICROBIOLOGICAL ANALYSIS

2.1 COLIFORM TESTING: MPN (Most Probable Number) METHOD

Three-Stage Sequential Procedure:
Stage Medium Temperature & Time Positive Indicator Principle
1. Presumptive Lactose Broth (with inverted Durham tube) 35°C, 24-48 hrs Gas in Durham tube (top inverted tube) All coliforms ferment lactose producing CO₂/H₂. Mixed with non-coliform bacteria.
2. Confirmed Brilliant Green Bile Broth (BGBB) 35°C, 24-48 hrs Gas in Durham tube BGBB is selective for coliforms; suppresses many non-coliforms. Confirms presence of true coliforms.
3. Completed EMB Agar (Eosin Methylene Blue) 35°C, 24-48 hrs Dark colonies with green metallic sheen EMB is differential; E. coli ferments lactose rapidly → dark colonies. Confirms E. coli.
EMB Agar Colony Morphology (Critical for Identification):
  • E. coli: Dark blue-black colonies with green metallic sheen (pathognomonic). Caused by rapid lactose fermentation producing acid, which precipitates eosin-methylene blue dye.
  • Other Coliforms (Enterobacter, Klebsiella): Pink or mucoid colonies, NO metallic sheen
  • Non-lactose Fermenters (Salmonella, Shigella): Transparent or colourless colonies
MPN Calculation:
  • Count positive tubes (with gas) at presumptive, confirmed, and completed stages
  • Use standard MPN statistical tables (e.g., 3-tube or 5-tube method) to convert tube combinations to MPN/100 mL
  • Example: If 3 presumptive tubes positive, 2 confirmed, 1 completed → use table to find MPN value
IS 10500:2012 Drinking Water Standard:
Total Coliform: Shall not be detectable in any 100 mL sample (0 MPN/100 mL) - NO RELAXATION
Thermotolerant (Fecal) Coliform: Shall not be detectable
E. coli: Shall not be detectable

2.2 COLIFORM TESTING: MEMBRANE FILTER (MF) METHOD

Procedure:
  1. Filter a known volume of sample (usually 100 mL or appropriate dilution) through a sterile 0.45 μm pore size cellulose acetate membrane filter
  2. Place membrane on selective medium (m-Endo agar for total coliform, m-FC agar for fecal coliform)
  3. Incubate:
    • Total Coliform: 35°C for 22-24 hrs
    • Fecal Coliform: 44.5°C for 24 hrs (elevated temperature selects for fecal organisms)
  4. Count dark red/pink colonies with metallic sheen (coliforms)
  5. Result: CFU (Colony Forming Units) per 100 mL
Advantages over MPN Method:
  • Speed: 24 hrs vs 72+ hrs for MPN (3 sequential stages)
  • Precision: Direct colony count (more accurate than statistical estimation)
  • Quantitative: Exact number of organisms rather than range
Limitations:
  • Not suitable for highly turbid water (suspended solids clog membrane)
  • Cannot be used for all water types (some inhibitory substances may interfere)

2.3 THERMOTOLERANT (FECAL) COLIFORMS

Key Difference from Total Coliform:
  • Incubation Temperature: 44-44.5°C (elevated temperature selects for organisms of fecal/faecal origin)
  • E. coli is the primary thermotolerant coliform indicator
  • Non-fecal coliforms (Enterobacter, Klebsiella) do not grow at 44.5°C
IMViC Test for E. coli Confirmation:
  • I: Indole production (+)
  • M: Methyl Red test (+)
  • V: Voges-Proskauer test (−)
  • C: Citrate utilisation (−)
  • E. coli Pattern: ++−− (positive for I and MR; negative for VP and Citrate)
Why More Specific?
  • Thermotolerant coliforms are almost exclusively of fecal origin
  • Total coliforms include environmental coliforms (Enterobacter, Klebsiella) that are ubiquitous in soil/plants
  • Thermotolerant testing is more reliable for detecting actual faecal contamination
IS 10500:2012:
Thermotolerant (fecal) coliform: Shall not be detectable
E. coli: Shall not be detectable

SECTION 3: HEAVY METALS & ADVANCED ANALYSIS

3.1 ATOMIC ABSORPTION SPECTROSCOPY (AAS)

Principle: Sample is atomised (converted to ground-state atoms) in a high-temperature flame or furnace. These ground-state atoms absorb light at characteristic wavelengths specific to each element (from a Hollow Cathode Lamp). The amount of light absorbed is proportional to the concentration of the element (Beer-Lambert Law: A = εbc).
Procedure:
  1. Prepare sample in solution
  2. Aspirate into flame or graphite furnace
  3. Hollow Cathode Lamp emits characteristic wavelength for target element (e.g., Pb = 283.3 nm)
  4. Ground-state atoms absorb light
  5. Detector measures absorbance (proportional to concentration)
  6. Compare with calibration curve
Types of AAS:
  • Flame AAS: Air-acetylene flame (~2100°C). Detection limits: 0.01-0.1 mg/L. Fast, economical.
  • Graphite Furnace AAS (GF-AAS): Graphite tube furnace (~2700°C). Detection limits: ~0.001 mg/L (100-fold more sensitive). Better for trace elements, ppb-level analysis (e.g., As, Pb in drinking water). Slower, more expensive.
Element-Specific Wavelengths (Common Metals):
  • Pb: 283.3 nm
  • Cd: 228.8 nm
  • Cr: 357.9 nm
  • Cu: 324.7 nm
  • Ni: 232.0 nm
  • Zn: 213.9 nm
Advantages: Specific, accurate, sensitive, economical, simple operation.
Limitation: One element at a time (requires changing HCL for each element or using multi-element lamp).

3.2 ICP-OES (Inductively Coupled Plasma - Optical Emission Spectrometry)

Principle: Sample is nebulised into an argon plasma at extremely high temperature (6,000-10,000 K). At this temperature, all atoms are excited (energised) and emit characteristic wavelengths. The intensity of emitted light at each wavelength is proportional to the concentration of that element.
Major Advantage over AAS: MULTI-ELEMENT SIMULTANEOUS ANALYSIS - Can measure 20-70 elements in a single run in just a few minutes. All elements analysed simultaneously from one sample injection.
Detection Limits: ~0.001-0.01 mg/L for most metals (comparable to graphite furnace AAS).
Other Advantages:
  • Lower detection limits for many elements (vs flame AAS)
  • Wider dynamic range (concentration range measurable)
  • Less chemical interference
  • Excellent for trace element analysis
ICP-MS (Mass Spectrometry): Similar to ICP-OES but uses mass spectrometer for detection instead of optical spectrometry. Even lower detection limits (ppt, parts per trillion) and better handling of spectral interferences. Most sensitive technique available.

3.3 FLUORIDE ANALYSIS: SPADNS METHOD

Principle: Zirconium forms a complex with SPADNS (sodium 2-(para-sulfophenylazo)-1,8-dihydroxy-3,6-naphthalene disulfonate) reagent, forming a red-coloured lake. When fluoride is added, it competes for the zirconium, causing dissociation of the zirconium-SPADNS complex and release of free SPADNS dye. The resulting colour bleaching (decrease in red colour) is proportional to fluoride concentration.
Procedure:
  1. Add SPADNS reagent (red colour - zirconium-SPADNS complex)
  2. Add sample containing F⁻
  3. F⁻ preferentially binds Zr: F⁻ + Zr-SPADNS → Zr-F + SPADNS (free dye)
  4. Colour decreases (bleaches) with increasing F⁻
  5. Measure absorbance at 570 nm (proportional to F⁻)
Alternative Methods:
  • Ion-Selective Electrode (ISE): More accurate and preferred for routine monitoring. Uses TISAB buffer (Total Ionic Strength Adjustment Buffer) to maintain constant ionic strength and release all F⁻ ions.
  • High Performance Liquid Chromatography (HPLC): For very accurate/sensitive measurements
Health Effects of Fluoride:
  • < 0.5 mg/L: Dental caries (cavities) risk
  • 0.5-1.5 mg/L: Optimal for dental health
  • 1.5-3.0 mg/L: Dental fluorosis (enamel mottling, cosmetic discolouration)
  • 3-6 mg/L: Skeletal fluorosis (bone pain, stiffness)
  • > 10 mg/L: Crippling skeletal fluorosis (disabling joint damage)
Endemic Fluorosis Areas in India: Rajasthan, Andhra Pradesh, Tamil Nadu, Gujarat, Uttar Pradesh, Haryana (geogenic origin from fluoride-rich minerals in aquifers).
IS 10500:2012 Drinking Water Limits:
Fluoride: Desirable 1.0 mg/L; Permissible 1.5 mg/L

3.4 ARSENIC ANALYSIS (ppb Level)

Challenge: Arsenic occurs at very low concentrations (ppb = parts per billion = μg/L) in drinking water, requiring sensitive analytical techniques.
Preferred Methods (Detection Limits in ppb):
  • Graphite Furnace AAS (GF-AAS): ~1 ppb (parts per billion)
  • ICP-MS (Inductively Coupled Plasma - Mass Spectrometry): ~0.01 ppb (parts per trillion) - most sensitive
  • Hydride Generation AAS (HG-AAS): ~0.1 ppb. Converts As to AsH₃ gas, which is more easily detected.
Field Test Kits: Colorimetric methods (cheaper, portable) but higher detection limits (~10 ppb), insufficient for drinking water compliance monitoring. Speciation Important:
  • As(III): More toxic, more mobile in groundwater
  • As(V): Less toxic, less mobile
  • Different analytical methods may be needed to distinguish species
Contamination in India:
  • Major endemic areas: West Bengal, Bihar, Uttar Pradesh, Assam
  • Origin: Geogenic (natural minerals in alluvial aquifers), not anthropogenic
  • Affects: Millions of people in these regions dependent on groundwater
IS 10500:2012 Drinking Water Limit:
Arsenic: 0.01 mg/L (10 ppb) - same as WHO guideline

SECTION 4: AIR MONITORING METHODS

4.1 HIGH VOLUME SAMPLER (HVS) FOR PARTICULATE MATTER

Purpose: Measure suspended particulate matter (SPM/TSP - Total Suspended Particles, PM₁₀, PM₂.₅) in ambient air. Used for NAAQS compliance monitoring.
Operating Procedure:
  1. Pre-sampling: Weigh a glass fibre filter (8×10 inches typical) on analytical balance to ±0.1 mg (pre-weighing, W₁)
  2. Sampling: Draw ambient air through the filter at 1.1-1.7 m³/min for 24 hours (standard sampling duration)
  3. Post-sampling: Re-weigh the filter (post-weighing, W₂)
  4. Calculation: Mass of PM collected = W₂ - W₁. Concentration = (Mass / Total Volume) × 10⁶ μg/m³
Size-Selective Inlets (Impactors):
  • TSP (Total Suspended Particles): No inlet, all particles collected
  • PM₁₀ Inlet: Size-selective impactor removes particles > 10 μm. Only particles < 10 μm pass through to filter.
  • PM₂.₅ Inlet: WINS (Well Impactor Ninety-Six) or cyclone inlet selects particles < 2.5 μm
Why Size-Selection Matters?
  • PM₁₀: Thoracic particles, reach bronchi; inhalable fraction
  • PM₂.₅: Fine particles, reach alveoli; respirable fraction; deeper lung penetration; higher health risk
Gravimetric Method: Direct mass measurement from filter weighing. This is the REFERENCE METHOD for NAAQS monitoring under NAMP (National Ambient Monitoring Programme).
Alternative Method: Beta attenuation monitors (continuous, real-time measurement at CAAQMS stations).
NAAQS 2009 Limits (24-hr averaging):
PM₂.₅: 60 μg/m³
PM₁₀: 100 μg/m³
(Annual standards also exist: PM₂.₅ = 40 μg/m³, PM₁₀ = 60 μg/m³)

4.2 SO₂ MEASUREMENT: WEST-GAEKE METHOD

Absorbing Reagent: Sodium tetrachloromercurate (TCM, Na₂HgCl₄). Stable at room temperature.
Principle & Procedure:
  1. Draw ambient air through impinger containing TCM absorbing reagent
  2. Chemical Reaction: SO₂ + Na₂HgCl₄ → Na₂[HgCl₂SO₃] (stable dichlorosulphito-mercurate complex)
  3. SO₂ is quantitatively absorbed and stabilised in this complex form
  4. Add pararosaniline reagent + formaldehyde (in acidic medium)
  5. Color Reaction: Forms magenta/red-violet colour (azo dye, fuchsin)
  6. Measure absorbance at 548 nm (wavelength of maximum absorption for the colour complex)
  7. Absorbance proportional to SO₂ concentration
Automated Alternative:
  • Pulsed Fluorescence Analyser: SO₂ absorbs UV light at specific wavelengths and emits fluorescence (re-emits light at longer wavelength). Fluorescence intensity proportional to SO₂ concentration. Provides continuous, real-time measurement.
Advantages of West-Gaeke Method:
  • Simple, reproducible
  • Specific for SO₂
  • Stable complex allows samples to be stored and analyzed later
  • Established reference method
NAAQS 2009 Limit (24-hr averaging):
SO₂: 80 μg/m³ (for industrial, residential, or rural areas)

4.3 NOx MEASUREMENT: CHEMILUMINESCENCE ANALYSER

Principle (Ozone Chemiluminescence):
  1. NO + O₃ (ozone) → NO₂* (excited state) + O₂
  2. The excited NO₂* molecule releases energy as light (photon): NO₂* → NO₂ + hν (light)
  3. Measurement: Photomultiplier tube detects emitted light
  4. Light intensity ∝ NO concentration
Measurement of NOx (Total Oxidised Nitrogen):
  • Direct NO Measurement: Sample air + O₃ → chemiluminescence → NO concentration
  • NO₂ Conversion: Sample air heated to 315°C with molybdenum catalyst → NO₂ reduced to NO
  • Total NO Measured: This now includes original NO + converted NO₂
  • Calculation: NO₂ = (Total NO with catalyst) - (Direct NO measurement)
  • NOx = NO + NO₂
Wavelength of Emission: ~600-3000 nm (near-infrared region).
Detection Limit: ~0.5 ppb (excellent sensitivity for ambient monitoring).
Reference Method: US EPA reference method. Used in Indian CAAQMS (Continuous Ambient Air Quality Monitoring Stations) for real-time NOx monitoring.
NAAQS 2009 Limit (24-hr averaging):
NO₂: 80 μg/m³

4.4 CARBON MONOXIDE (CO): NDIR (Non-Dispersive Infrared) ANALYSER

Principle: CO is a diatomic molecule that absorbs infrared (IR) radiation at specific wavelengths. Different gases absorb IR at different characteristic wavelengths (absorption spectrum).
NDIR Technology:
  1. IR Source: Emits broad-spectrum infrared radiation
  2. Sample Cell: CO in sample absorbs IR at its characteristic wavelength (~4.67 μm)
  3. Reference Cell: Contains a non-absorbing gas (e.g., N₂) for comparison
  4. Detector: Measures IR intensity difference between sample and reference cells
  5. Difference in Intensity ∝ CO concentration
Advantages:
  • Specific for CO (wavelength-specific)
  • Not affected by other gases (each has unique absorption wavelength)
  • Provides continuous, real-time measurement
  • Stable baseline
Also Used For:
  • CO₂ Measurement: Absorption at 4.26 μm (same technology, different wavelength)
Detection Limit: ~0.05 ppm (very sensitive).
NAAQS 2009 Limits:
CO: 2 mg/m³ (8-hr averaging); 4 mg/m³ (1-hr averaging)

4.5 NAMP & CAAQMS MONITORING NETWORK

NAMP (National Ambient Monitoring Programme):
  • Manual Monitoring Stations: ~900+ across 300+ cities/towns in India
  • Parameters Measured: SO₂, NO₂, PM₁₀, PM₂.₅ (at select stations)
  • Sampling Frequency: 2 days per week, 24-hour sampling (typically Wed & Sun, or Sat & Wed)
  • Method: HVS for particles (gravimetric); absorbers for gases (colorimetric analysis in lab)
  • Coverage: Represents overall ambient air quality trends; data available on CPCB website and SAMEER app
CAAQMS (Continuous Ambient Air Quality Monitoring Stations):
  • Automated Monitoring: ~400+ continuous stations across major cities
  • Parameters: PM₂.₅, PM₁₀, SO₂, NOx, CO, O₃, NH₃, Pb, Benzene (varied by station)
  • Real-Time Data: Measurements every 15 minutes (or 1-hour average)
  • Instruments: Automated analysers (beta attenuation for PM, chemiluminescence for NOx, NDIR for CO, etc.)
  • Data Display: CPCB AQI (Air Quality Index) portal, SAMEER app (mobile), real-time on web
CPCB Air Quality Index (AQI) - 6 Categories (based on the worst-performing pollutant):
Good: 0-50 (Green)
Satisfactory: 51-100 (Blue)
Moderately Polluted: 101-200 (Yellow)
Poor: 201-300 (Orange)
Very Poor: 301-400 (Red)
Severe: >400 (Maroon)

SECTION 5: ENVIRONMENTAL CHEMISTRY FUNDAMENTALS

5.1 pH, ALKALINITY & BUFFER CAPACITY

pH Definition: pH = −log₁₀[H⁺] (negative logarithm of hydrogen ion activity)
Scale: 0 (most acidic) to 14 (most alkaline) at 25°C
pH 7 = Neutral (at 25°C)
< 7 = Acidic
> 7 = Alkaline/Basic
Significance of pH:
  • Disinfection: Hypochlorous acid (HOCl) is the active disinfectant. HOCl is dominant at pH < 7.5. As pH increases above 7.5, HOCl dissociates to hypochlorite ion (OCl⁻, less effective), reducing disinfection efficiency.
  • Metal Solubility: pH controls whether metals precipitate (insoluble hydroxides) or remain dissolved (soluble ions). This affects:
    • Metal removal (coagulation works best at specific pH ranges for each metal)
    • Corrosion of pipes (low pH → corrosive, can leach Pb, Cu from plumbing)
  • Biological Treatment: Optimal pH for microbes is typically 6.5-8.0. Extreme pH inhibits microbial growth.
Measurement:
  • Glass electrode pH meter (reference electrode + glass sensing electrode) - most accurate
  • pH indicator papers/solutions - qualitative/semi-quantitative
Alkalinity Definition: Acid-Neutralising Capacity (ANC) of water. The amount of strong acid required to bring pH to 4.5 (or total capacity to neutralise acids). Primarily due to dissolved bases:
  • Bicarbonate (HCO₃⁻): Dominant at pH 6.3-8.3 (natural waters)
  • Carbonate (CO₃²⁻): Dominant at pH > 8.3 (more alkaline waters)
  • Hydroxide (OH⁻): Significant only at pH > 12 (very alkaline, not natural waters)
Measurement of Alkalinity:
  • Titration Method: Add standard HCl (H₂SO₄) to sample, measure to two endpoints:
    • P-Alkalinity (phenolphthalein): Titrate to pH 8.3 (phenolphthalein endpoint, pink→colourless)
    • M-Alkalinity (methyl orange): Titrate to pH 4.5 (methyl orange endpoint, red→yellow)
  • Interpretation Rules: Based on P and M values, calculate individual species (HCO₃⁻, CO₃²⁻, OH⁻)
Buffer Capacity Definition: The ability to resist pH change when small amounts of strong acid or base are added. Higher buffer capacity = smaller pH change for same amount of acid/base added.
Buffer System in Natural Waters: The carbonate system (CO₂ ↔ H₂CO₃ ↔ HCO₃⁻ ↔ CO₃²⁻) is the primary buffer in natural water. Higher alkalinity (more HCO₃⁻/CO₃²⁻) = greater buffer capacity.
Importance:
  • Wastewater Treatment: Nitrification (oxidation of ammonia to nitrate) consumes alkalinity. If alkalinity is insufficient, pH will drop, inhibiting nitrification. Alkalinity supplementation may be needed.
  • Stream Water Quality: Adequate alkalinity protects streams from pH crashes due to acid rain or other acid inputs, protecting aquatic life.
  • Corrosion Control: Water with optimal pH and alkalinity is less corrosive to pipes.
IS 10500:2012 Drinking Water Limits:
pH: 6.5-8.5 (no relaxation - strict requirement)
CPCB Discharge Standard: pH 5.5-9.0 (inland surface water)

5.2 REDOX POTENTIAL (ORP/Eh) & Eh-pH DIAGRAMS

ORP/Eh (Oxidation-Reduction Potential): Measured in millivolts (mV) using a platinum electrode (inert sensor) paired with a reference electrode. Indicates the oxidising or reducing nature of water.
Eh Ranges & Environmental Conditions:
  • +200 to +600 mV: Strongly oxidising, aerobic conditions. Dissolved oxygen (O₂) present, or NO₃⁻ as electron acceptor. Supports aerobic organisms.
  • 0 to +200 mV: Anoxic conditions. O₂ depleted, NO₃⁻ available as electron acceptor. Denitrification occurs (NO₃⁻ → N₂).
  • −100 to −400 mV: Strongly reducing, anaerobic conditions. No O₂ or NO₃⁻. SO₄²⁻ reduction occurs (SO₄²⁻ → H₂S). Methanogenesis (CH₄ production).
Eh-pH Diagram (Pourbaix Diagram): A thermodynamic diagram showing stability regions of different chemical species of an element as a function of both Eh (y-axis, in volts) and pH (x-axis).
Features:
  • X-axis: pH (0-14)
  • Y-axis: Eh or pe (redox potential, typically -0.4 to +1.0 V)
  • Regions: Each region represents the stable form of the element under those conditions (dissolved ion, solid oxide, hydroxide, gas, etc.)
  • Upper Boundary: O₂/H₂O line (limit of oxidising conditions with water)
  • Lower Boundary: H₂/H₂O line (limit of reducing conditions with water)
Example: For Iron (Fe):
  • At high Eh (oxidising): Fe³⁺ (dissolved, ferric) or Fe₂O₃ (solid rust)
  • At low Eh (reducing): Fe²⁺ (dissolved, ferrous)
  • Different pH: Different hydroxide or oxide forms (Fe(OH)₃, Fe(OH)₂)
Applications:
  • Predicting Metal Mobility: Where will a metal stay dissolved vs precipitate, how mobile is it in groundwater
  • Corrosion Analysis: Are pipes in oxidising or reducing environment, will they corrode
  • Treatment Process Design: What oxidation state will the contaminant be in, which treatment technology is best
  • Chromium Reduction: Cr⁶⁺ (oxidising conditions) vs Cr³⁺ (reducing conditions). Cr⁶⁺ is toxic, mobile, must be reduced to Cr³⁺ (less toxic, can be precipitated).

5.3 BIOACCUMULATION & BIOMAGNIFICATION

Bioaccumulation: Progressive increase in the concentration of a chemical substance in an organism over time, because the rate of uptake (from all sources: water, food, sediment) exceeds the rate of excretion and metabolism (biological degradation).
Conditions Favoring Bioaccumulation:
  • Lipophilic (Fat-soluble): Dissolves in fats/lipids of organism tissue, not water-soluble → difficult to excrete
  • Low Water Solubility: Hydrophobic (water-avoiding)
  • Persistent: Resists biodegradation (long half-life)
  • Examples: DDT, PCBs, methylmercury, dioxins, legacy POPs
Bioconcentration Factor (BCF): BCF = Concentration in organism / Concentration in surrounding water (at steady state equilibrium)
  • BCF < 100: Low bioconcentration (chemical easily excreted, not a concern)
  • BCF 100-1000: Moderate
  • BCF 1000-5000: High bioconcentration
  • BCF > 5000: Very high, significant concern
BCF Correlation with Kow (Octanol-Water Partition Coefficient): log BCF ≈ 0.85 × log Kow
Higher Kow (more lipophilic) → higher BCF (more bioconcentration)
Examples of BCF Values:
  • DDT: ~100,000
  • PCBs: 10,000-100,000
  • Methylmercury: 10,000-100,000
  • PBDEs (flame retardants): High
Biomagnification: The progressive increase in concentration of a persistent substance at successive higher trophic levels of a food chain. Also called biological magnification.
Why Does Biomagnification Occur?
  • Lower trophic level organism accumulates chemical (e.g., algae).
  • Higher trophic organism eats many lower organisms to survive.
  • Persistent chemical is not excreted or degraded → remains in tissue.
  • Over many predator-prey interactions, concentration increases exponentially with trophic level.
Classic Example - DDT Biomagnification in Aquatic Food Chain:
  • Water: 0.000003 ppm DDT
  • Phytoplankton: 0.04 ppm (13,000× concentration)
  • Zooplankton: 0.5 ppm
  • Small fish: 2 ppm
  • Large fish: 25 ppm
  • Fish-eating birds (Osprey, Bald Eagle): 75 ppm (25 million× water concentration!)
Consequence: DDT in raptors caused eggshell thinning, reproductive failure, population collapse. DDT was banned in many countries (but still used in some under malaria exemptions).
General Rule: Only persistent, lipophilic chemicals biomagnify. Most chemicals are readily degraded or excreted → don't biomagnify.

5.4 PERSISTENCE & DEGRADATION PATHWAYS

Environmental Persistence: Described by the environmental half-life (t₁/₂) = the time required for 50% of the chemical to be degraded/removed from the environment.
Degradation Pathways:
  1. Hydrolysis: Reaction with water molecules. Sensitive to pH and temperature. Important for esters, amides.
  2. Photolysis (Photochemical Degradation): UV light breaks chemical bonds. Occurs in surface water/soil exposed to sunlight. Half-lives can range from hours to days.
  3. Biodegradation (Microbial Degradation): Microorganisms (bacteria, fungi) enzymatically degrade chemical. Depends on microbial presence, conditions, chemical structure. Usually slowest pathway.
  4. Chemical Oxidation: Atmospheric oxidants (OH radicals, ozone) oxidise the chemical.
Examples of Environmental Half-Lives:
  • DDT: 2-15 years (very persistent, now mostly banned)
  • PCBs (Polychlorinated Biphenyls): Decades to centuries (extremely persistent)
  • Dioxins: 7-11 years (highly persistent)
  • PFAS (Per- and Polyfluoroalkyl Substances): Decades to centuries, often called "'forever chemicals'" (extremely resistant to degradation due to carbon-fluorine bonds)
  • Most Natural Organic Compounds: Hours to weeks (readily degradable)
Persistent Organic Pollutants (POPs):
  • Definition: Chemicals that are persistent (resist degradation), bioaccumulative (accumulate in organisms), toxic, and undergo long-range environmental transport.
  • Stockholm Convention (2001): International treaty addressing POPs. Specifies chemicals to be eliminated globally.
    • Original "Dirty Dozen": Aldrin, Chlordane, DDT, Dieldrin, Endrin, Heptachlor, Hexachlorobenzene, Mirex, Toxaphene, PCBs, Dioxins, Furans
    • Expanded List: Now includes ~30 chemicals
  • DDT Special Status: Annex B (restricted, not completely eliminated). Exempted for disease vector control (malaria, dengue) under WHO approval. India uses DDT for malaria control under this exemption; agricultural use banned since 1989.

SECTION 6: TOXICOLOGY & RISK ASSESSMENT

6.1 TOXICITY PARAMETERS: LD50, LC50, NOAEL, LOAEL, RfD

LD50 (Lethal Dose 50): The dose of a substance that causes death in 50% of test animals under specified conditions. Expressed as mg/kg body weight (oral, dermal, or inhalation route specified separately). Lower LD50 = more toxic.
Examples of LD50 (Oral, Rat):
  • Botulinum toxin: 0.001 mg/kg (extremely toxic)
  • Ricin: 0.01 mg/kg
  • Cyanide (sodium): 6.4 mg/kg (highly toxic)
  • Aspirin: ~1500 mg/kg
  • Caffeine: 192 mg/kg
  • Table salt (NaCl): 3000 mg/kg (surprisingly toxic in large doses)
  • Water: > 90,000 mg/kg (essentially non-toxic)
WHO Classification of Acute Toxicity (based on LD50):
  • Class Ia (Extremely Hazardous): LD50 (oral) < 5 mg/kg
  • Class Ib (Highly Hazardous): LD50 5-50 mg/kg
  • Class II (Moderately Hazardous): LD50 50-500 mg/kg
  • Class III (Slightly Hazardous): LD50 > 500 mg/kg
LC50 (Lethal Concentration 50): The concentration of a substance in air (ppm or mg/m³) or water (mg/L) that causes death in 50% of test organisms during a specified exposure period. Lower LC50 = more toxic.
Typical Exposure Durations:
  • Inhalation: 4 hours (standard)
  • Aquatic Toxicity:
    • Fish: 96-hour LC50
    • Daphnia (water flea): 48-hour LC50 (or EC50 for immobility)
    • Algae: 72-hour EC50 (for growth inhibition)
NOAEL & LOAEL (Subchronic/Chronic Toxicity):
  • NOAEL (No Observed Adverse Effect Level): Highest dose at which there is NO statistically significant increase in adverse effects (compared to control). Used as basis for setting RfD (Reference Dose) and ADI (Acceptable Daily Intake). More reliable than LOAEL.
  • LOAEL (Lowest Observed Adverse Effect Level): Lowest dose at which adverse effects are observed. Used when NOAEL cannot be established (higher uncertainty, larger safety factors applied).
  • Always: NOAEL < LOAEL (by definition)
Reference Dose (RfD) - Chronic Human Risk Assessment: RfD = NOAEL / (UF × MF)
where: UF = Uncertainty Factors (typically 10 each for different types of uncertainty), MF = Modifying Factor (professional judgment, usually 1-10)
Typical Uncertainty Factors:
  • 10× for animal-to-human extrapolation (interspecies variability)
  • 10× for human variability (intraspecies - differences between individuals)
  • 10× for subchronic-to-chronic extrapolation (if only subchronic data available)
  • 10× for LOAEL-to-NOAEL extrapolation (if only LOAEL available, no NOAEL)
  • Total typical UF = 100 to 1000
Example RfD Calculation: If NOAEL = 10 mg/kg/day, UF = 100 (interspecies 10 × intraspecies 10)
Then RfD = 10 / 100 = 0.1 mg/kg/day (safe daily dose per kg body weight)
Use in Risk Assessment - Hazard Quotient (HQ): HQ = Estimated Exposure Dose / RfD
  • HQ < 1: No concern (exposure below safe level)
  • HQ ≥ 1: Potential health concern (exposure exceeds RfD, detailed risk assessment needed)

6.2 SYNERGISM VS ANTAGONISM

Synergism: Combined toxic effect of 2 or more chemicals is GREATER than the sum of their individual effects. (1 + 1 > 2, supra-additive).
Example: Carbon tetrachloride (CCl₄) + Ethanol (alcohol)
Liver damage from combination is much more severe than expected from adding individual liver damage from each substance alone. The chemicals interact to amplify toxicity.
Antagonism: Combined effect is LESS than the sum (sub-additive). One substance reduces the toxicity of another. (1 + 1 < 2).
Example: Atropine counteracts organophosphate poisoning
Organophosphates inhibit acetylcholinesterase enzyme. Atropine blocks acetylcholine receptors, reducing symptoms of over-stimulation.
Additive Effect (Baseline): Combined effect equals the sum. (1 + 1 = 2). Most common when mechanisms are independent.
Potentiation: A non-toxic substance enhances the toxicity of another (special case of synergism).
Importance in Environmental Health: Real-world exposure is rarely to single chemicals - we're exposed to chemical mixtures. Understanding interactions is crucial for accurate risk assessment and environmental protection.
