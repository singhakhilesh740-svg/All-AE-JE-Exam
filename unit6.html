SECTION 1: SECTOR-SPECIFIC INDUSTRIAL POLLUTION

Pollution profile, key pollutants, treatment options, and CPCB standards by sector. Most-asked sectors: pulp & paper, tannery, textile, sugar, distillery, electroplating, thermal power, cement.

1.1 17 CATEGORIES OF HIGHLY POLLUTING INDUSTRIES (CPCB)

CPCB classifies industries into Red / Orange / Green / White categories based on Pollution Index (2016 revision; PI = f of air, water, hazardous waste, and resource consumption).
  • Red category (PI > 60): 60 sectors — most polluting; CTE/CTO with strict conditions; not allowed in residential areas
  • Orange (PI 41–59): 83 sectors — moderately polluting
  • Green (PI 21–40): 63 sectors — relatively less polluting
  • White (PI ≤ 20): 36 sectors — practically non-polluting; only intimation needed, no consent required (e.g., solar PV cell manufacture, chalk-making, biscuits without baking)
"17 highly polluting" originally identified categories (Indo-German project 1991; the basis of MINAS — Minimum National Standards):
  1. Aluminium
  2. Caustic soda
  3. Cement
  4. Copper smelter
  5. Distillery
  6. Dyes & dye intermediates
  7. Fertiliser
  8. Integrated iron & steel
  9. Leather (tanneries)
  10. Oil refinery
  11. Pesticides
  12. Petrochemicals
  13. Pharmaceuticals
  14. Pulp & paper
  15. Sugar
  16. Thermal power
  17. Zinc smelter

1.2 PULP & PAPER INDUSTRY

Process: Wood / bagasse / agro-residue → debarking → chipping → pulping (kraft / soda / sulphite) → washing → bleaching → paper machine. Key pollutants:
  • Wastewater: high BOD, COD, TSS, dark colour from lignin; AOX (Adsorbable Organic Halides) from chlorine bleaching; high TDS
  • Air: SO₂, H₂S, mercaptans (TRS — Total Reduced Sulphur in kraft); PM
  • Solid waste: primary sludge, biological sludge, lime mud, bark
  • Water consumption: 100–250 m³/t paper (large mills); > 200 m³/t (small)
Treatment: primary clarifier → activated sludge / ASB → tertiary (chemical coagulation, MBR for water reuse). Black liquor recovery — Tomlinson recovery boiler (recovers Na, S; produces steam).
Cleaner production: ECF (Elemental Chlorine Free) and TCF (Totally Chlorine Free) bleaching; closed-loop water; agro-residue use.
CPCB standards (large pulp & paper, > 24,000 t/yr):
  • BOD < 30 mg/L (inland); COD < 250; TSS < 100; pH 6.5–8.5
  • AOX < 1.0 kg/t product (large mills, ECF mandated)
  • Water consumption norm: < 130 m³/t (large mills); < 50 m³/t (agro-based)
  • SO₂ < 200 mg/Nm³; PM < 150 mg/Nm³; TRS < 5 ppm

1.3 LEATHER / TANNERY

Process: raw hide/skin → soaking → liming & unhairing → fleshing → deliming → bating → pickling → chrome tanning → re-tanning → dyeing → fatliquoring → finishing. Key pollutants:
  • Wastewater: very high BOD (~1500–6000 mg/L), COD, sulphide (from unhairing — toxic), Cr³⁺/Cr⁶⁺ (~3000–4000 mg/L Cr in spent chrome liquor), high TDS, TSS, ammonia
  • Solid waste: chrome shavings, fleshing waste, hair, sludge (50–80 kg/t hide processed)
  • Air: H₂S, ammonia, VOCs from finishing solvents
Treatment: stream segregation (chrome stream → precipitation as Cr(OH)₃ at pH 8–9 → recovery; sulphide stream → catalytic oxidation with Mn²⁺) → equalisation → primary clarifier → ASP → tertiary (RO/NF for ZLD). Most Indian tanneries use CETPs (Kanpur Jajmau, Vellore Ranipet, Erode, Tamil Nadu).
Cleaner production: chrome recycling (precipitation & redissolution in H₂SO₄), low-chrome syntans, vegetable tanning, salt-free curing, splitting before tanning, enzyme-assisted unhairing.
CPCB standards (tannery):
  • BOD 30 mg/L; COD 250; TSS 100; pH 6.5–8.5 (inland)
  • Total Cr < 2.0 mg/L; Cr⁶⁺ < 0.1 mg/L (very strict)
  • Sulphide < 2.0; oil & grease < 10; chloride < 1000 (inland)
  • ZLD mandated for tannery clusters (NGT Kanpur 2020 order)

1.4 TEXTILE INDUSTRY (DYEING & PROCESSING)

Process: sizing → desizing → scouring → bleaching → mercerising → dyeing → printing → finishing. Key pollutants:
  • Wastewater: COD high, BOD moderate (BOD/COD 0.2–0.4 — refractory), strong colour, high pH (8–11), high TDS (5000–25000 mg/L), heavy metals (Cu, Cr, Zn from dyes), surfactants
  • Synthetic dyes: azo (~70% of dyes), reactive, vat, disperse, direct, sulphur
  • Water consumption: 200–300 L/kg fabric
Treatment: equalisation → coagulation/flocculation (alum/PAC, FeCl₃) → DAF → ASP/MBR → tertiary (carbon adsorption / ozonation / Fenton oxidation for colour) → RO + MEE for ZLD.
Tirupur ZLD model (Tamil Nadu): SC order 2011; ~750 dyeing units forced to install ZLD (RO + evaporator + crystalliser); recovers salt for reuse. World's first major textile ZLD cluster.

1.5 SUGAR & DISTILLERY

Sugar industry:
  • Wastewater: cooling, condensate, mill house, boiler — BOD 1000–1500 mg/L; SS, oil, grease
  • Air: bagasse-fired boilers (PM)
  • Bagasse cogeneration: India sugar mills produce ~9 GW; major RE source
  • Press mud (~3% of cane) → composting / biomethanation
Distillery (molasses-based):
  • Spent wash: very high BOD (40,000–60,000 mg/L) & COD (80,000–100,000 mg/L); dark brown (melanoidins from caramelisation)
  • Generation: 8–15 L/L alcohol produced; ~14 BL/year India
  • Treatment: anaerobic digestion (UASB / lagoon) → biogas recovery (28–35 m³/m³ spent wash) → aerobic polishing → composting (with press mud) OR incineration
  • ZLD mandated for distilleries since 2015 (CPCB, Ganga Action Plan); concentration & incineration of slop

1.6 ELECTROPLATING & METAL FINISHING

Process: degreasing → pickling (HCl/H₂SO₄) → rinsing → electroplating bath (Cr/Ni/Cu/Zn/Cd/Au) → rinsing → drying.
Key pollutants:
  • Wastewater: Cr⁶⁺, Cd, Ni, Cu, Zn, Pb, CN⁻ (from cyanide baths); acids/alkalis; rinse waters with low metal but high volume
  • BOD/COD low (inorganic); TDS high
Treatment (segregated streams):
  • Cr⁶⁺ stream: reduce with NaHSO₃ at pH 2–3 to Cr³⁺; then precipitate at pH 8–9
  • Cyanide stream: alkaline chlorination — NaOCl + NaOH at pH > 10; first to cyanate (CNO⁻), then to N₂ + CO₂
  • Acid/alkali: neutralisation
  • Heavy metals: hydroxide precipitation at pH 8–10 (Cd 11; Zn 9.5; Ni 10.8; Cu 6.5; Pb 9.0)
  • Polishing: ion exchange, RO, electrodialysis

1.7 THERMAL POWER PLANTS

Pollutants & sources:
  • Air: PM (fly ash), SO₂, NOₓ, Hg, CO₂; from coal combustion
  • Water: cooling tower blowdown, ash pond overflow, boiler blowdown, oily water
  • Solid waste: fly ash + bottom ash (~25–35% of coal mass; India produces ~270 MT/yr)
  • Thermal pollution: condenser cooling water raises river/sea temperature
Control technologies:
  • ESP (mandatory; > 99% PM removal)
  • FGD — wet limestone scrubber (CaCO₃ + SO₂ → CaSO₄·2H₂O); efficiency > 95%
  • SCR (NH₃ + V₂O₅/TiO₂) or low-NOₓ burners + OFA for NOₓ
  • Fly ash utilisation: cement (substitute for clinker), bricks, road construction, mine filling, agriculture
  • Cooling tower (closed-cycle) replacing once-through to reduce thermal pollution
MoEFCC norms — TPP installed 2017 onwards:
  • PM: 30 mg/Nm³
  • SO₂: 100 mg/Nm³ (units > 500 MW); < 600 mg/Nm³ for older units
  • NOₓ: 100 mg/Nm³ (new); 300–600 (old)
  • Hg: 0.03 mg/Nm³
  • Water consumption: < 3 m³/MWh (new); 3.5 m³/MWh (existing)
  • Fly ash utilisation: 100% target (Fly Ash Utilisation Rules, 2009; latest amendment 2021 — extended to 5-year cycle)

1.8 CEMENT INDUSTRY

Pollutants:
  • Air: PM (kiln, raw mill, cooler), SO₂ (from raw mat S), NOₓ (high T flames), CO₂ (calcination + fuel)
  • CO₂ footprint: ~0.85 t CO₂ / t cement (60% from calcination, 40% from fuel) → cement = ~7% of global CO₂
  • Negligible wastewater (dry process)
Control: bag filter (very high efficiency, < 30 mg/Nm³); ESP for kiln; low-NOₓ burners; SNCR; cement kiln co-processing of hazardous waste, plastics, RDF (substitutes coal — Indian Thermal Substitution Rate ~5% target 25%).
Cleaner production: blended cements (PPC with fly ash, PSC with slag) — Indian cement uses ~35% of fly ash; reduces clinker factor.
CPCB stack norms (cement):
  • PM: 30 mg/Nm³
  • NOₓ: 600 mg/Nm³
  • SO₂: 100 mg/Nm³ (without pyrite); 700–1000 (with pyrite in raw mat)

SECTION 2: ZERO LIQUID DISCHARGE (ZLD)

2.1 ZLD CONCEPT

ZLD: wastewater treatment process designed to eliminate any liquid discharge from a facility. All water is recovered & reused; only solid waste (salt, sludge) leaves the system.
Drivers in India:
  • NGT/SC orders; CPCB directions (textile, distillery, tannery clusters)
  • Water scarcity in industrial regions
  • Reduces pollution load on water bodies (Ganga, Tirupur Noyyal)
  • High TDS effluents not amenable to discharge under CPCB norms

2.2 TYPICAL ZLD TRAIN

Raw effluent → Equalisation → Primary clarifier → Biological (ASP/MBR) → Tertiary (sand filter, GAC) → Ultrafiltration (UF) → Reverse Osmosis (RO) → MEE (Multiple-Effect Evaporator) → ATFD (Agitated Thin Film Dryer) / Crystalliser → Dry salt + sludge

Recovery: 60–95% as treated water for reuse; 5–40% as solid salt/sludge to landfill.

2.3 KEY UNIT OPERATIONS IN ZLD

2.4 ECONOMICS & CHALLENGES

  • CAPEX: ₹15–40 crore per MLD (vs ₹5–10 cr for conventional ETP)
  • OPEX: ₹150–300/m³ (vs ₹15–40/m³ for conventional)
  • Energy: 20–35 kWh/m³ wastewater (vs 1–2 for conventional)
  • Recovered salt often impure (mixed Na, Cl, SO₄, organics) — limited resale
  • Dependence on grid power → indirect carbon footprint
  • Successful: Tirupur dyeing cluster, distilleries; struggling: small textile / tannery units

SECTION 3: COMMON EFFLUENT TREATMENT PLANT (CETP)

3.1 CETP CONCEPT

CETP: centralised facility for collective treatment of wastewater from cluster of small & medium industries (SSI/MSME) in industrial estates / clusters. Justified when individual ETPs are uneconomical or technically inadequate.
  • Each member unit must achieve "inlet standards" (pre-treatment) before discharge to CETP
  • CETP achieves CPCB outlet standards (Schedule VI)
  • Funded by member units (capital + O&M cess); often partly subsidised under MoEFCC scheme
  • Operated by SPV / cooperative / private operator
Notable Indian CETPs: Vapi (Gujarat — chemicals/dyes), Ankleshwar (chemicals), Naroda & Vatva (Ahmedabad), Tarapur (Maharashtra), Kanpur Jajmau (tanneries), Tirupur (textile), Vellore Ranipet (tanneries), Manali Chennai (chemicals), Patancheru (Hyderabad — pharma).

3.2 CETP DESIGN

Typical train: Equalisation (24–48 hr; pH/composition averaging) → Neutralisation → Primary clarifier → Biological (ASP / MBBR) → Secondary clarifier → Tertiary (sand filter, AC) → Disinfection → Discharge / Reuse.
For high-TDS / refractory streams: + RO + MEE (ZLD).
Common challenges: highly variable inlet (composition, pH, BOD); shock loads; non-compliance by member units (toxic shock); sludge management.

SECTION 4: CLEANER PRODUCTION (CP) & END-OF-PIPE

4.1 CP — DEFINITION (UNEP, 1990)

Cleaner Production: "continuous application of an integrated, preventive environmental strategy to processes, products and services to increase eco-efficiency and reduce risks to humans and the environment".
  • For processes: conserve raw materials, water, energy; eliminate toxic substances; reduce waste at source
  • For products: reduce environmental impact across life cycle (DfE — Design for Environment)
  • For services: incorporate environmental concerns in design & delivery

4.2 CP HIERARCHY (PREVENTION vs END-OF-PIPE)

Preferred order (top to bottom):
1. Source reduction (best — prevents waste generation)
   a. Input substitution (less toxic raw materials)
   b. Process change (better technology)
   c. Good housekeeping (training, maintenance)
   d. Product reformulation
2. Recycling on-site (closed loop)
3. Recycling off-site
4. Treatment (end-of-pipe)
5. Disposal (least preferred)
End-of-Pipe (EOP): traditional approach — treat waste after generation. Disadvantages:
  • Increases production cost without revenue
  • Transfers pollution between media (e.g., wastewater → sludge → air during incineration)
  • Doesn't address root cause
  • Often retrofitted (sub-optimal)
CP advantages: reduces waste & cost simultaneously; competitive advantage; regulatory compliance; better worker safety; reduced liability.

4.3 CP ASSESSMENT METHODOLOGY (UNEP/UNIDO 6 phases)

  1. Planning & Organisation: management commitment, team formation
  2. Pre-assessment: walk-through audit, identify focus areas
  3. Assessment: material & energy balance; identify CP options
  4. Feasibility analysis: technical, economic (NPV/IRR), environmental
  5. Implementation: install & operationalise selected options
  6. Continuation: monitor, document, replicate

4.4 LIFE CYCLE ASSESSMENT (LCA) FOR INDUSTRIAL PRODUCTS

(Detailed coverage in Unit 6.)
  • ISO 14040/14044 — 4 phases: Goal & Scope → LCI → LCIA → Interpretation
  • Helps choose between alternative materials/processes (e.g., glass vs PET; aluminium vs steel)
  • Common impact categories: GWP, AP, EP, ODP, HTP, water footprint
  • Software: SimaPro, GaBi, OpenLCA
  • Use in Eco-design, EPDs, green procurement

SECTION 5: GREEN CHEMISTRY & INDUSTRIAL ECOLOGY

5.1 GREEN CHEMISTRY — 12 PRINCIPLES (Anastas & Warner, 1998)

  1. Prevention — better to prevent waste than treat after
  2. Atom Economy — synthesis methods should incorporate all materials in final product
  3. Less Hazardous Synthesis — use/generate substances with little or no toxicity
  4. Designing Safer Chemicals — products should fulfil function with minimal toxicity
  5. Safer Solvents & Auxiliaries — avoid auxiliary substances (solvents, separation agents); if used, make innocuous
  6. Energy Efficiency — minimise energy requirements; conduct at ambient T & P where possible
  7. Renewable Feedstocks — raw material from renewable rather than depleting sources
  8. Reduce Derivatives — minimise blocking groups, protection/deprotection (each step uses reagents & generates waste)
  9. Catalysis — catalytic reagents (selective) superior to stoichiometric
  10. Design for Degradation — products break down to innocuous products at end-of-life
  11. Real-time Analysis — for pollution prevention; in-process monitoring
  12. Inherently Safer Chemistry — choose substances/forms to minimise potential for chemical accidents (releases, explosions, fires)
Atom Economy = (MW of desired product / Σ MW of all reactants) × 100
Example: Diels-Alder → 100% atom economy; Wittig reaction → low (~20%) atom economy

E-Factor (Sheldon, 1992) = mass of waste / mass of product
Bulk chemicals: < 1 | Fine chemicals: 5–50 | Pharma: 25–100+

5.2 INDUSTRIAL ECOLOGY (IE)

Concept: industrial systems modelled on natural ecosystems — waste of one process becomes input for another (closed loops, no waste).
Foundational concept: Frosch & Gallopoulos, Scientific American 1989.

5.3 KALUNDBORG SYMBIOSIS (DENMARK) — CASE STUDY

World's first industrial symbiosis; evolved organically since 1960s; now 30+ exchanges among 12+ partners.
Key exchanges:
  • Asnaes coal power plant → steam to oil refinery (Statoil), pharmaceutical (Novo Nordisk), district heating (Kalundborg town); fly ash → cement; gypsum (from FGD) → wallboard (Gyproc)
  • Statoil refinery → cooling water to power plant; sulphur → sulphuric acid plant
  • Novo Nordisk → biomass sludge → fertiliser to farms
  • Gyproc → uses waste gypsum from power FGD
Reported savings: CO₂ (~635,000 t/yr), water (~3 million m³/yr), several materials including oil & gypsum.

5.4 OTHER IE CONCEPTS

  • Eco-Industrial Park (EIP): planned industrial cluster designed for symbiosis (vs Kalundborg's organic evolution)
  • Circular Economy: broader concept — design out waste, keep products in use, regenerate natural systems (Ellen MacArthur Foundation)
  • Cradle-to-Cradle (C2C): materials flow in two cycles — biological (compost) & technical (closed-loop recycle); no "waste" (McDonough & Braungart)
  • Material Flow Analysis (MFA): quantitative tracking of materials through economy
  • Substance Flow Analysis (SFA): for specific substances (Hg, Pb, P)

SECTION 6: CARBON TRADING & CDM

6.1 KYOTO MECHANISMS & INDIA

Kyoto Protocol (1997, in force 2005) introduced 3 flexibility mechanisms:
  • Clean Development Mechanism (CDM): Annex I countries fund GHG reduction projects in non-Annex I → earn CER (Certified Emission Reduction) credits (1 CER = 1 t CO₂e)
  • Joint Implementation (JI): between two Annex I countries; ERUs (Emission Reduction Units)
  • Emissions Trading (ET): trading of AAUs (Assigned Amount Units)
India under CDM: 2nd largest host of CDM projects globally (after China); ~1700 registered (mostly RE, energy efficiency, biomass, methane recovery from landfills).

6.2 PARIS AGREEMENT — ARTICLE 6 MECHANISMS (post-Kyoto)

  • Article 6.2: bilateral trading of ITMOs (Internationally Transferred Mitigation Outcomes)
  • Article 6.4: centralised mechanism (successor to CDM); rules finalised at COP26 Glasgow (2021); operationalised at COP28 (2023) & COP29 (Baku 2024)
  • Article 6.8: non-market approaches

6.3 INDIAN CARBON MARKET — CCTS 2023

Carbon Credit Trading Scheme (CCTS):
  • Notified June 2023 under Energy Conservation (Amendment) Act 2022
  • Two segments:
    • Compliance segment: obligated entities (energy-intensive industries) must surrender carbon credits equivalent to their target
    • Voluntary segment: non-obligated entities/projects
  • Administered by BEE; regulated by CERC; trading on power exchanges (IEX, PXIL)
  • Builds on PAT (Perform, Achieve, Trade) Scheme — ESCerts (Energy Saving Certificates)
  • RECs (Renewable Energy Certificates): for RE generation; tradable

6.4 OTHER MARKET-BASED INSTRUMENTS

  • Carbon Tax: directly taxes CO₂ emissions per tonne (e.g., Sweden ~$130/t — highest globally; India had Coal Cess ₹400/t, subsumed under GST 2017)
  • Cap-and-Trade: regulator caps total emissions; emitters trade allowances (EU ETS — largest globally; California, China)
  • Carbon Border Adjustment Mechanism (CBAM): EU 2026 — tariff on imported carbon-intensive goods (steel, aluminium, cement, fertiliser, electricity, hydrogen); affects Indian exports
  • Voluntary Carbon Market: Verra (VCS), Gold Standard, ACR, CAR — corporate offset purchases
